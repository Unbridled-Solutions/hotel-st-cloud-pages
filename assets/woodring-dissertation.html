The Core Question

What Problem Does This Research Solve?

Responsible Leadership (RL) research has focused almost exclusively on leader behavior — what leaders do, say, and decide. Almost nothing exists about how followers actually perceive, define, and construct their view of responsible leadership.

This dissertation fills that gap. Using Narrative Inquiry, Woodring explores how eight entry-level Microsoft employees built their understanding of CEO Satya Nadella's responsible leadership — despite never having met him.

⚠️

The Trust Crisis

59% of people believe business leaders intentionally mislead them (2025 Edelman Trust Barometer). Yet businesses still rank as the most trusted institution. Leaders have both a mandate and a liability.

🔍

The Research Gap

No widely accepted definition of Responsible Leadership exists. Empirical research on the follower perspective is nearly absent — almost all studies examine the leader, not the led.

🏢

The Scale Problem

55%+ of U.S. private-sector employees work in organizations with 250+ people. At that scale, employees rarely interact with executives — they construct leadership impressions from a distance.

🎯

The Study Design

Single company (Microsoft), single CEO (Satya Nadella), 8 entry-level employees. A controlled design that eliminates variation from multiple orgs or cultures, isolating the follower perception process.

Methodology

How the Research Was Conducted

Narrative Inquiry (NI) — a qualitative method that captures lived experience through storytelling. Rather than surveys or lab experiments, Woodring conducted deep interviews and then "restoried" each participant's account — reconstructing their narrative arc from first impression through disruption and trust recalibration.

Constructivist Framework

RL is treated as a co-created, relational phenomenon — not a fixed trait of a leader. It is shaped by organizational systems, constituent expectations, and ongoing interpretation.

Narrative Restorying

Each participant's interview was reconstructed into a "restory" — a chronological narrative arc showing how their perception of leadership formed, was tested, and was revised over time.

The Eight Voices

Participants and Their Interpretive Lenses

Each participant filtered the same CEO through a completely different identity lens — same events, radically different readings.

Paul
Lens: Fairness & Sacrifice

Troubled by executive pay during mass layoffs. RL requires distributive fairness, not just strategic competence. Peer conversations drove most of his impressions.

Shu
Lens: Discipline & Visibility

Raised in Shanghai with values of accountability and honesty. Judged Satya by systemic cues — Teams status, email tone. Criticized silence on the Israel-Gaza conflict as a missed moral moment.

Francisco
Lens: Humility & Consistency

Son of Venezuelan immigrants; work = economic mobility. Respected Satya's background; skeptical of scripted communication and sudden strategic pivots. "If you're going to talk about your values, show them."

Lainey
Lens: Emotional Intelligence & Moral Alignment

Evaluated Satya by affective tone — eye contact in videos, cadence, how teams treat each other. Felt moral dissonance when "care" rhetoric didn't match layoff decisions.

Micah
Lens: Pragmatism & System Coherence

Results-oriented; evaluated long-term consistency over symbolic gestures. Withheld judgment on ethical controversies until sufficient evidence existed. Modeled on Mario Cuomo: principle over political expediency.

Darby
Lens: Vision & Character Alignment

Resonated with Satya's calm tone and strategic boldness. Viewed the OpenAI investment as future-oriented. High trust — character matched strategy in Darby's framework.

Ahmed
Lens: Moral Courage & Global Accountability

Demanded transparency, empathy, and moral clarity — especially on international conflicts. Strategic competence was secondary to moral expectation. CEO silence = failure of responsibility.

Siva
Lens: Cultural Proximity & Critical Scrutiny

Indian American — shared regional identity with Nadella but refused to let that lower her bar. Common identity created higher expectations, not lower ones. Skeptical of contradictions between stated values and decisions.

Cross-Case Findings

6 Themes in How Followers Construct Responsible Leadership

These themes emerged across all eight participants. They are processual — each feeds into the next.

1

Leadership Signals

Followers read strategic decisions (cloud pivot, OpenAI, acquisitions), structural moves (layoffs, compensation), symbolic communications (town halls, email tone), and even silence as data points about a leader's character. Signals aren't received passively — they're raw material for ongoing interpretation.

2

Mediation Through Distance

No participant had direct CEO access. Perceptions flowed through managers, peer conversations, digital artifacts (Teams status, keynote videos, company emails). Leadership was interpreted — not transmitted. Local managers served as amplifiers or filters of the CEO's signal.

3

Identity Filtering

Each participant ran signals through their personal identity — cultural background, religious beliefs, family values, career goals. The same event (layoffs, AI acceleration, geopolitical silence) was read as visionary, pragmatic, disturbing, or morally reprehensible depending on the lens. Differences came from differing value hierarchies, not conflicting facts.

4

Responsibility Domain Weighting

Participants subconsciously sorted RL into three domains: Market (strategic competence), Organizational (culture and people), and Moral (ethics and global accountability). They diverged based on which domain they weighted most heavily — not based on disagreements about the facts.

5

Narrative Disruption

Trust was never stable. Disrupting events — mass layoffs, geopolitical silence, sudden strategy pivots — forced followers to recalibrate. Some recovered trust. Some didn't. The disruption itself became part of the leader's narrative, not an exception to it.

6

Conditional Trust Under Leadership Distance

Trust was never unconditional. It was earned, disrupted, and rebuilt in a continuous recursive loop. Under hierarchical distance, followers constructed a tentative, ongoing relationship with a leader they had never met — constantly open to revision.

Original Contribution

The RL Follower Interpretive Process Model (RL/P)

Woodring's central theoretical contribution is a new model describing how followers construct responsible leadership from a distance. It is recursive — the process doesn't end; it feeds back into itself as new signals arrive.

The RL/P Process Flow

Leadership Signals → Mediated by Distance → Filtered through Identity → Weighted by Domain → Disrupted by Events → Conditional, Recursive Trust

The model is recursive — each trust outcome becomes a lens through which the next signal is interpreted. Responsible leadership is not a fixed evaluation; it is an ongoing, co-constructed story.

Theoretical Propositions

4 Propositions Emerging from the RL/P Model

P1

Responsible Leadership is constructed mainly through mediated signals under conditions of hierarchical distance — not through direct contact or personal relationships.

P2

Followers filter leadership signals through identity-based interpretive lenses — cultural background, values, life experience — producing divergent evaluations of the same events.

P3

Responsible Leadership is categorized across distinct responsibility domains (market, organizational, moral), and follower divergence emerges from differential domain weighting, not factual disagreement.

P4

Trust in Responsible Leadership is conditional and recursive — continuously calibrated, disrupted, and rebuilt rather than established once and held.

So What?

Practical Implications for Leaders and Organizations

📡

Design leadership signals with interpretive awareness

Tone, timing, word choice, and silence all communicate. What a CEO says in a layoff email is a signal. What they don't say in a geopolitical crisis is a louder one. Signals should be designed, not just delivered.

🔗

Recognize the manager as an interpretive mediator

Managers don't just pass messages down — they translate and filter them. How a manager frames the CEO's decisions can reinforce or undermine trust at the front line. Manager behavior IS leadership experience for most employees.

⚡

Prepare for moral inflection points

Crises — layoffs, geopolitical events, ethics controversies — are not exceptions to the leadership narrative. They are the test of it. Organizations should have a prepared posture for high-visibility ethical moments, not improvise in real time.

⚖️

Account for domain-weighted responsibility

Different employees care most about different things — strategy, culture, ethics. A single "responsible leadership" message won't resonate uniformly. Leaders need to acknowledge all three responsibility domains to reach the full workforce.

🔄

Treat trust as recursive, not static

Trust is not established and maintained — it is continuously earned, disrupted, and rebuilt. Leaders who act as though trust is "locked in" after a cultural win are operating on a false assumption. Ongoing signals matter as much as founding moments.

Honest Assessment

Limitations and Future Research

Study Limitations

  • Single organization (Microsoft) — limits generalizability across industries
  • Single CEO (Satya Nadella) — cannot claim findings transfer to other leadership styles
  • Small sample (8 participants) — rich data, not statistically representative
  • Snapshot in time — no longitudinal tracking of how trust evolved over years

Future Research Directions

  • Test domain weighting across industries, cultures, and organization types
  • Study hierarchical distance as a moderating variable — does more distance = less trust?
  • Longitudinal studies of recursive trust calibration after disruption events
  • Integrate RL theory with political corporate responsibility (AI governance, geopolitics)
  • Expand Narrative Inquiry into leadership-as-practice frameworks

The Bottom Line

Why This Matters

Responsible leadership isn't just what a CEO does — it's what followers construct from everything they see, hear, and don't hear. At scale, in large organizations, the vast majority of employees will never have a direct conversation with their CEO. Yet they hold a vivid, detailed, constantly-updated view of whether that leader is responsible.

Woodring's work puts the follower's experience on the map in a field that's long ignored it — and offers a practical framework for leaders who want to actually reach people, not just broadcast at them.
