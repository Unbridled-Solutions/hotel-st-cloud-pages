Before the Plan Runs You — Sit With These

Question 01
Why development, specifically?
Is it the building? The deal structure? Owning something tangible? Leaving a legacy? Your answer tells you which sub-path fits your personality — and keeps you going when year 3 feels like a grind.
Question 02
What does "time freedom" actually look like?
Early developers often work harder than any W-2 job. Are you okay with 60-hour weeks in years 2–5 if it buys freedom at year 8? Where's your actual line?
Question 03
What's your honest relationship with risk?
Development means personal guarantees, borrowed capital, and projects that can go sideways. Have you sat with that? What's your backup plan if a deal goes wrong in year 7?
Question 04
How does your hospitality background become a weapon?
You're not leaving hospitality behind — you're carrying a rare edge into a field that mostly lacks it. Boutique hotels, mixed-use, adaptive reuse. Have you thought about that lane?
Question 05
Who's the version of you you're building toward?
Not the job title. The person. What does 32-year-old you value? What does he regret not doing at 22? Write that down somewhere you'll actually find it.
Question 06
Have you had the real conversation with your partner?
The developers who burn out are the ones who didn't. The ones who make it had a partner who understood what the climb years actually look like. That conversation is part of the plan.

Read before you launch your LLC. The difference between working in a business vs. on one will save you years of frustration.

Not RE-specific — but the thinking on wealth, leverage, and long games is foundational for where you're going. Read it twice.

When the plan gets hard — and it will — this is the book that keeps you oriented. Not a business book. A life book.

Real Talk — The Tensions Worth Naming

The Triangle You're Managing

Family stability + developer income uncertainty + time freedom. These three things are in genuine tension during years 2–6. That's not a reason to change the goal — it's a reason to be explicit about what the climb years look like. With yourself and with your future partner.

The Climb Years Are Real

Early-stage developers work brutal hours. The plan should acknowledge that clearly — not pretend the timeline is clean or that six figures comes without sacrifice in the early phases. Know what you're signing up for.

The Plan Is a Compass, Not a Contract

You wrote this so the future stressed version of you has something to hold. But the best plans adapt. Review this document every 12 months. Update it. Let it breathe. A rigid plan is a plan that breaks under pressure.
