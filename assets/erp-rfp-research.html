Section 02
What Makes a Strong RFP — Critical Sections

Implementation partners consistently say the same thing: weak RFPs produce weak proposals. The gap isn't usually in the feature list — it's in the organizational context that helps a vendor understand what you're actually trying to solve.

Must Include · Often Missing
Organizational Structure Map
Diagram or narrative of the holding company → 32 entity relationship. Which entities share a legal structure? Which are truly separate legal entities with distinct EINs? Which entities share staff? This is the single most important context for a vendor to scope correctly.
Must Include · Often Missing
Current State Process Flows
How does AP work today? How does payroll run today? Who touches each process across which entities? Even a rough narrative eliminates ambiguity that otherwise costs you in change orders post-contract.
Commonly Skimped
Success Definition & KPIs
What does a winning implementation look like in 18 months? Specific and measurable — "reduce intercompany reconciliation from 5 days to same day," "consolidated P&L available by 2nd business day of each month." Without this, vendors over-promise and you have no basis to hold them accountable.
Commonly Skimped
Use Cases / Day-in-the-Life Scenarios
Write 3–5 realistic scenarios: "The shared AP team pays a vendor invoice that spans 4 entities — describe how your system handles cost allocation, intercompany billing, and payment." Vendors who answer well understand your model. Vendors who give generic answers don't.
Strong Differentiator
Internal Resource Commitment
Tell vendors exactly who will be on your project team, their time availability (% FTE), and their authority level. Implementations that fail usually do so because the client team was under-resourced. A vendor who asks about this in their questions back to you is a good sign.
Strong Differentiator
Phased Go-Live Intent
Are you planning to go live with all 32 entities at once (high risk), or phase by entity group? Stating your intent here lets vendors price and plan accordingly — and surface the risk if your intent is unrealistic.
Section 03
Multi-Entity Requirements — Must-Ask Questions

This is where most RFPs for multi-entity organizations are weakest. Generic questions get generic answers. These are the specific capabilities you need to probe — with RFP questions that force vendors to show their work, not just check a box.

Chart of Accounts Consolidation
Standardized Group CoA + Entity-Level Variations
Can the system maintain a single group-level Chart of Accounts while accommodating entity-specific accounts where local reporting or operational differences require them? How are entity accounts mapped to the consolidated CoA?
Describe how your system supports both a consolidated group Chart of Accounts and entity-specific charts. Walk us through how a new entity (#33) would be integrated into the existing CoA structure. Show us what the mapping interface looks like.
Intercompany Eliminations
Automated IC Transaction Detection & Elimination
This is the most operationally painful task in a multi-entity close. The system must automatically identify and eliminate intercompany sales, expense recharges, loans, and equity transactions — not require manual journal entries.
Demonstrate your system's automated intercompany elimination capabilities. How does it handle: (a) intercompany goods/services sales, (b) cost recharges from a shared services entity to operating entities, (c) intercompany loans. What is the audit trail for eliminations prior to consolidation? Provide a screenshot or demo walkthrough.
Consolidated Reporting
Real-Time Consolidated P&L, Balance Sheet, Cash Flow
How long does it take to produce a consolidated P&L across all 32 entities after month-end? "Lightning-fast" is marketing copy — ask for a real number and a reference client with comparable entity count.
What is the typical time-to-close for a consolidated financial statement across 30+ entities in your platform? Provide a reference client with 20+ entities we can call. Describe how multi-currency translation and FX rate management work during consolidation. Can we drill from a consolidated line item to the originating entity transaction?
Shared AP / AR
Centralized Payables & Receivables Across All Entities
A shared services AP team paying invoices on behalf of multiple legal entities is a specific workflow that many ERPs handle awkwardly. This needs to be airtight.
Describe how a centralized AP team in entity "Unbridled Shared Services" can enter, approve, and pay a vendor invoice on behalf of entity "Hotel St. Cloud" — and have that transaction: (1) appear on Hotel St. Cloud's entity P&L, (2) generate an intercompany payable/receivable, and (3) be eliminated correctly in the consolidated view. Walk us through each step.
Critical gap most RFPs miss: Entity addition scalability. Ask vendors: "If we acquire a 33rd entity mid-year, how long does it take to bring them onto the platform, and what is the cost?" A good answer is "days." A bad answer is "6–8 weeks and a change order."
Section 04
HR Module Requirements — Shared Services Function

A shared HR function running benefits, onboarding, time & attendance, and payroll for 32 entities has specific requirements that a standard HRIS doesn't cover. These need to be in the RFP explicitly.

Employee Self-Service
Multi-Entity ESS Portal
Employees across all 32 entities need one portal — not 32 separate logins. Policies may differ by entity (different PTO rules, different benefit eligibility). The system must handle entity-specific rulesets within a unified employee experience.
Benefits Administration
Variable Benefits Across Entity Groups
Some entities may offer different benefit plans. The system needs to manage different eligibility rules, enrollment windows, and deduction schedules while feeding a single centralized benefits administration workflow.
Compliance — Colorado
CO-Specific: FAMLI, Paid Leave, New Hire Reporting
CO-specific requirements you must ask about: Colorado FAMLI (Family & Medical Leave Insurance) — mandatory as of 2024. CO Paid Sick Leave. New hire reporting to CO DOLE. COUI (unemployment insurance) rate management per entity. Ask specifically by name — generic "state compliance" answers aren't good enough.
Onboarding Workflows
Automated Multi-Entity Onboarding
New hires across 32 entities need consistent but entity-configurable onboarding. I-9, W-4, direct deposit, benefits enrollment, IT provisioning trigger — all wired into one flow.
Time & Attendance
Multi-Policy Time Tracking at Scale
Different entities likely have different overtime rules, minimum wage rates (some CO municipalities have rates above state), and shift structures. The system must handle these variations without requiring separate configurations per entity.
Payroll Integration
Financial Posting Across 32 Entities
Even if payroll processing is outsourced (ADP, Paychex), the ERP must receive gross-to-net data and post payroll costs to the correct entity GL, cost center, and department — automatically.
Walk us through how your system handles a new hire at entity 17 (a small restaurant) whose benefits differ from entity 3 (a hotel). How does onboarding workflow configuration differ by entity? How does the ESS experience look from the employee's perspective — do they know they're in a multi-entity system, or is it seamless?
Describe your compliance coverage for Colorado-specific requirements: FAMLI, Colorado Paid Sick Leave, new hire reporting to DOLE, and multi-entity COUI management. How does the system alert our HR team to compliance deadlines and changes in state law?
Section 05
Integration Landscape — 32-Entity Portfolio

A 32-entity portfolio almost certainly runs on a patchwork of tools. Your RFP needs to surface integration requirements and force vendors to address them specifically — not promise a generic API.

Point of Sale
POS → ERP Daily Sales Flow
Include in RFP: List every POS system in use across the portfolio (Toast, Square, Lightspeed, etc.). Ask vendors whether they have native connectors or require middleware. Request a description of the data flow: how do daily revenue, COGS, and payment-type splits land in the GL for each entity?
Property Management
PMS → ERP Financial Sync
For hotel/rental entities — Cloudbeds specifically is in the Unbridled ecosystem. Ask vendors about native Cloudbeds integration or Cloudbeds API compatibility. Reservation revenue, occupancy data, and department-level charges need to flow to the right entity GL automatically.
Banking & Treasury
Multi-Entity Bank Reconciliation + ACH
32 entities likely means 32+ bank accounts. Automated bank feeds and reconciliation across all of them is a major time saver. Ask vendors: which banks do they have native feeds with? What's the reconciliation workflow for a newly added entity? Can ACH payments be initiated directly from the ERP?
Payroll Processors
ADP / Paychex / Gusto → GL Posting
If payroll is processed externally, you need a clean integration that posts payroll journal entries to the correct entity, cost center, and account. Ask for: supported payroll platforms, integration frequency (real-time vs. batch), and how payroll allocation rules are configured per entity.
Scheduling / Workforce
WFM → Time & Attendance → Payroll
For hospitality and F&B entities — 7shifts, HotSchedules, Deputy are common. Approved schedule + actual hours need to flow to payroll without manual re-entry. Ask vendors to demonstrate the end-to-end: schedule approved → hours validated → payroll triggered.
Critical Ask
Integration Ownership Post-Go-Live
Most RFPs miss this: Who maintains integrations when a third-party API changes? When Toast updates their API version, does your team own the fix, or the vendor? Clarify in the contract — this is where post-implementation costs hide.
Provide your complete library of pre-built connectors for the following categories: POS (list specific platforms), Property Management Systems, payroll processors, workforce management/scheduling tools, and banking/treasury platforms. For each integration not covered by a native connector, describe your standard middleware or API framework and who owns ongoing maintenance.
Section 06
Implementation Partner Evaluation — Scoring Rubric

The platform choice is table stakes. The partner choice is what determines whether you go live on time, on budget, and actually use the system. Score proposals on these dimensions.

25%
Understanding of Requirements
Did they demonstrate they actually read your RFP? Do they reference your specific 32-entity model, your shared AP/HR structure, your Colorado compliance requirements? Generic answers that could apply to any company = red flag.
20%
Methodology & Project Approach
Is there a detailed, phased project plan? A realistic timeline that acknowledges 32-entity complexity? A named risk register? Data migration strategy with specific steps? "We use an agile methodology" is not a plan.
20%
Team Expertise & Multi-Entity Experience
Who specifically will be on your project? Named consultants with resumes and certifications, not "a team of experienced professionals." Require at least one reference from a multi-entity (20+ entities) implementation they led.
15%
Post-Go-Live Support & Training Plan
Hypercare period — how long? Response SLAs by severity? Who is your named support contact? Training plan that addresses different user types across 32 entities (shared services power users vs. entity-level read-only users).
10%
Pricing Transparency & Total Cost of Ownership
Itemized breakdown: licensing, implementation, customization, integrations, training, data migration, ongoing support. Ask for a 3-year TCO model. Proposals with vague "to be determined" line items will turn into change orders.
10%
Cultural Fit & Communication Quality
How did they behave during the RFP process? Did they ask good clarifying questions (a very good sign) or just answer what was written? This relationship lasts 12–18 months of intense collaboration — it matters.
Red Flags — Disqualify or Downweight Heavily:
  • Unable to provide a reference with 20+ entities — this is not a standard implementation; you need someone who's done it
  • Timeline under 9 months for 32 entities — almost certainly unrealistic; anyone promising faster hasn't scoped it properly
  • No named project team members — "we'll assign the right resources" means you might get their B team
  • Vague data migration plan — data migration is always where projects blow up; "we have tools for that" is not an answer
  • No mention of change management — technology is 30% of the problem; people adopting it is 70%
  • Cost significantly below other proposals — usually means scope gaps that become change orders post-contract
  • Dismissing your multi-entity complexity — "it's all standard functionality" from a vendor who hasn't probed your intercompany structure
Section 07
Common RFP Gaps — What Gets Forgotten

These are the sections that organizations routinely underspec in their RFPs — and that come back as project failures, cost overruns, or post-go-live chaos. Add these to your RFP if they're not already there.

  • 💾
    Data Migration — The Silent Project Killer
    Most RFPs ask "can you migrate our data?" — which every vendor says yes to. What you actually need to ask: How many years of history? What source systems are you migrating from? What's the data cleansing process? Who owns validation? What's the cutover plan? Data migration routinely accounts for 20–30% of implementation cost and schedule — if your RFP doesn't address it specifically, expect a large change order.
  • 🧠
    Change Management — The Human Half
    You are asking 32 business units to change how they do everything financial and HR-related. Require vendors to submit a change management plan — stakeholder map, communication strategy, resistance mitigation, executive sponsorship approach. Without this baked into the contract, it becomes a nice-to-have that gets cut when timelines compress.
  • 🎓
    Training — Not One Session, Not One Size
    Your user population spans shared services power users (full system access), entity-level managers (departmental reports and approvals), and front-line staff (ESS only). Each group needs different training. Ask for: training plan by user type, training modalities (in-person, video, self-paced), materials ownership, and a plan for training new users as staff turns over post-go-live.
  • 🚀
    Hypercare / Go-Live Support Period
    The 30 days after go-live are the highest-risk period of any ERP implementation. Require vendors to specify: dedicated hypercare team availability, hours of support during hypercare, escalation path for critical issues, and the formal handover process when hypercare ends. Many vendors consider go-live the finish line. It's actually the starting line.
  • 📋
    SLA Definitions — Uptime, Response, Resolution
    Vague support agreements produce disputes. Define these explicitly in the RFP and require vendors to respond to each: Uptime SLA (99.9%? 99.99%?), P1 response time (system down — 1 hour?), P2 response time (major function impaired — 4 hours?), P3 (minor issue — next business day?), scheduled maintenance windows, and remedies for SLA breach.
  • 📈
    Scalability — Adding Entities
    Ask specifically: "We may acquire 3–5 additional entities over the next 3 years. What is the process, timeline, and cost to add an entity to the system?" This is often priced per entity in ways that aren't disclosed upfront, and the answer tells you a lot about how the platform is architected.
  • 🔒
    Role-Based Access Controls Across Entities
    Entity-level users should see only their entity. Shared services should see all. Finance managers from Entity 7 should not see Entity 12's P&L. Ask vendors to demonstrate their RBAC model for a multi-entity structure — specifically how you configure a user who has full access to entity financials but read-only access to HR, across some but not all entities.
  • 📅
    Month-End & Year-End Close Process
    For 32 entities, the close process is a coordinated exercise across the whole portfolio. Ask vendors to walk through their close workflow: entity-level close steps, intercompany reconciliation trigger, consolidation run, approval gates, and final financial statement production. Get a realistic timeline for how long a full portfolio close takes in their system.
Section 08
Budget Benchmarks — Implementation + Licensing

These are rough benchmarks based on 32-entity shared services deployments. Actual costs vary significantly based on customization, integration count, and data migration complexity. Use these to sanity-check proposals.

Implementation Cost Range
$300K–$800K+
For a 32-entity shared services deployment. This assumes cloud deployment, 3–6 major integrations, standard data migration from 1–2 legacy systems, and a defined phased rollout. Does NOT include internal staff time (which is significant). Highly customized deployments can exceed $1M.
Annual Licensing Cost Range
$60K–$300K/yr
For 30–75 full users across shared services and entity-level managers. Most platforms also offer cheaper "light" user or ESS-only licenses for employees who only access self-service features — leverage these to reduce per-seat cost.
Typical Implementation Timeline
12–18 months
For a phased rollout across 32 entities. Anyone promising under 9 months has not scoped the data migration and training requirements. A credible phased plan: Shared services core + 5–8 pilot entities in Phase 1 (6–9 months), then rolling entity additions through Phase 2.
Per-User Licensing (Reference)
$80–$400/user/mo
Full user range across evaluated platforms. NetSuite and Sage Intacct tend toward higher end. Acumatica and Dynamics BC tend lower. ESS/light users often $15–$30/mo. At 50 full users + 200 ESS users: ~$7,500–$20,000/mo in licensing alone.
Budget tip: Require vendors to provide a 3-year Total Cost of Ownership model in their response — Year 1 (implementation + licensing), Year 2 (steady state + support), Year 3 (with projected entity growth). The delta between Year 1 and Year 2 tells you a lot about how they're structuring their economics.

MC's Take — Top Recommendations
Platform Shortlist
1. Send the RFP to NetSuite OneWorld + Sage Intacct First
These are the two platforms purpose-built for your use case. Add Acumatica as a third if you want pricing competition. Everything else on the market is a compromise for a 32-entity shared services model.
RFP Strengthening — Highest Priority
2. Add the Organizational Structure Map
Draw the holding company → entity relationship before you send the RFP. Which entities share a legal structure, share GL codes, share staff? This one artifact will dramatically improve proposal quality and eliminate scoping surprises.
Contract Protection
3. Define SLAs and Entity-Addition Pricing in the Contract
Not in the RFP — in the actual contract. Uptime SLA with remedies. Named hypercare team for 60 days post-go-live. Explicit per-entity cost for adding entities in years 2–5. These three items prevent the most common post-contract disputes.
Risk Management
4. Phase the Rollout — Don't Big-Bang 32 Entities at Once
Start with shared services core + your 3–5 most operationally mature entities. Get to a working state, learn what you didn't know, then roll out in waves. Big-bang go-lives at 32 entities are how ERP projects become cautionary tales.
