Section 02

Why Ecosystems Win

Command and control scales to the limit of one person's bandwidth. Ecosystems scale to the limit of the mission. Those are very different ceilings — and the companies that figured this out are the ones that built legends.

Egosystem · Command and Control
  • Decisions flow up before they flow out
  • Speed is limited by the leader's calendar
  • Talent is managed, not unleashed
  • The org exists to serve the structure
  • Innovation requires permission
  • Presidents become executors, not owners
  • Culture becomes compliance
→
Ecosystem · Inspire · Influence · Innovate
  • Decisions are made closest to the work
  • Speed is limited only by clarity and trust
  • Talent is trusted, therefore unleashed
  • The structure exists to serve the mission
  • Innovation is everyone's responsibility
  • Presidents own their business unit fully
  • Culture becomes competitive advantage
Best-in-class parallels
Berkshire Hathaway
Warren Buffett acquires world-class companies and leaves their management alone. "We don't tell our managers what to do." The result: 60 years of compounding no ego system has matched. That's the CCO model at its highest expression.
Alphabet / Google
Waymo, DeepMind, Verily — each has its own CEO, P&L, and culture. Sundar Pichai doesn't run Waymo. He leads the leaders. The parent provides capital, talent pipeline, and brand. The companies provide innovation and execution.
LVMH
75 luxury brands. Each with its own creative director and president, full creative and operational autonomy. Bernard Arnault provides scale, financing, and shared infrastructure. The brands don't lose their identity — they gain the power to grow it.
Virgin Group
Richard Branson never managed Virgin Atlantic's flight schedules. His role was to inspire, protect the brand, and give each president the runway to build. Chief Commercial Officer — not chief controller. 400+ companies. One mission.
Elon Musk · SpaceX, Tesla, Starlink, xAI
Musk doesn't run Tesla's factories and SpaceX's launch teams simultaneously from a single command center. Each company has its own CEO, its own P&L, and its own culture — while sharing Musk's vision, capital network, and audacity ceiling. The relational equity he builds with his top leaders is what enables each company to move fast without waiting for permission. The CEOs who stay aren't managed. They're enrolled in a mission larger than any one company — and they wouldn't trade it.
Danny Meyer · Union Square Hospitality Group
Meyer built one of America's most celebrated restaurant empires — Shake Shack, Gramercy Tavern, The Modern — on a single principle: enlightened hospitality starts with your team, not your guests. Each restaurant has its own GM, its own identity, its own culture. Meyer's role is not to run the kitchens. It is to build leaders who do. His book "Setting the Table" is the closest analog to the Unbridled model in the hospitality world — and the reason his restaurants outlast trends while competitors chase them.
The Hub and Spoke and Wheel Model

The hub provides alignment and direction. Spokes connect leaders to teams. Wheels represent evolving groups that drive the business forward. As people grow, wheels expand or split into new specialties while staying connected to the hub. The result is a dynamic, adaptive system that reflects the fluid nature of business — and enables more collaborative, responsive ways of working.

Section 03

The Chief Commercial Officer

The CCO role in this ecosystem carries three distinct and equally important responsibilities — none of which are about control. They are about vision, voice, and velocity. The CCO leads the global sales function, champions the client experience above all else, and earns the right to lead Presidents not through authority but through relational equity and a future worth running toward.

The three mandates
Lead Globally

One Sales Organization

The CCO leads a consolidated global sales function — a shared service, like Finance or People and Possibilities, that serves the entire ecosystem. One team. One strategy. One voice in the market. Presidents partner with it; they don't duplicate it.

Champion the Client

The Client's Most Powerful Advocate

The CCO owns the client experience layer — making it strategic, effective, enjoyable, and valuable at every touchpoint. This is not a soft responsibility. It is the center of everything. The client voice lives here, and Presidents honor that as the north star of all we do.

See What's Next

Strategic Vision Carrier

The CCO is the conduit between the customer voice and the strategic future — translating what clients need into where the ecosystem goes next. Not reactive. Not operational. Forward-facing, always. The question is never "what happened?" It's "what's possible?"

The Global Sales Function

The global sales organization operates like Shared Services — one consolidated team led by a Head of Global Sales, reporting to and aligned with the CCO. It serves all six brands in the ecosystem. Presidents don't build their own sales infrastructures. They leverage this one.

This creates scale, consistency, and a unified client experience from first contact forward. The client never feels like they're navigating six separate companies. They feel Unbridled — one brand, one standard, one promise.

What Shared Services provides
  • → Unified go-to-market strategy across all 6 brands
  • → Consistent client experience from prospect to renewal
  • → Cross-brand pipeline visibility and opportunity routing
  • → Ecosystem-wide revenue intelligence and forecasting
  • → Client voice captured and distributed to all Presidents
  • → Strategic account growth and retention programs
The Client Experience Layer

The CCO is the most powerful client advocate in the ecosystem. Not because of title — because of presence, attention, and accountability. This means the client experience is never an afterthought assigned to account managers. It is a strategic priority owned at the highest commercial level.

Enjoyable

"The CCO is the conduit to the customer voice and the strategic vision of where we go next. Presidents need to honor the client experience as the center of all we do — not a department, not a metric. A way of being."

Relational Equity Over Authority

This is the most important leadership principle for the CCO — and the one most often missed. In an ecosystem of autonomous Presidents who hold equity in their own companies, authority is a blunt instrument. It breeds compliance, not commitment. The CCO earns alignment through relational equity — through trust built over time, through a vision so compelling that Presidents choose to run toward it, and through a future that makes joining this ecosystem the best decision they ever made.

Leading by Authority

  • Presidents comply because they have to
  • Alignment is forced, not earned
  • Vision is handed down
  • Resistance goes underground
  • Talent stays until something better comes
  • The future is managed, not inspired
  • Decisions happen in meetings
  • Presidents protect their turf

Leading by Relational Equity

  • Presidents commit because they believe
  • Alignment is chosen, not imposed
  • Vision is co-created and co-owned
  • Resistance surfaces and gets resolved
  • Talent stays because this is the best place
  • The future is inspired and pursued together
  • Decisions happen through trust
  • Presidents invest in each other's success
Leaders who built empires through relational equity — not authority
Steve Jobs · Apple
Jobs didn't manage Jony Ive, Tim Cook, or his creative leads through authority — he enrolled them in a vision of the future so vivid they couldn't imagine being anywhere else. His famous "reality distortion field" wasn't manipulation. It was conviction. People followed because the destination was worth it, and because he made them believe they were the only team capable of getting there.
Satya Nadella · Microsoft
When Nadella took over Microsoft, he didn't reorganize through force. He changed the story. "Mobile first, cloud first" was a vision that re-enrolled 150,000 people in a future they could believe in. The culture shifted from "know-it-alls" to "learn-it-alls" — and it happened through inspiration, not mandates. Microsoft's market cap went from $300B to over $3T. Relational equity at scale.
Howard Schultz · Starbucks
Schultz built Starbucks by making baristas feel like partners in something meaningful — not employees in a coffee chain. He extended health benefits to part-time workers decades before it was expected. The result: loyalty that no competitor could buy, and an army of brand ambassadors who chose the mission. You can't manufacture that with a policy. You earn it.
Reed Hastings · Netflix
Netflix's famous culture deck — "Freedom and Responsibility" — is a masterclass in relational equity. Hastings gave his leaders radical freedom and trusted them to act like owners. No approval chains. No micromanagement. Just a shared set of values and a vision for what great looks like. The people who stayed weren't the most obedient — they were the most inspired.
Pat Lencioni · The Table Group
Lencioni's entire body of work — Five Dysfunctions, The Advantage, The Ideal Team Player — is about one thing: trust as the foundation of leadership. His research shows that teams led by authority produce output. Teams led through relational equity produce breakthroughs. The difference is whether people feel safe enough to bring their full selves to the work. Sound familiar?
Danny Meyer · Union Square Hospitality
The godfather of "enlightened hospitality" built a restaurant empire on the principle that you take care of your team first, and they take care of guests. His GMs and leaders stayed not because of hierarchy — but because they believed in what they were building together. His book "Setting the Table" is the closest analog to the Unbridled model in the hospitality world. Culture as a commercial strategy.

"Authority gets people to show up.
Relational equity gets them to believe."

The CCO earns the right to lead Presidents by investing in the relationship before the ask, by co-creating the vision instead of dictating it, and by making the future of this ecosystem so compelling that no President would choose to be anywhere else.
