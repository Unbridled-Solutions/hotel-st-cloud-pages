Section 02

Why Ecosystems Win

Command and control scales to the limit of one person's bandwidth. Ecosystems scale to the limit of the mission. Those are very different ceilings — and the companies that figured this out are the ones that built legends.

Egosystem · Command and Control
  • Decisions flow up before they flow out
  • Speed is limited by the leader's calendar
  • Talent is managed, not unleashed
  • The org exists to serve the structure
  • Innovation requires permission
  • Presidents become executors, not owners
  • Culture becomes compliance
→
Ecosystem · Inspire · Influence · Innovate
  • Decisions are made closest to the work
  • Speed is limited only by clarity and trust
  • Talent is trusted, therefore unleashed
  • The structure exists to serve the mission
  • Innovation is everyone's responsibility
  • Presidents own their business unit fully
  • Culture becomes competitive advantage
Best-in-class parallels
Berkshire Hathaway
Warren Buffett acquires world-class companies and leaves their management alone. "We don't tell our managers what to do." The result: 60 years of compounding no ego system has matched. That's the CCO model at its highest expression.
Alphabet / Google
Waymo, DeepMind, Verily — each has its own CEO, P&L, and culture. Sundar Pichai doesn't run Waymo. He leads the leaders. The parent provides capital, talent pipeline, and brand. The companies provide innovation and execution.
LVMH
75 luxury brands. Each with its own creative director and president, full creative and operational autonomy. Bernard Arnault provides scale, financing, and shared infrastructure. The brands don't lose their identity — they gain the power to grow it.
Virgin Group
Richard Branson never managed Virgin Atlantic's flight schedules. His role was to inspire, protect the brand, and give each president the runway to build. Chief Catalyst — not chief controller. 400+ companies. One mission.
The Hub and Spoke and Wheel Model

The hub provides alignment and direction. Spokes connect leaders to teams. Wheels represent evolving groups that drive the business forward. As people grow, wheels expand or split into new specialties while staying connected to the hub. The result is a dynamic, adaptive system that reflects the fluid nature of business — and enables more collaborative, responsive ways of working.

Section 03

The Chief Catalyst Officer

The CCO role in an ecosystem is not smaller than in a command-and-control structure. It's bigger. Because instead of managing six companies, you're building six leaders who build companies that last hundreds of years. The title may say "Commercial" — but the job is Catalyst.

The three mandates
💡 Inspire

Cast the Vision

Paint a picture of the future so compelling that six Presidents choose to run toward it every day. Inspiration is the highest leverage thing a leader can do. One great vision multiplies across the entire ecosystem.

🌊 Influence

Shape Without Controlling

Use relationship, trust, and shared values to align six autonomous leaders — not directives. The most powerful influence is when a President makes the right call before you ever say a word.

🚀 Innovate

See What's Next

Spot opportunities the ecosystem can pursue together that no single brand could alone. Create the conditions for adjacent companies to spin out. Make the ecosystem more capable than the sum of its parts.

What shifts in this role

Command and Control

  • Approval required before Presidents decide
  • Information flows up before it flows out
  • Success measured by compliance
  • Presidents are executors of your vision
  • Problems escalate to the CCO
  • The CCO is the bottleneck
  • Meetings to update, not to create
  • Talent tolerates the structure

Ecosystem Leadership

  • Presidents decide — you align on direction
  • Information flows freely across the ecosystem
  • Success measured by growth and culture
  • Presidents are co-authors of the vision
  • Problems are solved where they occur
  • The CCO is the accelerant
  • Meetings to create, challenge, and connect
  • Talent chooses you because of the structure

"The CCO's job is not to manage six companies. It is to build six leaders who build companies that last hundreds of years. Control is a ceiling. Culture is a catalyst."

Decision Rights

Clarity is the antidote to control. When everyone knows who owns what, control becomes unnecessary. This matrix defines the decision rights across the ecosystem — so Presidents can move fast, and the CCO can lead the whole.

Decision Owner CCO Role
Commercial strategy — brand unit
Pricing, service mix, client approach		 President Aligns with ecosystem direction. Consults on cross-brand opportunity.
Hiring within the brand unit
Roles, offers, team structure		 President Sets cultural standards. Approves senior hires (VP+).
Client relationships
Who to pursue, how to serve, delivery standards		 President Unlocks cross-ecosystem client introductions. Protects brand reputation.
P&L management
Margin, spend, resource allocation		 Shared Sets ecosystem financial targets. Presidents manage to them.
Cross-brand collaboration
Joint pitches, shared resources, referrals		 CCO Orchestrates. Creates conditions. Removes friction between brands.
Ecosystem growth strategy
New companies, market expansion, acquisitions		 CCO Leads strategy. Presidents contribute market intelligence and readiness.
Cultural standards
Values, cornerstones, team member experience		 Shared Holds the line on culture. Presidents are culture carriers in their units.
Annual compensation — President level
Base, variable, equity		 CCO Owns. Informed by unit performance and ecosystem contribution.
Day-to-day operations
Scheduling, delivery, team management		 President Not involved. This is the President's domain entirely.
Brand identity and positioning
How the brand shows up in market		 Shared Sets ecosystem brand standards. Presidents own brand voice within units.

This matrix is a living document. When ambiguity arises, the answer is always: who is closest to the work and most equipped to decide?
