Overall Assessment
Where This RFP Stands

The document is a solid foundation — well-organized, comprehensive in functional scope, and clearly written. The functional requirements sections (5.1–5.8) are notably strong. The gaps are concentrated in the back half: implementation rigor, data migration specifics, multi-entity depth, and the contractual teeth that prevent post-signature surprises.

Below are 14 gaps ranked by severity, followed by suggested language to add. Most are additions to existing sections — not rewrites.

What's Working
Strengths to Keep
  • ✅
    Section 5.3 Marketing Operations is an excellent addition
    Most ERP RFPs skip this entirely or lump it under CRM. Your inclusion of email/SMS/social campaigns, A/B testing, consent management (CAN-SPAM/CASL/GDPR), and closed-loop CRM reporting is thorough and will surface real platform differences. Keep it exactly as written.
  • ✅
    User table (Section 6) is specific and useful
    ~225 total users across 5 roles gives vendors what they need to price correctly. Most RFPs say "approximately X users" without the breakdown — yours is better than most.
  • ✅
    Deltek/ClickUp/Nimble/Lattice + optional UKG is clearly scoped
    Naming the incumbent systems up front is exactly right. Vendors know what they're replacing and can speak to migration paths and feature parity. Don't bury this — consider moving it to an earlier section.
  • ✅
    EMEA + UK compliance callout in Section 5.6
    Most SMB-positioned RFPs miss international compliance entirely. Including US/UK/EMEA data privacy signals organizational sophistication and will filter out vendors who can't actually support it.
  • ✅
    Evaluation criteria (Section 11) is well-balanced
    Six criteria covering functional fit, vendor expertise, methodology, pricing, scalability, and support is a solid rubric. Consider adding weighted percentages so vendors know what matters most.
Gap Analysis
14 Gaps — Ranked by Severity
  • 💾
    Data Migration — Dangerously Underspecified
    Critical
    Section 3 mentions "data migration" as part of scope in one line. That's it. You're migrating from Deltek Vantagepoint, ClickUp, Nimble, and Lattice — four systems with very different data models, years of history, and no clean export standards. Data migration is where 20–30% of ERP implementations fail or blow their budget. Without specifics in the RFP, you'll get a generic "we have a migration tool" response and a change order after contract.
    Add to Section 3 (Scope of Work): A dedicated Data Migration sub-section listing: source systems and approximate record counts (Deltek GL history, Nimble contacts, ClickUp projects, Lattice performance data), years of history required, data cleansing responsibilities (client vs. vendor), validation and sign-off process, cutover approach (big bang vs. parallel run), and who owns post-migration data issues.
  • 📋
    SLA Definitions — Completely Absent
    Critical
    Section 8 asks vendors to "detail SLAs" but provides zero parameters. Without anchoring what you expect, vendors will propose whatever they know they can hit — which is rarely what you actually need. A system-down event at month-end across 32 entities is a P1 emergency with a 1-hour response requirement. Your RFP doesn't say that.
    Add to Section 8 (Post-Implementation Support): Define SLA tiers explicitly and ask vendors to respond to each. Minimum expectations: P1 system-down = 1-hour response, 4-hour resolution target; P2 major function impaired = 4-hour response; P3 minor issues = next business day. Uptime: 99.9% minimum for cloud, with financial remedies for breach. Scheduled maintenance windows. Ask vendors to propose SLAs above these minimums as a differentiator.
  • 🏢
    Multi-Entity Section (5.6) is Too Thin for a 32-Entity Portfolio
    Critical
    Section 5.6 is three sentences covering "separate ledgers, entity-specific tax requirements, and consolidated reporting." For an organization with 32 entities, this is the most complex requirement in the entire RFP — and it gets less space than the marketing email section. Generic answers to vague requirements lead to surprises post-contract.
    Expand Section 5.6 significantly. Must add: automated intercompany elimination workflow, consolidated CoA with entity-level mapping, centralized AP/AR with multi-entity payment processing, entity-level P&L and balance sheet generation, time-to-close for consolidated financials (ask for a real number from a reference), process for adding a new entity mid-year (timeline + cost), and RBAC that restricts entity-level users from seeing other entities' data. See suggested language block below.
  • 🧠
    Change Management — Not Mentioned Anywhere
    Critical
    You're asking ~225 users across multiple entities to abandon Deltek, ClickUp, Nimble, and Lattice — four systems with established workflows — and adopt a single new platform. The technology risk is manageable. The people risk is 70% of why implementations fail. The RFP never asks vendors how they handle it.
    Add to Section 8 (Implementation and Support): Require vendors to submit a change management plan covering stakeholder identification, executive sponsorship approach, communication strategy, resistance mitigation, and role-specific adoption plans. Ask specifically: "How do you handle resistance from power users of the legacy systems being replaced?" Weight this in your evaluation criteria.
  • 🚀
    Hypercare / Go-Live Support Period Not Defined
    High
    Section 8 asks for "post-implementation support" details but doesn't distinguish between the hypercare period (first 30–60 days after go-live, highest risk, requires dedicated support) and steady-state support. These are very different things. Many vendors consider go-live the project finish line. It's actually the starting line.
    Add to Section 8: "Describe your hypercare model — specifically: duration of dedicated hypercare support post-go-live, team availability (hours/days), escalation path for critical issues during hypercare, and the formal criteria and process for transitioning from hypercare to steady-state support."
  • 📅
    RFP Schedule Dates Are All Blank
    High
    Section 12 has placeholder [Date] fields for RFP release, submission deadline, shortlist presentations, and final selection. A vendor receiving this RFP can't plan their response effort without a timeline. This also signals the RFP isn't quite ready to send — which you probably know, but it's the first thing vendors notice.
    Fill in all four dates before distributing. Recommended timeline: 3 weeks for vendor response, 1 week for initial scoring, 2 weeks for shortlist demos, 1 week for final selection. That's a 7-week process from release to decision — realistic for this scope.
  • 🗺️
    No Organizational Structure Context for Vendors
    High
    Vendors know you have "multiple companies and regions" (Section 1) but don't know how many entities, what industries they're in, how the shared services relationship works, or which entities are legally distinct vs. divisions of the same entity. This directly affects their scoping, pricing, and proposed architecture.
    Add to Section 2 (Project Background): A brief org structure note — number of legal entities, general industry mix (hospitality, professional services, etc.), geographic footprint (CO-based? + UK/EMEA?), and how the shared services function operates (centralized finance + HR for all entities). You don't need to name every entity — just give vendors enough to scope correctly.
  • 📈
    Evaluation Criteria Has No Weights
    High
    Section 11 lists six evaluation criteria but doesn't weight them. This creates two problems: vendors don't know what to emphasize, and your internal evaluation team will score inconsistently. Functional fit and pricing will get debated endlessly without an agreed-upon weighting framework.
    Add percentage weights to Section 11. Suggested: Functional/Technical Fit 30%, Implementation Methodology 20%, Vendor Expertise 20%, Pricing/TCO 15%, Scalability 10%, Support/Service 5%. Adjust to your priorities — but commit to weights before proposals arrive.
  • 💰
    Pricing Section Doesn't Ask for 3-Year TCO Model
    High
    Section 10 asks for implementation fees, licensing, training, and support — which is good. But it doesn't require a multi-year total cost of ownership model. Year 1 costs are usually distorted by implementation. Years 2–3 reveal whether the platform is actually affordable at scale.
    Add to Section 10: "Provide a 3-year Total Cost of Ownership model — Year 1 (implementation + licensing + training), Year 2 (steady-state licensing + support), Year 3 (with projected growth to [X] users and [Y] entities). Itemize all cost components. Flag any costs that could vary outside the proposal assumptions."
  • 🎓
    Training Plan Lacks User-Type Differentiation
    Medium
    Section 8 mentions "end-user training plan, documentation, and knowledge transfer to internal IT staff" — which is the right intent. But your user table shows 5 very different user types (Financial Management, CRM, PM, HCM, and General/T&E). A barista logging time has different training needs than a finance controller running consolidations. One training plan cannot serve both.
    Add to Section 8: "Provide a role-differentiated training plan addressing the five user groups identified in Section 6. Include training modalities for each group (in-person, video, self-paced), duration, materials ownership, and a plan for training new users as staff turns over post-go-live."
  • 🔒
    RBAC Requirements Are Implicit, Not Explicit
    Medium
    Section 7 mentions "role-based security" as a technical requirement but doesn't describe what the access model needs to look like. In a 32-entity shared services model, RBAC is operationally critical — entity-level users should not see other entities' financial data, and shared services staff need cross-entity access. This needs to be specified.
    Add to Section 7: "Describe your role-based access control model for multi-entity deployments. Specifically: how are permissions scoped to individual entities vs. across entities? Can a single user have different permission levels for different entities? How are new entity-level users provisioned without granting access to other entities?"
  • ⚙️
    Integration Ownership Post-Go-Live Not Addressed
    Medium
    Section 7 says "ability to integrate with third-party solutions, such as sales tax software and UKG payroll" — which surfaces the requirement but leaves a major gap: who maintains integrations when a third-party API changes? UKG releases API updates. Tax software vendors update endpoints. When that happens 18 months after go-live, who owns the fix — and at what cost?
    Add to Section 7: "For each proposed integration, describe: who owns ongoing maintenance when third-party APIs change, what the update process looks like, and how integration failures are detected and resolved. Is integration maintenance included in the support contract or billed separately?"
  • 🏗️
    Phased vs. Big-Bang Go-Live Not Addressed
    Medium
    Section 8 asks for a project plan but doesn't state whether you intend to go live with all entities simultaneously or in phases. For 32 entities, a big-bang approach is high risk. Your silence on this will produce a mix of proposals — some phased, some not — that are impossible to compare apples-to-apples.
    Add to Section 8: State your preference or ask vendors to recommend an approach: "Given our 32-entity portfolio, propose a phased implementation approach that balances go-live risk with speed to value. Identify which functional modules or entity groups you would recommend for Phase 1 and your rationale."
  • 🌍
    EMEA/UK Compliance Is Named But Not Specified
    Low
    Section 5.6 mentions "United States, United Kingdom, and EMEA regions" without specifying what compliance requirements you actually face (GDPR, UK GDPR post-Brexit, VAT, employment law differences). Vendors will likely respond with boilerplate compliance statements. If you have real exposure in these jurisdictions, you need to be specific.
    Add to Section 5.6: If you have employees or operations in the UK or EMEA, enumerate the specific requirements: GDPR data residency requirements, UK payroll and employment compliance (HMRC, RTI), VAT treatment in the UK and EU. If you don't currently have EMEA operations but are planning for them, say so — "forward compatibility required."
Ready-to-Use Text
Suggested Language to Add
These are sections you can drop directly into the RFP with minimal editing.
Add to Section 5.6 — Multi-Entity Support (Expanded)

The ERP solution must support Unbridled Solutions' multi-entity structure, which includes up to 32 legal entities operating under a shared services model. The shared services group provides centralized financial management and HR infrastructure for all entities. Specific requirements include:

Intercompany management: Automated intercompany transaction recording, reconciliation, and elimination. Cost recharge workflows from the shared services entity to operating entities. Intercompany loan and equity transaction support.

Chart of Accounts: A standardized group-level CoA that all entities map to, with the flexibility to maintain entity-specific accounts where local operational or regulatory requirements differ.

Consolidated reporting: Real-time or near real-time consolidated P&L, Balance Sheet, and Cash Flow Statement across all entities. Drill-down from consolidated line items to entity-level transactions. Please provide the typical consolidation run time for a client with 20+ entities.

Entity scalability: Describe the process, timeline, and cost to add a new legal entity to the system. We anticipate adding 3–5 entities over the next 3 years.

Role-based entity access: Entity-level users must be restricted to their entity's data. Shared services staff require cross-entity access with configurable permission levels per entity.

Add to Section 3 — Data Migration (New Sub-Section)

Data migration is a critical component of this implementation. Unbridled Solutions is migrating from four source systems: Deltek Vantagepoint (ERP/financials/project accounting), ClickUp (project and resource management), Nimble (CRM/contacts), and Lattice (performance management). UKG payroll data migration is in scope if UKG is replaced.

Vendor proposals must address: (1) migration scope and approach for each source system, (2) years of historical data to be migrated (to be confirmed during discovery), (3) data cleansing responsibilities — what is the client's obligation vs. the vendor's, (4) validation and sign-off process before cutover, (5) cutover approach (big-bang vs. parallel run) with recommendation and rationale, and (6) post-migration support process for data issues discovered after go-live. Please provide an estimated cost range for data migration as a separate line item.

Add to Section 8 — Change Management

Given the scope of this implementation — replacing four established systems used by approximately 225 users across multiple entities and functional roles — change management is a required deliverable, not an optional add-on.

Vendor proposals must include a change management plan covering: stakeholder identification and engagement approach, executive sponsorship strategy, communication plan for affected user groups, resistance mitigation approach (specifically for power users of the systems being replaced), and role-specific adoption milestones. Please describe how you have handled change management challenges in comparable multi-entity, multi-system replacement implementations.

Add to Section 8 — SLA Requirements

Proposals must include defined Service Level Agreements for the following categories. Unbridled Solutions' minimum expectations are listed; vendors are encouraged to propose SLAs that exceed these thresholds:

System availability: 99.9% uptime minimum for production environment, excluding pre-scheduled maintenance windows. Maintenance windows must be outside business hours (MT) and require 5 business days' advance notice.

Incident response: P1 (system down or critical function unavailable) — 1-hour response, 4-hour resolution target. P2 (major function impaired, workaround available) — 4-hour response, next business day resolution. P3 (minor issues, no workaround needed) — next business day acknowledgment.

SLA remedies: Describe financial or service credits applied when SLA thresholds are not met.

Hypercare: Describe the dedicated hypercare support model for the first 60 days post-go-live, including team availability, escalation path, and criteria for transitioning to steady-state support.
