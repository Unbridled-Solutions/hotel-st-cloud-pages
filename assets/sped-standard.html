Section 01

The Legal Foundation

Know this before you coach anyone. These aren't guidelines — they're legal requirements with federal and state enforcement teeth.

Federal Law: IDEA

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) guarantees every eligible student ages 3–21 a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) delivered in the Least Restrictive Environment (LRE). Every student with a disability must have an Individualized Education Program (IEP). Every teacher responsible for implementing that IEP must have access to it — and must follow it.

Colorado State Law: ECEA

Colorado's Exceptional Children's Educational Act (ECEA), implemented via 1 CCR 301-8 (updated May 2025), mirrors IDEA and adds Colorado-specific requirements:

  • LRE is mandatory — removal from gen ed requires documented, defensible justification
  • IEPs must be accessible to every teacher and service provider before instruction begins
  • Related services (OT, PT, speech, counseling, transportation) are legal obligations — not suggestions
  • Transition services for students 16+ must be documented, coordinated, and results-oriented
  • Progress toward IEP goals must be measured and reported to parents regularly
The Point

"A teacher who doesn't know a student's IEP, doesn't follow it, or removes that student from gen ed without justification isn't just falling short professionally. They're exposing the student, themselves, and this district to a legal violation."

What Every Teacher with an IEP Student Is Required to Do

  • Know the student's IEP goals, accommodations, and modifications
  • Implement accommodations and modifications as written
  • Collect and report progress data on IEP goals
  • Attend IEP meetings when required
  • Communicate with the SPED case manager when a student isn't making progress
  • Never remove a student from gen ed without IEP team authorization
Legal Citation
IDEA 34 CFR §300.323(d): IEPs must be accessible to all teachers and service providers responsible for implementation as soon as possible following the development of the IEP — not when convenient.
Section 02

The LRE Continuum

LRE is not a room. It's a continuum. Placement decisions must start at the most inclusive end and move toward restriction only when — and only as far as — the evidence requires.

The Presumption

"The law presumes general ed. The IEP team must document why it can't work — not why the student should be there."

Key question Sam should ask about any student in full-time pull-out: "Does this student's IEP contain a documented, IEP-team-approved rationale for why gen ed with supports cannot be achieved satisfactorily?" If the answer is no — that's a compliance issue, not a scheduling issue.

Section 03

CDE Teacher Effectiveness Rubric — SPED Lens

Every expectation Sam sets can and should be tied to how teachers are evaluated. This isn't just about compliance — it's about professional performance. Two rubrics apply: the standard Teacher Rubric and the Special Education Teacher Rubric (both available at ed.cde.state.co.us).

Quality Standard SPED Connection What Failure Looks Like Coaching Lever
I — Content Mastery & Pedagogy Differentiation is a requirement for every learner, including IEP students Refusing to modify instruction; treating IEP as "someone else's problem" "An IEP isn't a reason to send a kid out. It's your instruction guide for that student."
II — Safe, Inclusive Environment IDEA's LRE mandate is the legal expression of an inclusive environment Unnecessary removal from gen ed; treating SPED students as outsiders "An inclusive environment isn't a preference. It's a legal requirement we evaluate you on."
III — Curriculum, Instruction & Assessment IEP accommodations ARE the assessment plan for that student Giving standard assessments when IEP mandates extended time or alternate format "Ignoring IEP accommodations on a test isn't just a policy problem. It may be a legal one."
IV — Reflection & Continuous Improvement IEP goal data is the evidence of reflection and adjustment No data collected; goals copied year over year unchanged "If you can't show me data on a student's IEP goals, I have no way to evaluate whether you're doing your job."
V — Leadership, Collaboration & Design Co-teaching, IEP participation, and case manager collaboration are leadership behaviors Missing IEP meetings; refusing to co-plan; treating SPED teacher as a para "Collaboration with the SPED team is part of how we measure whether you're an effective educator."
VI — Professional Responsibility FAPE, LRE, and IEP obligations are professional duties with legal force Knowingly ignoring IEP requirements; attributing it to time or workload "You agreed to educate every student assigned to you. That agreement has legal force."
Section 04

Scenarios & Coaching Responses

Six situations Sam will encounter. Each is color-coded by teacher posture: green = good faith (coaching first), yellow = negligence (coaching with accountability), red = active resistance (formal and documented from day one).

Scenario 01 · Good Faith

"That's Not My Student" — Gen Ed Teacher Refusing IEP Accommodations

"I don't have time for all these modifications. That's the SPED teacher's job."
IDEA 34 CFR §300.323(d) Rubric: QS II, III, VI
Sam's Response — First Instance
  • 1One-on-one, neutral and curious: "Walk me through what you're currently doing for [student]."
  • 2Put the IEP in front of them: "Here's what's required. Which of these are you currently doing?"
  • 3Problem-solve together: "What would you need from me or the SPED team to make this work?"
  • 4Set a 30-day check-in with observable expectations and a specific date.
  • 5Document the conversation. Follow up by email the same day with a written summary.
If Pattern Continues Formal observation with rubric scoring → written feedback → formal improvement plan citing specific IEP violations → notify principal and district SPED director. Repeated documented failure to implement IEPs can constitute a district compliance violation reportable to CDE.
Scenario 02 · Negligence

Students Parked in the Resource Room All Day

Students with mild-to-moderate disabilities spending 80–100% of the day in a self-contained room — without IEPs that justify full removal.
ECEA §5.01(2) / IDEA 34 CFR §300.114 Rubric: QS II
Sam's Response — Systemic Audit Required
  • 1Pull IEPs for every student in full-time pull-out. Ask for each: Does this IEP justify full removal? Is there a documented reason gen ed with supports won't work?
  • 2Where the answer is no → schedule IEP team meetings to revisit placement.
  • 3Coach the SPED teacher: "Walk me through your rationale for each student's current placement."
  • 4Introduce the LRE continuum as a shared team framework: "Our starting point is gen ed. We move toward restriction only when we have documented reasons."
  • 5Build a re-integration plan. Partner with gen ed teachers to prepare for returning students.
If SPED Teacher Resists IEP Team Review Formal coaching plan. If the IEP team itself is complicit → escalate to principal and district SPED coordinator. This is the scenario most likely to generate a parent complaint or a CDE compliance investigation. Document everything.
Scenario 03 · Negligence

IEP Goals Are Never Tracked or Updated

Goals are boilerplate. Data doesn't exist or isn't shared with parents. Same goals reappear every year.
IDEA 34 CFR §300.320(a)(3) Rubric: QS III, IV
Sam's Response
  • 1Request a random sample of IEPs and progress documentation. Ask: "Show me the data you're collecting on [student's] goals."
  • 2If data doesn't exist: "Walk me through your current system. I want to help you build one that works."
  • 3Introduce or require a consistent data collection method — even simple (weekly tallies, work samples, brief observation notes).
  • 4Establish that progress reports to parents are required per IEP schedule and must reflect real data — not "making progress."
  • 5Coach on goal quality: "A goal that says 'will improve reading' isn't measurable. Let's rewrite it so we can actually tell when it's been met."
If Pattern Continues Formal plan tied to rubric scores on QS IV. If parent complaints arise about lack of progress reporting → escalate to principal and district SPED director immediately.
Scenario 04 · Active Resistance

Teacher Knows the Rules and Is Choosing Not to Follow Them

"I've been doing this 20 years." / "Admin doesn't actually enforce this." / "These kids don't belong in my classroom."
IDEA / ECEA — all applicable Rubric: QS VI — with intent
Sam's Response — Formal from Day One
  • 1This is a formal coaching conversation from the first instance: "I want to be direct. What I'm observing is not in compliance with [student's] IEP and Colorado law. That needs to change."
  • 2State expectations specifically: "By [date], I expect to see [X]. This is not optional."
  • 3Document the conversation and email a written summary the same day.
  • 4Conduct a formal observation within two weeks. Score the rubric honestly — Basic or below where warranted.
  • 5Issue a formal written improvement plan if no change is evident. Engage HR and principal early — do not carry this alone.
What Not to Do
Don't accept "I was busy" when the teacher clearly knew. Don't soften rubric scores to avoid conflict — that protects the teacher, not the students. Don't wait for multiple incidents before documenting. Start on day one.
Escalation Path Formal evaluation with accurate rubric scores → formal improvement plan with time-bound expectations → if no improvement, recommendation for non-renewal or termination through district HR. Notify district SPED director of the compliance risk. This protects Sam and the district.
Scenario 05 · Negligence

IEP Meetings — Teachers Never Attend, Never Contribute

Gen ed teachers aren't being invited, or they're invited and skip. IEP teams are making decisions without the people responsible for implementation.
IDEA 34 CFR §300.321 — IEP Team Composition Rubric: QS V
Sam's Response
  • 1Set the expectation at the start of the year: "If you have a student with an IEP in your class, you are part of that student's team. IEP meetings are a professional obligation."
  • 2Work with case managers to confirm gen ed teachers are being notified correctly and ahead of time.
  • 3If a teacher misses a meeting: "You were required to be at [student's] IEP meeting. Walk me through why you weren't there."
  • 4Build a system for brief IEP summaries — teachers don't need 30-page documents, but they do need the accommodations, goals, and services for their students.
  • 5Include IEP meeting attendance as part of professional evaluation documentation.
Legal Note An IEP developed without a required gen ed teacher may be procedurally invalid. If a teacher's absence contributed to this → notify district SPED director and general counsel.
Scenario 06 · Good Faith / Structural

Co-Teaching Model Is Being Abused

The SPED co-teacher is in the room but functioning as a para — grading papers, managing behavior, sitting in the back. No real co-teaching is happening.
IDEA — FAPE via specially designed instruction Rubric: QS I, III, V
Sam's Response
  • 1Observe co-taught classrooms explicitly. Note: who is leading instruction, who has active relationships with IEP students, who is co-planning.
  • 2Debrief with both teachers: "Walk me through how you're co-planning. Who owns what, and when?"
  • 3Introduce co-teaching models formally (One Teach/One Assist, Station Teaching, Parallel Teaching, Team Teaching) and set expectations.
  • 4Require joint lesson planning — the SPED teacher should be in the plan, not just in the room.
  • 5Evaluate co-teachers as a team and individually. The SPED teacher's rubric scores should reflect actual instructional leadership.
Section 05

Coaching Language That Lands

These phrases work because they're specific, professional, and grounded — not personal. Sam can pull them before a hard conversation and adapt to the moment.

To Open a Coaching Conversation
"Walk me through what you're currently doing for [student]."
"Help me understand your thinking on this placement."
"Show me the data you're collecting on [student's] goals."
To State an Expectation Clearly
"Here's what I need to see by [date]. This isn't optional — it's a legal requirement and a professional standard."
"What I observed doesn't match what [student's] IEP requires. Let's fix that."
"The standard here is clear. Right now we're not meeting it."
For the Resistant Teacher
"I hear that you've been doing this a long time. The standard is still the standard."
"I'm not here to make your life harder. I am here to make sure these students get what they're owed."
For the Good-Faith Teacher Who Just Doesn't Know
"This is fixable. Here's what I need from you, and here's how I'll support you."
"Let's build a system together so this doesn't fall through the cracks."
"What would you need from me or the SPED team to make this work in your classroom?"
Section 06

First 90 Days

The moves that establish Sam as credible, professional, and serious — without coming in hot. The goal of the first 90 days isn't to fix everything. It's to make clear that things are going to be different, and that "different" is grounded in the law and in genuine care for students.

Days1–30

Listen. Audit. Observe.

  • Schedule 1:1s with every SPED staff member: "I want to understand what's working and where you feel stuck."
  • Pull a random IEP sample. Read them. Check for measurable goals, LRE justification, documented accommodations.
  • Observe at least one period per SPED teacher — formal and informal.
  • Talk to case managers about the biggest compliance pain points they're navigating.
  • Meet with the district SPED director. Understand the compliance history.
  • Say very little publicly. This phase is about knowing what's real.
Days31–60

Set the Standard. Build the Culture.

  • Host a SPED staff kickoff: "Here's who I am, here's what we're here to do, and here's the standard we're holding."
  • Introduce LRE framework and IEP implementation expectations — clearly and in writing.
  • Establish a shared data collection system for IEP goal progress.
  • Begin formal IEP audits. Identify highest-risk placements for LRE review.
  • First 1:1 coaching conversations with gen ed teachers about IEP accommodations.
  • Coach toward compliance before documenting non-compliance — give good-faith teachers a runway.
Days61–90

Accountability in Motion.

  • First formal observations with rubric-based, specific written feedback.
  • IEP team meetings for students where placement needs to be revisited.
  • Formal coaching plans for teachers not meeting expectations.
  • Communicate wins — catch people doing it right and say so publicly.
  • Build the documentation habit: every coaching conversation summarized in writing, same day.
  • Check in with the district SPED director. This is not a solo project.
Section 07

Quick-Reference Legal Citations

Pull these when a conversation requires backing up an expectation with law. Teachers who push back on "the rules" should see the actual citation.

FAPE — Free Appropriate Public Education
IDEA 20 U.S.C. §1400 · ECEA §2.21

Special education and related services provided at public expense, meeting state standards, in conformity with an IEP.

LRE — Least Restrictive Environment
IDEA 34 CFR §300.114 · ECEA §5.01(2)

Students with disabilities must be educated with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. Removal requires documented justification.

IEP Requirements
IDEA 34 CFR §300.320 · ECEA §5.02

Measurable annual goals, accommodations, progress reporting, LRE explanation, and transition services (age 16+).

IEP Team Composition
IDEA 34 CFR §300.321

Must include at least one regular education teacher when the student is (or may be) participating in general ed. Not optional.

IEP Accessibility to Teachers
IDEA 34 CFR §300.323(d)

All teachers and service providers responsible for implementation must have access to the IEP as soon as possible following its development.

Progress Reporting to Parents
IDEA 34 CFR §300.320(a)(3)

IEP must include how parents will be regularly informed of progress toward annual goals — per IEP schedule, not once a year.

Related Services
IDEA 34 CFR §300.34 · ECEA §2.38

OT, PT, speech, counseling, transportation, psychology, school health services — all legal obligations when written into an IEP.
