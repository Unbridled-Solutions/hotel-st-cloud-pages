Chapter I
The World Before

The guard at the compound gate was named Emmanuel and he had been at his post since six and Gloria could tell from the way he stood that it had been a quiet night.

She knew all three of the guards this way — by their posture, by the particular quality of their attention at different hours, by the small signals that a woman learned to read when she had been living inside a walled compound for four years and the guards were part of the daily texture of her life. Emmanuel on the day shift stood differently when something had disturbed the night than when nothing had. Right now his shoulders were relaxed, his weight on his left foot, his eyes moving over the street outside with the unhurried scan of a man whose night had given him nothing to report.

Gloria noted this the way she noted all of it — without making a production of it, without letting it rise to the level of conscious thought. She had been doing it long enough that it had become automatic. Read the compound. Read the street. Read the faces of the people who moved through her world. Jackson had never asked her to do this. She had started doing it on her own, sometime in the first year in Jinja, and had never stopped.

She crossed the courtyard to the kitchen with the morning still cool around her and the bougainvillea on the compound wall very pink in the early light and the smell of the lake coming over the wall from the north, that particular clean smell that she had not expected to love as much as she did when they first arrived and that she had come to think of as the smell of home.

Jackson had been gone eight days.

* * *

The kitchen was hers in the morning. By seven-thirty it would belong to the children — Marcus arriving at full velocity, already talking, the overnight accumulation of thoughts and questions and things he had decided were important releasing the moment his feet hit the tile. Amara following at her own pace, quieter, finding her chair and her cup with the deliberate movements of a child who had decided at some point that mornings required ceremony. But before seven-thirty the kitchen was Gloria’s and she moved through it with the efficiency of a woman who had learned to take her stillness in the early hours because the hours after would not offer it.

She put the kettle on. Opened the window above the sink. The compound’s back garden was in the window — the jacaranda tree that Amara had claimed, its lower branches worn smooth where small hands gripped them, and the vegetable bed that Marcus had started with enormous ambition six months ago and tended with decreasing frequency since. Three tomato plants still alive. A row of something that had come up green and vigorous and that neither of them had been able to identify with certainty.

Jackson had planted a mango tree the week before he left for Congo. In the far corner, small now, a stake beside it to keep it straight. He had dug the hole himself on a Sunday afternoon while Marcus supervised and offered suggestions about depth and Amara sat in the dirt beside him handing him things he didn’t need. Gloria had watched from the kitchen window. Jackson in the dirt with his children on a Sunday afternoon in the garden of the house they had made together in this city on this road above the lake.

She looked at the mango tree now in the early light. It was still straight.

The kettle came to boil and she made tea and took it to the table and opened her Bible and read for twenty minutes in the quiet of the kitchen before the compound woke fully around her.

* * *

Nile Crescent was not the road she had expected to live on when Jackson first told her about Jinja.

She had looked it up on a map and seen a secondary road looping through a residential neighborhood north of the town center, not far from the lake, the kind of road that appeared on maps as a thin line with no particular significance. She had formed a picture of it that turned out to be wrong in almost every detail. The road itself was real enough — unpaved in sections, the red earth of it washing across the tarmac after heavy rains, lined with compound walls behind which the actual life of the neighborhood was invisible from outside. But the neighborhood was not what she had imagined. It was alive in the specific way of East African residential areas that had grown organically over decades — compounds of different sizes beside each other, some with the high walls and razor wire of serious security, some with low decorative walls and open gates, the whole mixture creating a texture that she had walked for four years and still found new things in.

The neighbors she knew. The Nakigozi family three compounds down, whose daughter Patience was in the same class as Amara and came home with her twice a week. The old man next door, Mr. Wasswa, who had lived in his compound for forty years and grew things over his wall that dropped into their courtyard and that Marcus collected as specimens. The Kapere family across the road, recent arrivals from Kampala, who had introduced themselves formally and correctly and whom Gloria had been slowly learning over the past year.

She had not expected to feel at home here.

She had expected to manage it — to create a functional life in a place she would always know was not her original place, the way she had managed the various postings and relocations that Jackson’s work had produced before Jinja. Managed was the right word for those earlier places. Competent management of circumstances not of her choosing.

Jinja was different. She had not managed Jinja. She had arrived and paid attention and at some point the attention had become something else — recognition, maybe. The feeling that a place had the qualities she had been looking for without knowing she was looking for them.

The lake. The climate, which was warm without the suffocating humidity of coastal cities. The market at the town center where she had started going on Saturday mornings and where she now had vendors who recognized her and set things aside. The clinic, where she volunteered two days a week and where the work was real in a way that clinic work in other places hadn’t always been. The women from the church who had included her without requiring anything of her first, the rare generosity of a community that let you arrive before deciding whether you were worth keeping.

She had told Jackson on one of his returns — a Tuesday night, the children in bed, the two of them on the back step with the garden dark around them and the lake somewhere beyond the wall — that she thought she could live here the rest of her life.

He had looked at her for a moment. Then he had said: good. And she had heard in that single word everything he couldn’t say about what it meant to him that the life he’d asked her to build in this place had become something she wanted.

* * *

Marcus came down at seven twenty-three, which was ahead of schedule.

He was eight years old and he moved through the world at a velocity that Gloria had stopped trying to moderate. He had Jackson’s physical directness — the way of moving toward things rather than around them — and her own determination once he had decided something mattered. The combination produced a child who was exhausting and delightful in roughly equal measure and who had, in Gloria’s assessment, the gift of making other people feel that their company was interesting to him. Teachers reported this. The neighbors’ children reported it in their own way by appearing at the compound gate asking for Marcus with the frequency of children who had found somewhere they wanted to be.

He came into the kitchen with his animal notebook under his arm. This was a composition book he had been keeping for two years, in which he recorded every animal he encountered with a drawing and a notation about the circumstances. The drawings had improved considerably over the two years. The notations were precise in the way of someone who had been told that precision mattered and had taken the instruction to heart.

‘I was thinking about the hippos,’ he said, pulling out his chair.

‘Good morning,’ Gloria said.

‘Good morning.’ He sat. ‘If a hippo is in the water you can see it from the boat. But Robert said sometimes they come onto the bank at night. So technically there could be a hippo right outside the lodge at night and we wouldn’t know.’

‘Robert said we’d be in our rooms at night.’

‘I know. But I could hear it. If a hippo walked past the window I would hear it.’ He opened the notebook to a page where he had been working on a hippo drawing. The hippo was extremely round. ‘They’re very loud.’

‘Where did you learn that.’

‘Joseph told me. He said hippos are louder than you expect.’ Marcus looked at the drawing critically. ‘I’m not sure I have the legs right. The legs are short but they’re also quite thick.’

‘Eat something,’ Gloria said.

He ate. Between bites he revised the legs. Gloria watched him and drank her tea and thought about what the drive would be like with Joseph and the two children in the vehicle for four hours and felt, not for the first time since Jackson’s call, the shape of his absence in what should have been there.

The park had been planned for months. All four of them — Jackson had booked it himself, which was unusual, sitting at the kitchen table on a Sunday with his laptop and the children leaning over his shoulders making suggestions he sometimes accepted and sometimes didn’t. He had shown Marcus the map and Marcus had identified which animals he expected to see and Jackson had confirmed the plausible ones and deflected the implausible ones with the straight face of a man who had decided that his son’s optimism about encountering a mountain gorilla in a savannah park was not worth correcting at this particular moment.

Then Congo had extended.

Jackson’s call had come nine days ago, the connection uncertain, his voice with the quality it had from certain locations where he was speaking carefully without being able to say why. It’s going to be longer, he’d said. I don’t know how much longer. Don’t cancel the park.

She hadn’t argued. There was nothing to argue with — Jackson didn’t extend trips by choice, and argument over a bad connection resolved nothing. She had said: all right. He had said: take Joseph. She had said: I know. He had said: I’m sorry. She had said: I know that too.

The line had held for another forty seconds during which neither of them spoke and then it dropped.

She had not called back.

* * *

Amara came down at seven forty with the elephant.

The elephant was a small fabric animal, perhaps fifteen centimeters long, made from a printed cotton in shades of grey with embroidered eyes that had slightly different expressions, which Amara had noticed and periodically commented on. Its name was Elephant, which was what you got when you asked a four-year-old to name things and then let two years pass without revision.

Amara was six now and she still slept with it. Gloria had no particular opinion about this. The child psychologist she had consulted once about something unrelated had mentioned that comfort objects in children this age were developmentally appropriate and Gloria had filed this without needing it confirmed. Amara knew what she needed. She had always known. It was one of her most consistent qualities — a kind of self-knowledge that other people, including adults, often lacked and that Amara exercised without drama or announcement.

She came into the kitchen and put the elephant on the table beside her placemat and sat down and looked at Marcus’s drawing.

‘The legs are wrong,’ she said.

‘I know,’ Marcus said.

She poured herself juice with the careful attention she gave to pouring things and drank it and looked at the elephant. Then she looked at Gloria.

‘Is Joseph coming today.’

‘Tomorrow morning,’ Gloria said. ‘Early. So tonight we pack.’

Amara nodded. She picked up the elephant and looked at it for a moment — the look she gave it when she was thinking about something else and using the elephant as a focusing point. Then she put it down and began to eat her breakfast with the ceremony she brought to all mornings.

‘Daddy isn’t coming,’ she said. Not a question.

‘Not this time.’

‘Is he safe.’

Gloria looked at her daughter. Six years old and the question was entirely matter-of-fact, asked in the same tone as questions about the hippos and the packing, the question of a child who had been living with a father who went to difficult places long enough that the question had become part of the ordinary vocabulary of their days.

‘Yes,’ Gloria said. ‘He’s safe.’

Amara nodded and returned to her breakfast.

Marcus had been watching this exchange over the top of his notebook. He looked at his mother for a moment with the expression he sometimes had — the expression of a boy who understood more than he was saying and had decided, for reasons of his own, not to say it.

Then he went back to the legs of the hippo.

* * *

The day moved through its ordinary sequence.

School drop-off at seven fifty-five — the gate of St. Andrew’s Primary where the security guard Nelson knew both children by name and where Marcus’s teacher Mrs. Acan had told Gloria at the last conference that Marcus was the most relentlessly curious child she had encountered in twenty-two years of teaching, which she had said as a statement of fact rather than either praise or complaint. Amara’s teacher said that Amara listened better than any child she had known and that this was a rarer gift than most people understood.

Gloria drove back to the compound. Changed into clinic clothes. Drove to the Jinja Regional Referral Hospital where she worked in the outpatient clinic two mornings a week as a volunteer nurse. The clinic was always full and the work was real and requiring and she had stopped noticing, mostly, the gap between what the clinic had and what it needed because the gap was a permanent condition and not something that individual attention resolved. What individual attention resolved was the patient in front of her at this particular moment and that was the practice she had trained herself into.

She was good at it. She had been good at nursing before she stopped doing it professionally and she was good at it now in the clinic in Jinja and the goodness of it — the competence, the usefulness, the days that ended with something accomplished — was part of what had made Jinja feel like home in a way that places where she had not been useful had not.

At noon she left the clinic and drove to the market. Saturday market was the big one but Wednesday’s was sufficient for what she needed and she knew the vendors now, knew which one had the best tomatoes and which one’s mangoes ran small but sweet and which one she had learned to avoid because his scale was unreliable in a direction that consistently favored him. She bought what the week needed and put it in the back of the Land Rover and drove home.

Lunch alone at the kitchen table. Tea. The compound quiet in the midday heat.

She thought about what to pack.

* * *

She had been to Murchison Falls once before, years ago, before Marcus, the two of them taking a long weekend that Jackson had arranged with the particular focused efficiency he brought to planning things he wanted to happen. They had stayed in a banda at the top of the falls — a simple structure, a bed and a mosquito net and the sound of the falls constant through the night, so constant that by the second night she had stopped hearing it separately and it had become part of the silence.

They had taken a boat to the base of the falls. The Nile below the falls was a different river from the Nile above it — wider, calmer, the violence of the falls behind you and the water spreading into something that felt like relief. Hippos in the shallows. Crocodiles on the banks that the guide pointed out one by one with the patience of a man who had pointed them out ten thousand times and had not yet stopped finding them worth pointing at. Jackson standing at the boat’s rail with his arms crossed, watching the bank, saying nothing, and Gloria watching him watch things and thinking that this was one of the things she loved about him — the capacity for complete attention without narration. He didn’t describe what he saw. He just saw it.

This trip would be different. The children changed everything about how you moved through a place. The pace, the tolerance for discomfort, the radius of acceptable risk. She had accepted all of it and found that she did not miss the other version. The version with children was noisier and more demanding and occasionally exhausting and it was also more alive. Marcus at a game park would see things she would not see alone. His attention was different from hers, organized by a different set of priorities, and following it had shown her things she would have missed.

Amara was different again. She would find one thing and stay with it. A single bird for twenty minutes. A termite mound. The specific texture of the grass at the edge of the path. You took a walk with Amara and came back knowing one thing very well.

Gloria was looking forward to it.

And underneath the looking forward, steady and familiar, was the shape of what was missing. Jackson should be here for this. He had booked it himself. He had shown Marcus the map. He had said he was sorry over a bad phone line from eastern Congo and she had said she knew and she did know and it was still the shape of something absent.

She thought about Victoria Falls.

Six months ago, Jackson home for three weeks between a deployment that had ended and the one that hadn’t started yet, the four of them in a vehicle with Joseph driving south and west for two days. The cabin at the falls — a proper structure, stone floors, a veranda that looked toward the mist that hung over the gorge day and night. The sound of the falls constant and immense, a sound so large it stopped being noise and became weather.

The walk to the bridge.

She remembered the bridge — a footbridge over the gorge, the falls to the left, the mist coming up from below in great slow billows that soaked through your clothes within thirty seconds of reaching the midpoint. Jackson had lifted Amara to the railing so she could see over it. The gorge below — the Zambezi going through it green and fast, the walls of the gorge red and black and dripping. Amara had looked at it with the concentrated attention she brought to remarkable things and then said, with complete certainty: it’s a very big water.

Jackson had laughed. The full laugh, the one that didn’t come out often but when it did it changed the quality of the air around him.

It is, he had said. It’s a very big water.

And then the path behind the falls.

The guide had taken them along a narrow ledge carved into the face of the cliff, the rock wall on one side and the falls on the other, the falls so close you could put your hand into them, the noise beyond noise, the spray so thick it was indistinguishable from rain. Marcus in front, serious with responsibility, gripping his section of the guide rope with both hands. Amara on Jackson’s back with her face pressed into his neck. Gloria behind them both, watching Jackson’s back, his shoulders, the way he moved through the spray with Amara’s small arms around his throat.

She had thought at that moment: this. Whatever comes before and after, this is the thing.

She thought about it now at the kitchen table in the Jinja midday heat with the compound quiet around her and Jackson somewhere in eastern Congo where the signal was uncertain.

This is the thing.

* * *

Joseph arrived at the compound gate at six the following morning.

Gloria heard the vehicle before Emmanuel called up to the house — the particular sound of Joseph’s Land Cruiser, which had a quality in its engine that she had learned to recognize the way you learned the sound of anyone who came regularly to your house. She was already dressed. The bags were stacked at the bottom of the stairs. She had been up since five and the children since five-thirty, which had required only the information that Joseph was coming today to produce in Marcus an immediate and complete wakefulness and in Amara a somewhat more gradual transition to readiness.

She heard the gate open and then the vehicle in the courtyard and then Marcus was past her and down the stairs and out the door at a pace that suggested he had been restraining himself for some time.

Joseph was getting out of the vehicle when Marcus arrived in the courtyard.

He was perhaps forty-five, lean, with the economical movements of a man for whom physical efficiency had been a survival requirement and had become permanent habit. His face was the face of someone who had seen serious things and had arrived, through some process that Gloria didn’t know the details of, at a quality of settled equanimity that most people didn’t reach. Jackson trusted him completely, which was not a standard Jackson applied loosely, and Gloria had learned over three years of Joseph driving them to trust her own read of him, which was the same as Jackson’s.

‘Marcus,’ Joseph said, with the tone of a man who was genuinely glad to see this particular boy.

‘Joseph.’ Marcus had the notebook. ‘I have a question about the hippos.’

‘Of course you do,’ Joseph said. ‘Put your bag in the back first.’

Marcus got his bag and put it in the back with the focused efficiency of someone who understood that the question had to wait for the task, and then he was back beside Joseph with the notebook open.

Amara came across the courtyard with the elephant under her arm and stood in front of Joseph and looked up at him.

‘Good morning, Amara,’ Joseph said, with the gravity he always used with her, which was the right register. Amara responded to being taken seriously.

‘Good morning, Joseph,’ she said. ‘Are the roads good today.’

This was a question she had heard Gloria ask and had adopted. Joseph considered it with appropriate weight.

‘The roads are acceptable,’ he said. ‘The road past Karuma has some construction. We’ll go around.’

Amara nodded as though she had needed to know this and now did. She took her bag to the vehicle.

Gloria came out with the last bag and Joseph took it from her without being asked.

‘How is he,’ Joseph said, loading the bag. He didn’t say Jackson’s name. He never did in front of the children in this context.

‘Extended,’ Gloria said. ‘He said to take good care of us.’

‘That I will do,’ Joseph said. He closed the rear door and looked at her with the directness that was one of his consistent qualities. ‘We will have a good trip and we will bring back many photographs and he will be sorry he missed it.’

Gloria smiled. ‘Yes,’ she said. ‘That’s exactly right.’

She went around to the passenger side. Through the courtyard Emmanuel was watching from the gate with the expression of a man who had been told the family was going and who took that information as an adjustment to his responsibilities. Gloria caught his eye and nodded and he nodded back.

She got in.

Joseph started the engine.

Marcus opened the notebook.

Amara put the elephant in her lap and looked out the window as the compound gate opened and Nile Crescent appeared in the morning light and Joseph pulled out and turned north and the drive to Murchison Falls began.

* * *

The road north out of Jinja ran through the town center and then along the edge of the lake for a stretch where you could see the water between the buildings, bright in the morning, before the road turned inland and the lake disappeared behind the density of the town’s outskirts. Gloria watched it go. She always did. The lake from a moving vehicle was a different thing from the lake at the end of Nile Crescent — glimpsed, intermittent, the quality of a thing seen in passing that you couldn’t quite hold.

Marcus had been talking to Joseph since the compound gate and showed no signs of stopping. Joseph answered each question with the patience of a man who either genuinely didn’t mind or had learned to produce the appearance of not minding so completely that it had become the same thing. Hippos, elephants, whether crocodiles actually slept. Joseph had grown up in the north and had opinions on all of these.

The first roadcheck came forty minutes out of Jinja.

Gloria had seen hundreds of them. The police post at the side of the road, the officer stepping out, the vehicle slowing and stopping, the brief transactional exchange through the window. She watched Joseph handle it the way she always watched — his window down before the officer reached the vehicle, his papers already out but held loosely, his manner the particular combination of relaxed and respectful that she had observed produced the smoothest outcomes. He spoke in a mixture of Luganda and English, something about the children going to the park for a school trip, and the officer looked into the back seat where Marcus was watching him with frank curiosity and Amara was watching out the other window, and the officer nodded and waved them through.

‘School trip,’ Marcus said when they were moving again.

‘Easier,’ Joseph said.

‘We’re not a school trip.’

‘No. But your mother is a teacher.’

‘I’m a nurse,’ Gloria said.

‘You teach things at the clinic,’ Joseph said. ‘I have heard you explain things to patients.’

Marcus found this satisfying and wrote something in the notebook.

The road moved north through country that changed around them as Jinja fell behind. The density of the town giving way to the denser green of the rural interior, red earth roads going off to either side, roadside commerce at the villages — women with goods arranged on cloth, men at motorcycle taxis, children at the edge of everything watching the vehicles pass. The light was full now, the morning well established, the kind of East African morning that was warm without yet being hot and that she had learned to take seriously as a gift.

An hour out, north of Kampala’s outer reach, the landscape began to open. The hills of the south and center flattening toward something wider, the sky larger, the quality of the light changing to the longer, flatter light of the north. Gloria had not been to this part of Uganda before their first Murchison trip and she had not expected to find it beautiful. She had expected it to feel emptier than the south, which it did, and she had not expected that the emptiness would have the quality it had — not absence but space. Room for things to be what they were.

She looked out at it.

Marcus had finished with the hippos and moved on to a series of questions about the park’s geography that Joseph was answering with increasing specificity. Amara had fallen asleep against the door, the elephant in her lap, her face turned toward the window and the passing country.

Gloria watched her sleep.

She thought about Jackson watching Amara from the boat rail on the Murchison trip years ago, the two of them before Marcus, the falls somewhere behind them and the hippos in the shallows and Jackson watching his daughter in the particular way he had of watching his children — as though he was memorizing them, storing them against the times when he would not be there to see them.

He was not here now.

She looked at the country going north.

* * *

She had not planned to ask Joseph about his childhood. It came out of a longer conversation that began with Marcus asking him whether he had been to Murchison Falls before and Joseph saying yes, many times, and Marcus asking whether he had grown up near there and Joseph saying not far, in the north, Gulu area, and Marcus asking what it had been like to grow up in the north.

Joseph was quiet for a moment. Gloria watched his hands on the wheel.

‘When I was young,’ Joseph said, ‘it was not an easy time in the north.’

‘Why not.’

‘There were men doing bad things. Taking people from their homes. Burning villages. The people of the north had to leave their homes and go to camps where they would be safe.’

Marcus was listening with the complete attention he gave to things he recognized as important. ‘Did your family go to a camp.’

‘Yes.’

‘Did you go with them.’

Joseph’s hands moved slightly on the wheel. ‘Not at first.’

He was quiet for a moment and Gloria thought he was going to leave it there, which would have been his right, and she would not have pushed. But Joseph continued.

‘The men who were doing bad things — they would take children,’ he said. ‘They would make the children fight for them. I was taken when I was twelve.’

The vehicle was very quiet except for the road and the engine.

‘You were twelve,’ Marcus said. His voice had changed. The questions had stopped. He was just saying back what he’d heard, the way you did when something needed to be said again to be real.

‘Yes.’

‘What happened.’

‘I was with them for almost two years,’ Joseph said. ‘I did things I do not want to tell you about because you are eight years old and you don’t need to know them yet. But one time there was a big fight. Government soldiers came. And in the fight almost everyone was killed or ran away. Only me and my friend Emmanuel were left. We hid in the bush for a day and a night. And when it was quiet we went and we found the government soldiers and we gave them our weapons and we said we didn’t want to fight anymore.’

Marcus was very still. Gloria could see him in the mirror, the notebook closed in his lap, his face doing the work of someone processing something that mattered.

‘Were they angry at you,’ Marcus said.

‘They understood,’ Joseph said. ‘The government knew that the children had not chosen to fight. They gave us a choice. We could join the army if we were old enough. We could go to school. Or they would help us find our families.’

‘What did you choose.’

‘I chose to find my family first,’ Joseph said. ‘And then school.’ A pause. ‘Emmanuel chose the army.’

‘Did you find them,’ Marcus said. ‘Your family.’

The pause was brief but Gloria felt it.

‘Some of them,’ Joseph said.

Marcus absorbed this. Gloria watched him absorb it in the mirror — the way he sat with it, didn’t ask the follow-on question, understood that some of them was the complete answer and that asking which ones was not the right question.

She felt something for her son in that moment that she didn’t have immediate words for. The recognition of a quality in him that was his own, not taught, not inherited, just present. He knew when to stop.

‘I’m glad you found them,’ Marcus said. ‘The ones you found.’

‘Yes,’ Joseph said. ‘I am also.’

The road went north. Amara was still asleep, the elephant in her lap, the country moving past her window. Joseph drove and said nothing more about it and Marcus opened his notebook after a while and found a clean page and wrote something in it that Gloria couldn’t see.

She looked at the back of Joseph’s head. The steadiness of him. The way he had told that story to an eight-year-old boy on a road in Uganda with exactly the right measure of everything — not more than Marcus could hold, not less than the truth required.

She understood again why Jackson trusted him.

She understood why she did.

* * *

The park gate appeared in the late morning.

Marcus saw it before anyone else, which was expected. He made a sound that was not quite a word — the sound of someone for whom a long-anticipated thing has finally become visible and the gap between anticipation and reality is closing fast. Amara woke at the sound, looked out the window, looked at the gate, looked at the elephant in her lap.

Joseph slowed for the gate and the ranger check. Papers, park fees, the formality of entry that Joseph handled in the same way he handled roadchecks — unhurried, prepared, producing what was needed before it was asked for. The ranger looked into the back seat. Marcus was looking at the ranger with the expression of someone with questions that he was, for once, holding back out of understanding that this was not the moment.

The gate opened.

The park road was different from the road they had been on — unpaved, red earth, the vegetation on both sides thicker and less managed, the sense of entering something that had different rules from what was behind the gate. Joseph drove it at the pace the road required, steady and unhurried, and through the windshield the park opened ahead of them.

A family of warthogs crossed the road fifty meters ahead. The two adults first, then four small ones, all of them running with their tails straight up like antennae.

Marcus said: ‘Warthogs.’ In a voice entirely different from his regular voice. Quieter. Specific. He wrote in the notebook without looking down, his eyes on the warthogs until they were gone into the grass.

Amara was watching out her window at the place where the warthogs had disappeared. Then she looked at the elephant and back at the window, as though comparing them. The elephant seemed to pass whatever assessment she was conducting.

Gloria looked at her children and then at the park going past and thought: Jackson should be here. And then: he will hear about the warthogs tonight. In exact detail. From Marcus, with illustrations. And it won’t be the same as being here and it will still be good.

She took out her phone and took a photograph of the road ahead, the red earth and the grass and the space where the warthogs had been.

She sent it to Jackson with no message. The image said everything a message would have said and more.

The vehicle moved through the park toward the lodge and the five days ahead and the morning of the game drive that was still three days away and that none of them were thinking about yet, because the warthogs had been real and close and there were five days of this and everything was still entirely as it was supposed to be.

Chapter II
Five Days

The lodge sat on a rise above the north bank of the Victoria Nile, close enough to the falls that on still nights you could hear them, a low constant presence underneath the other sounds of the park. The main building was stone and timber, broad-roofed, open on the river side to catch whatever moved off the water in the evenings. Behind it the guest rooms were arranged in a curved line facing east, each one a separate structure with a covered veranda and a view of the park’s interior, the grass and the scrub and the distant tree line where the savannah began.

Gloria stood on the veranda of their room while Joseph unloaded the bags and took in the park around her.

It was different from what she remembered. She had been here once before, years ago, before the children, and the memory had been accurate in its details but wrong in its atmosphere — she had remembered a quiet place, and it was quiet, but the quiet was not the absence of things. It was the presence of things that made no noise for you. The grass moving without wind. The bird she couldn’t identify in the acacia thirty meters from the veranda, the one that was the green of something that had decided green was the correct response to this environment. The quality of the air, which was different from Jinja’s lake air — drier, with something in it that she associated now, having been here before, with the smell of the park itself. Grass and distance and animal and something older than either.

Marcus came out of the room behind her and stood at the veranda rail and looked at the park with the expression of someone taking formal possession.

‘I can see a lot from here,’ he said.

‘Yes.’

‘Robert said the game drive goes north of the lodge. Into the acacia section.’

‘You’ve already talked to Robert.’

‘He was at the reception when we came in. He told me about the northern section.’ Marcus had the notebook. ‘He said the lions prefer the northern section in the morning because of the shade.’

‘You asked him about the lions immediately.’

‘I asked him about everything,’ Marcus said. ‘The lions came up because I asked about the northern section and he brought them up. I didn’t lead with lions.’

Gloria looked at the park.

‘Where’s your sister.’

‘She found a chair,’ Marcus said. ‘Inside. She’s sitting in it.’

This was accurate. Amara had identified a wooden chair near the window within thirty seconds of entering the room and had established herself in it with the elephant on her knee and the expression of someone who had found what they were looking for and saw no reason to look further. She would emerge when she was ready. She always did.

Joseph appeared at the veranda steps.

‘Bags are in,’ he said. ‘I’m going to the staff area to get something to eat before I head north.’ He looked at Gloria. ‘I have a number for the lodge. I’ll check in tonight.’

‘You don’t have to check in,’ Gloria said.

‘I know I don’t have to.’ He said it with the particular quality he had, the one that acknowledged what she’d said and proceeded anyway. ‘I’ll check in tonight.’

She looked at him. The steadiness of him in the afternoon light, the park behind him, the Land Cruiser visible through the trees in the lodge parking area.

‘Thank you, Joseph,’ she said.

He nodded once. Then he looked at Marcus. ‘Monday at ten,’ he said. ‘I’ll be at the gate.’

‘Monday at ten,’ Marcus confirmed. He had his hand out. Joseph shook it with the seriousness the gesture required.

Then he walked back toward the main building and the staff area and a moment later the Land Cruiser started in the parking area and moved toward the park gate and turned north toward Gulu.

Gloria watched it go.

Marcus was already at the notebook.

* * *

The afternoon game drive left at three thirty.

The vehicle was an open-sided Land Cruiser with bench seats and a roof rack and a small cooler bolted to the rear that the driver — Pius, who introduced himself and started the engine without ceremony — had stocked with water and soft drinks. Robert was in the front passenger seat with a radio and a pair of binoculars and the air of a man who had done this many times and had not let the repetition make it routine.

The other guests were three Germans — a couple named Dieter and Hanna who appeared to be in their fifties and had the organized quality of experienced travelers, and a younger man named Klaus who was traveling alone and who had a camera with a lens that Marcus regarded with frank admiration. They were all polite and uncomplicated and had the good sense to let Robert manage the vehicle’s communication dynamics rather than attempting to establish their own.

Marcus installed himself directly behind Robert.

The vehicle left the lodge compound and turned onto the park track and the afternoon game drive began.

* * *

Robert’s knowledge was the kind that had been built from years of close observation of a specific place and that produced, in practice, an ability to see the park that the visitors could not replicate regardless of effort. He saw things before they were visible to anyone else in the vehicle — not because his eyes were better but because his attention was organized differently. He knew what to look for and where to look and what the landscape looked like when something was in it that hadn’t been there before.

An elephant herd forty meters off the track, partially screened by acacia, that Gloria had been looking directly at for thirty seconds before Robert pointed and she suddenly saw them. Not one elephant — eleven, including two young ones staying close to their mothers with the caution of animals that have learned that the world contains things larger than themselves.

Pius stopped the vehicle and cut the engine and for several minutes the only sound was the park and the occasional shift of the vehicle’s frame as the engine cooled.

The elephants moved through the acacia with a deliberateness that was not slowness — they covered ground efficiently, the herd maintaining its internal geometry, the young ones held in place by the gravitational field of the adults around them. One of the larger females stopped broadside to the vehicle and looked at them with the quality of attention that large animals have, the kind that makes you feel assessed rather than observed.

Gloria held her phone up and took photographs she knew would not fully capture it. The photograph would show elephants. What it wouldn’t show was the way the air had changed when Pius cut the engine, the stillness of a vehicle full of people who had simultaneously decided to stop being tourists and start paying attention.

Marcus had his notebook open. He was not drawing. He was watching the elephants with a focus that Gloria recognized as his deepest register of attention — the one that produced, hours later, accounts of things he had observed that turned out to be accurate in details she hadn’t noticed herself.

Amara was leaning against Gloria’s arm. She had the elephant in her lap. She was watching the herd with the particular stillness she brought to single things that mattered to her and Gloria did not speak or move so as not to disturb whatever was happening between her daughter and the elephants across the forty meters of park grass.

The herd moved on. Pius started the engine.

Marcus began to write.

* * *

They found a lion at five o’clock.

Robert had been on his radio for the previous twenty minutes, in contact with another guide vehicle working the northern track, and had directed Pius along a route that brought them to a long flat section of grassland where the track ran straight for two hundred meters and where, at the far end, a male lion was lying in the last of the direct afternoon sun with the comprehensive relaxation of an animal that had eaten recently and had no immediate concerns.

The lion was perhaps eight years old, Robert said, with a mane that was dark at the edges and lighter at the center and that gave him a slightly unfinished appearance that Robert said was normal for his age. He was aware of the vehicle. He demonstrated this by opening one eye when Pius brought them to a stop forty meters away and then closing it again, which Robert described as a positive sign.

Marcus had a series of questions about the mane that Robert answered in order.

The lion did not move for fifteen minutes. He breathed. His ribs expanded and contracted with the slow rhythm of a large animal at complete rest and his tail moved occasionally at the tip in a way that seemed autonomous, as though a separate and less relaxed creature lived at the end of it.

Klaus was photographing. Dieter and Hanna were watching through binoculars. Gloria watched without either, the lion forty meters away in the afternoon light, and felt the thing she had felt on the first Murchison trip years ago — the sensation of being in a place that was not organized around human presence, that would be exactly as it was if the vehicle and everyone in it simply wasn’t there. The lion would be here in this grass in this light whether or not anyone had driven four hours to see him. That fact, which should have been unremarkable, felt clarifying in a way she didn’t try to analyze.

She thought about Jackson.

She took out her phone and took a photograph of the lion and sent it to him with no message.

He would see it when he could see it. Wherever he was in eastern Congo, whenever the signal allowed. He would look at the lion in the afternoon grass and know where she was and that everything was fine.

* * *

Dinner was on the open veranda above the river.

The lodge set a long table for all the guests and the evening meal was communal, which suited Marcus entirely and required some adjustment from Amara, who preferred the known quantities of the family table to the open variable of strangers. She managed it by sitting between Gloria and Marcus and addressing most of her conversation to Robert, who had joined the guests for dinner and whom she had decided was a known quantity by the end of the afternoon drive.

Dieter and Hanna were from Hamburg, retired, and had been to East Africa seven times. They had the quality of people whose shared life had produced a private shorthand — they communicated in half-sentences and small gestures and the completions of each other’s thoughts that looked, from outside, like the efficiency of two people who had been paying close attention to each other for a long time. Gloria found them comfortable in the way of people who are not performing anything.

Klaus was a wildlife photographer, twenty-eight, in Uganda for three weeks on an assignment for a German nature magazine. He was quiet in the manner of someone whose professional life was organized around observation rather than conversation. When he spoke it was specific and direct and he listened to responses with the focused attention of someone who had trained themselves to see what was actually there rather than what they expected.

He showed Marcus some of the afternoon’s photographs on the camera’s screen. The elephants first — the large female broadside, looking at the vehicle, and the quality of her attention visible even in the photograph, the sense of assessment that Gloria had felt in the field. Marcus looked at it for a long time.

‘You can see what she’s thinking,’ Marcus said.

Klaus looked at the image. ‘A little.’

‘She’s deciding if we’re dangerous.’

‘Yes. And what did she decide?’

Marcus thought about it. ‘Not dangerous enough to worry about.’

Klaus almost smiled. ‘That’s probably right.’

Robert told them about the lion walk, which was conducted by the conservation area adjacent to the main park. Early morning, before the heat, through habitat where the resident lions had been habituated to human presence over years of careful management. Gloria watched Marcus receive this information and saw the expression that meant he had added something to a list he was maintaining internally. She had learned not to underestimate that list.

‘How early,’ he said.

‘Six o’clock,’ Robert said.

Marcus looked at Gloria.

‘We’ll discuss it,’ she said.

‘The cubs are also available to meet,’ Robert said. ‘In the afternoon. The younger ones — they have three females from last season and two males from the season before. Old enough to be mobile but still young enough to be—’ he searched for the word.

‘Soft,’ Amara said.

Robert looked at her. ‘Yes,’ he said, with the gravity he had apparently decided this child required. ‘Exactly right. Still soft.’

Amara picked up the elephant from the table and held it in her lap and said nothing further, apparently satisfied.

The river was dark below the veranda. Somewhere out in it something surfaced and went back under. Robert said hippopotamus without looking, identifying it from the sound alone, and Dieter and Hanna both looked at the water and Klaus had his camera up and Marcus wrote something in the notebook.

Gloria watched the darkness where the hippopotamus had been and felt the evening settle around her — the other guests, the river, the warm air, the park out there in the dark being what it was regardless of their presence.

She thought: Jackson should be here for this dinner.

And then: I’ll tell him about it tonight. Every detail. He’ll want the details.

* * *

She called him at nine, after the children were in bed.

Marcus had lasted until eight forty-five, which was later than his usual time but which the evening had earned. He had gone without negotiation, which was itself a measure of the day’s weight — the drive, the elephants, the lion, the dinner, Robert’s account of the lion walk and the cubs all accumulating into something that his body had decided required sleep before his mind was ready to agree. He was asleep within minutes, the notebook on the pillow beside him, open to the page where he’d been working on the lion sketch.

Amara had been asleep since eight. The elephant was under her chin.

Gloria sat on the veranda of their room with the park dark around her and the sound of the falls somewhere to the west, low and constant, and called Jackson.

It rang four times. Five. She was preparing for voicemail when he picked up.

‘Gloria.’ His voice with the quality it had from field locations — present but careful, the awareness of his environment audible in how he managed his volume.

‘Hi.’

‘How is it.’

‘Good,’ she said. ‘Better than good. We saw elephants this afternoon. Eleven of them, with two calves. And a lion.’

‘A lion.’

‘Robert found him in the northern section. Male, about eight years old. He was completely unconcerned about us.’

‘How did Marcus take the lion.’

‘He took it very seriously,’ Gloria said. ‘He had questions. Robert answered all of them.’

Jackson was quiet for a moment in the way that meant he was smiling. She knew his silences well enough to read them at this distance over a bad connection.

‘And the cubs,’ she said. ‘Tomorrow afternoon there are cubs to meet. Robert said they’re soft.’

‘Who said that.’

‘Amara, actually.’

The laugh came through the static clearly, the real one, the one that didn’t come out often. She sat with it for a moment.

‘How are you,’ she said. Not asking about the operation. Asking about him.

‘Getting there,’ he said. Which was what he said when the answer was complicated and the line wasn’t the place for it. ‘It’s taking longer than it should.’

‘I know.’

‘I’m sorry about the park.’

‘Stop apologizing,’ she said. ‘We’re here. We saw eleven elephants and a lion and tomorrow there are cubs and the dinner was good and the river sounds like the falls all night.’ She paused. ‘You’re missing it. That’s true. And we’ll tell you everything.’

‘I know you will.’

‘Marcus has drawings.’

‘Of course he does.’

The line shifted. The quality of it changing in the way that field connections changed — not dropping, just becoming less certain, the signal reaching her from somewhere in eastern Congo across the distance of the park and the country and everything between.

‘Jackson,’ she said.

‘I’m here.’

‘Amara put the elephant on the table at dinner.’ She didn’t know why she said this specifically. It had seemed important at the time and it seemed important now. ‘Just put it there. Like it was a guest.’

A silence. Then: ‘That’s our girl.’

‘Yes,’ Gloria said.

The line shifted again. The static increasing.

‘I’ll try to call tomorrow,’ he said. ‘After the cubs.’

‘We’ll be back by four.’

‘Four your time.’

‘Four my time.’

The static thickened.

‘I love you,’ she said.

The line dropped.

She sat with the dead phone for a moment. The park around her, dark and full of its own business. The falls somewhere to the west, their sound unchanged. A nightjar calling from the acacia by the veranda — a sound she had heard on the first visit and had looked up afterward, a bird she could now identify without looking, its call the sound of this place at night.

She didn’t call back.

She knew better than to call back when it dropped from a field location. It meant he’d moved. He would call when he could call.

She sat for a few more minutes on the veranda with the park sounds around her and the phone in her lap and thought about his laugh at Amara’s word for the cubs. The real laugh through the static from eastern Congo, the one that didn’t come out often.

Then she went inside and checked the children and went to bed.

The falls were audible through the window. She had forgotten how constant they were — not like rain, which had variation, but like the permanent condition of this piece of the earth, the river going over the edge the way it had been going over the edge for longer than anyone could account for. She lay in the dark and listened to it until the listening dissolved into sleep.

Outside the room the park moved through its night. Things hunted. Things slept. The hippopotamus Robert had identified by sound came up from the river somewhere in the darkness and moved along the bank and went back in.

The falls continued.

Monday was four days away.

Everything was as it was supposed to be.

Chapter III
The Game Drive

The guide’s name was Samuel and he had been walking with lions for eleven years.

He said this not as a boast but as a credential, the way a doctor states years of practice — information you needed to have before you decided to trust him with something that mattered. He was perhaps thirty-five, lean and unhurried, with the specific quality of physical confidence that came not from size but from complete familiarity with an environment. He stood in the conservation area’s small briefing room at five fifty in the morning and looked at the six people who had come for the walk — Gloria, Dieter and Hanna, Klaus with his camera, a South African couple who had arrived the previous evening — and told them what they needed to know.

The lions had been fed before dawn. This was standard — not to make them docile, Samuel said, because lions were not docile regardless of how recently they had eaten. To ensure they were not hungry. A lion that had eaten was a lion with no particular agenda. A lion with no particular agenda was a lion you could walk among, provided you understood the terms.

The terms were these.

You walked where Samuel walked. You stopped when Samuel stopped. You did not run under any circumstances, because running activated something in a lion that nothing else activated and once activated it did not easily stop. You carried your stick — he produced them from a rack near the door, a bundle of wooden staffs, each one a meter long, stripped smooth — not as a weapon but as an extension of yourself, a way of making yourself larger in the lion’s perception. If a lion came too close or became curious in a way that Samuel judged required a response, you raised your stick and held your ground.

‘You do not hit the lion,’ Samuel said. ‘You do not need to hit the lion. You simply become, for a moment, something larger than you were. That is sufficient.’

He looked around the room.

‘Questions before we go.’

Marcus had been standing very still since Samuel began speaking, which was unusual enough that Gloria had noted it. He was holding his stick with both hands, not fidgeting with it, his full attention on Samuel with the quality he brought to things he had decided were serious. He did not ask a question. He had asked Robert everything he needed to ask the night before and was now in the mode of someone who had done the preparation and was ready for the thing itself.

Samuel looked at him. ‘You’ve done your research,’ he said.

‘Robert told me about the walk,’ Marcus said.

‘Then you know what to expect.’

‘I know what Robert told me,’ Marcus said. ‘But Robert hasn’t been on the walk today.’

Samuel considered this. ‘True,’ he said. He picked up his own stick. ‘Let’s go.’

* * *

The conservation area was adjacent to the main park, a tract of managed habitat where the bush had been left to be what it was while the human activity around it had been carefully organized not to compromise that. At this hour, in the last of the dark before the real dawn, it had a quality that the park itself had in a different register — older, more enclosed, the trees thicker and the grass longer and the path through it worn by years of these morning walks into a line just visible enough to follow.

Samuel led. Gloria was directly behind him. Then Dieter and Hanna. Then Klaus. The South African couple at the rear.

Marcus was beside Gloria, which was where she had positioned him without making a production of it. He was eight years old and he was walking with lions before dawn and the position beside her was not negotiable regardless of how prepared he was. He had accepted this without argument, which told her he was more alert to the situation’s requirements than he usually let on.

The path went into the trees.

Samuel stopped.

He stopped without signal or announcement, simply ceased moving, and the people behind him read the cessation and ceased moving also within two seconds. The quality of the silence that followed was different from ordinary silence — it had direction to it, an orientation, the silence of people whose attention had been simultaneously organized toward the same point.

The lion was twelve meters off the path to the right.

Gloria saw her — it was a female, Samuel would say later, three years old, from the resident pride — at the same moment Marcus’s hand found her arm. Not gripping. Just finding. Contact, the kind that didn’t ask for anything except to establish that they were in the same place.

The lion was lying in the grass with her head up, looking at them. Not with alarm. With the comprehensive, unhurried attention of an animal assessing something in her environment that was present and known and not currently requiring action. Her eyes caught the early light in a way that produced a color Gloria could not have described accurately afterward — something between gold and amber, something that had no equivalent in any environment she had spent time in before this one.

She was very large.

Gloria had known intellectually that lions were large. The male from the afternoon drive had demonstrated this. But a male lion in the open grass at forty meters was a different experience from a female lion in the trees at twelve meters in the pre-dawn dark and the knowing was replaced by something more immediate and physical — the body’s own assessment, running beneath conscious thought, producing its results in the form of a heightened quality of attention that Gloria recognized as the closest thing she had felt in years to the sensation of being entirely present.

She was aware of Marcus’s hand on her arm. She was aware of her own stick, held as Samuel had described, her grip on it firm without being tight. She was aware of Samuel ahead of her, perfectly still, his body carrying the absence of alarm that she understood was itself a form of communication — not to the group but to the lion. Nothing here requires your attention. We are a known quantity. We have been here before.

The lion looked at them for a long moment.

Then she put her head back down in the grass.

Samuel began to walk.

Gloria walked.

Marcus walked beside her with his hand no longer on her arm and his stick carried correctly and his face doing something she had not seen it do before — not the excited focus of a boy who had wanted something and was getting it, but the quieter expression of someone who has arrived somewhere and found it larger than they expected and is adjusting to the size of it.

* * *

There were four lions in the first hour.

The female in the trees. A male, younger than the one from the afternoon drive, lying across a termite mound two hundred meters into the walk with the proprietary ease of an animal who had decided that this particular termite mound was his and had no plans to revise the decision. Two more females near the water source at the walk’s halfway point, drinking with the focused efficiency of animals in whom thirst, once addressed, would be completely resolved.

Samuel knew all of them. Not their names — he used reference numbers, the system of the conservation area’s monitoring program — but he knew their ages and their relationships within the pride and their individual qualities in the way that someone knows the people they have been paying close attention to for years. The male on the termite mound was bold, he said — the boldest of the pride’s young males, the one most likely to approach the walking group out of curiosity rather than staying at a distance.

He was correct.

The male got up from the termite mound when the group was fifty meters past it and walked after them with the unhurried directness of an animal for whom curiosity and confidence occupied the same space. Samuel heard him coming — turned, assessed, held up one hand to stop the group.

The lion came to twenty meters. Fifteen.

Samuel raised his stick.

The group raised their sticks.

The lion stopped. He looked at the line of raised sticks with the expression of an animal encountering a thing that was familiar but worth reassessing. He was not alarmed. He was deciding.

Gloria held her stick level, her eyes on the lion, and beside her Marcus held his stick with both hands and did not move.

The lion looked at them for a long moment. Then he turned and walked back to the termite mound with the complete dignity of an animal who had decided to return to his previous position for his own reasons.

Samuel lowered his stick. The group lowered their sticks.

Samuel looked at Marcus.

Marcus looked back.

‘You held,’ Samuel said. A statement, not a question.

‘You said to hold,’ Marcus said.

Samuel walked on. But Gloria saw the small adjustment in his posture that meant something had been noted and found satisfactory.

Marcus looked at her once. She looked back. Nothing was said. Nothing needed to be said.

They walked on into the morning.

* * *

The walk lasted two hours.

By the time Samuel led them back to the conservation area’s compound the sun was fully up and the air had lost the pre-dawn cool and the quality of the light had changed from the tentative grey-gold of early morning to the clear direct light of a Ugandan morning in full.

They stood in the compound’s small courtyard and drank the tea that someone had prepared and nobody said very much. It was the quiet of people who have shared something that doesn’t immediately require discussion. Dieter had his hand on Hanna’s arm the way he’d had it on the walk. Klaus was reviewing photographs on the camera screen with the focused privacy of someone doing internal work. The South African couple stood close together looking at nothing in particular.

Marcus had the notebook out. He was writing rather than drawing, which he did when the visual record was insufficient for what he wanted to capture. He wrote for several minutes without stopping, which was unusual — he was a precise writer who generally produced short entries and revised them rather than writing at length. Gloria did not look at what he was writing. It was his.

Samuel came out of the main building and stopped beside Marcus.

He looked at the notebook. Marcus showed him the page without being asked — the drawing he’d done of the male lion on the termite mound, accurate in its proportions, the mound’s shape correct, the lion’s posture catching something of the proprietary ease Gloria had felt from fifty meters away.

‘You drew this during the walk,’ Samuel said.

‘While we were stopped at the water source. I draw faster than most people.’

Samuel studied it. ‘This is F-seven,’ he said, pointing at the male. ‘That’s his termite mound. Has been for two years.’

Marcus wrote F-7 in small letters beside the drawing.

‘What are you going to do with this notebook,’ Samuel said.

Marcus considered the question seriously. ‘Keep it,’ he said. ‘And when I come back I’ll have a record of what I saw before so I can see what’s changed.’

‘You’re planning to come back.’

‘Yes.’ No uncertainty in it. ‘My dad hasn’t seen it yet. He was supposed to come this time but he couldn’t. So we have to come back.’

Samuel looked at Gloria over Marcus’s head.

She looked back at the park beyond the compound wall. The acacia and the grass and the quality of the light. The sounds of it — the birds, the wind in the dry grass, somewhere in the middle distance something moving that she couldn’t identify from here.

‘Yes,’ she said. ‘We’ll come back.’

* * *

Amara had been occupied during the morning walk with the lodge’s other offerings.

She had eaten breakfast with the slow ceremony of her mornings, the elephant beside her plate, and then she had found the lodge’s small library and extracted a book about East African birds that she could not read but whose photographs she examined with the systematic attention she brought to visual information. She had been sitting with it in the chair by the window when Gloria and Marcus returned, and she had looked up and assessed them both and apparently found them acceptable and returned to the bird book.

‘How was it,’ she said, without looking up.

‘Very good,’ Gloria said.

‘Were the lions soft.’

‘The lions were not soft,’ Marcus said, pulling out a chair. ‘They were lions.’

Amara considered this. ‘The cubs will be soft,’ she said. ‘Robert said.’

‘The cubs are this afternoon,’ Gloria said. ‘After lunch.’

Amara turned a page in the bird book.

* * *

The cubs were in a grass enclosure behind the conservation area’s main building, a space that was neither cage nor open habitat but something calibrated between them — enough room for five young lions to establish their own geography, bounded enough that the interaction with visitors could be managed safely. The grass was long enough to lie down in and short enough to move through, and when Gloria and the children arrived in the mid-afternoon with Klaus and the South African couple, all five cubs were visible — three sleeping in a loose pile near the enclosure’s shade structure and two moving through the grass with the investigative energy of animals for whom everything was still new enough to require examination.

The conservation guide, a young woman named Fatima, gave them the same kind of briefing Samuel had given for the morning walk but shorter and with a different emphasis. The cubs were eight months and fourteen months, she said. Old enough to be mobile and strong and occasionally willful. Young enough that their relationship with humans had been shaped by years of careful habituation and that their instinct toward people was curiosity rather than either fear or aggression. You could sit in the grass and they would come to you. You could pet them if they allowed it. You did not pick them up. You did not put your face close to their faces. If one became too energetic the guide would manage it.

She opened the enclosure gate.

Marcus went in first with the focused intention of someone who had been waiting for this since Robert mentioned it at dinner. He walked to the center of the enclosure and sat down cross-legged in the grass with the immediate practicality of a boy who understood that the correct posture was at their level and not above it. He put his hands on his knees and waited.

One of the moving cubs stopped. Looked at Marcus. Looked at the others in the group, still at the gate. Looked back at Marcus, who was sitting very still and not looking directly at the cub but slightly to the side in the way Fatima had demonstrated.

The cub walked over and sniffed Marcus’s knee.

Then it climbed onto his back.

The weight of it — eight months old, already substantial, the paws finding purchase on Marcus’s shoulders — pushed him forward slightly and he put his hands out and held his position and the cub settled with its chin on the top of Marcus’s head and regarded the enclosure from this elevated position with considerable satisfaction.

Marcus was laughing. Not the performative laugh of a child doing something for the reaction of the adults watching but the real one, surprised out of him by the weight and the warmth and the absurdity and the realness of a lion on his back in a grass enclosure in Uganda on a Tuesday afternoon.

Gloria took a photograph.

Then she looked at Amara.

Amara had come through the gate and stopped two meters inside it. She had the elephant in the crook of her arm. She was looking at the three cubs sleeping in the pile by the shade structure with the particular quality of attention she brought to single things that mattered.

She walked to them slowly. Not cautiously — Amara was not a cautious child in the ways that timidity produced caution. She walked slowly in the way that she did most things deliberately, at her own pace, knowing where she was going.

She sat down in the grass beside the sleeping cubs.

The nearest one opened its eyes. It looked at Amara. It looked at the elephant in her arm. It closed its eyes again.

Amara put the elephant on the grass in front of her. She looked at the cub. Then she put her hand, very gently, on the cub’s side.

The cub breathed. Amara’s hand rose and fell with it.

She sat like that for a long time, her hand on the cub’s side, the elephant on the grass between them, her face doing the thing it did when she was entirely somewhere and not partly somewhere else.

Gloria stood at the enclosure gate and watched her daughter and the cub and the elephant in the grass and took a photograph.

Then she stood there without taking more photographs because the photograph would keep and the moment wouldn’t and she chose the moment.

The afternoon light was going flat and gold across the conservation area. Marcus was still laughing somewhere behind her, the cub on his back apparently having decided this was an acceptable situation. Klaus’s camera was working. Dieter and Hanna were in the enclosure together with one of the mobile cubs investigating Hanna’s shoe with apparent seriousness.

Amara did not move.

The cub slept under her hand.

Gloria watched her daughter and thought: Jackson needs to see this. Not the photograph. This. Amara in the world when the world gives her something that matches the size of her attention.

She took out her phone and looked at the photograph she’d taken. Amara’s profile, the cub’s flank, the elephant in the grass between them, the afternoon light coming across all three at the same angle.

She sent it to Jackson.

No message. The photograph said it.

* * *

That evening Jackson called before she did.

She was on the veranda with the last of the light when the phone moved in her hand. She picked up before the second ring.

‘The photograph,’ he said. His voice different from the night before — still careful, still the quality of a field location, but something underneath it that she recognized as the feeling he had when one of the children did something that reached him across whatever distance he was at.

‘Yes,’ she said.

‘She’s not afraid of anything.’

‘No.’

‘That’s —’ He stopped. Started differently. ‘Tell me about Marcus.’

She told him. The lion on the termite mound. The sticks raised. Marcus holding his ground with both hands on the stick and not moving. Samuel’s assessment afterward. Marcus’s answer — you said to hold — delivered with complete simplicity, as though no other response had been available.

Jackson was quiet for a moment.

‘He’s eight years old,’ he said. Not in disbelief. In the tone of a man registering something about his son that he was adding to his permanent understanding of who his son was.

‘He’s your son,’ Gloria said.

The line held for a moment, clear and close, better than the night before.

‘I should be there,’ Jackson said.

‘You should,’ she said. Not to wound him. Because it was true and she had decided years ago that the truth between them was worth more than the comfort of softer versions of it. ‘And you’ll be sorry you missed it and they’ll tell you everything and it won’t be the same as being here. That’s just how it is.’

A pause.

‘How much longer,’ she said.

‘I don’t know yet. A few more days. Maybe less.’

‘We leave Monday morning.’

‘I know. I’m trying.’

‘I know you are.’

The falls were audible from the veranda. She could see the first stars over the park to the east, the sky darkening from the deep blue of the last light toward the black that out here, away from town, was a genuine black that the city never produced.

‘Two more days,’ she said. ‘Full game drive tomorrow. We haven’t been on the river yet.’

‘The hippos.’

‘Marcus has been building toward the hippos since Jinja,’ Gloria said. ‘Joseph told him they were louder than expected.’

‘Joseph would know.’

‘He knows everything about the north.’ She paused. ‘Jackson.’

‘Yes.’

‘Come home soon.’ Not urgency. Just the truth of it, stated simply.

‘I’m working on it,’ he said.

The line held a moment longer. Then he said: ‘Tell Amara the cub was lucky.’

‘I’ll tell her.’

‘Good night, Gloria.’

‘Good night.’

The line ended cleanly this time — not dropped, finished. She sat with the phone in her lap and the park around her and the falls in the background of everything and thought about two more days and the drive home with Joseph and Nile Crescent and the mango tree Jackson had planted before he left.

She would tell Amara in the morning. The cub was lucky.

She went inside to check the children.

Marcus had the notebook on the pillow again. She turned off his light and stood in the doorway for a moment looking at him sleep. Eight years old. Both hands on the stick. Not moving.

She closed the door.

In the other bed Amara slept with the elephant under her chin, her breathing even and slow, the room quiet around her.

Tomorrow: the river. The hippos. Two more days of this and then Joseph at the gate at ten and the road back to Jinja and the ordinary world.

Everything still as it was supposed to be.

She went to bed and slept without difficulty and the falls were there all night, the way they always were, the river going over the edge the way it had always gone.

Chapter IV
The Security Detail

The first thing Dutch did every morning at the school was walk the fence line.

Not because the fence line was the most likely point of concern — in three years of doing this work he had assessed the school’s vulnerability profile thoroughly enough to know that the fence was not where a serious problem would originate. He walked it because walking it was how the morning began, the physical act of it establishing the day’s first fact: the perimeter was known. Everything inside it was his responsibility. He had looked at every meter of it with his own eyes this morning and he knew its condition and he knew what had changed since yesterday and what hadn’t.

The east gate lock was still the same.

He had submitted four requests about the east gate lock. The mechanism worked — the gate locked, the lock held, the security function was not compromised. What was compromised was the speed of operation under stress, the lock requiring a particular sequence of handle pressure and key turn that took three seconds under normal conditions and longer under anything other than normal conditions. Three seconds was three seconds. Dutch had learned across thirty years of professional life that three seconds was not an abstraction.

He noted it in the morning log and continued the fence line.

The north corner, where the live oak overhung the fence from the neighbor’s yard. He had addressed this with the school’s administration twice. The tree was on private property. The branch extended over the fence at a height that represented a theoretical access point. The administration had written to the neighbor. The neighbor had been polite and non-committal. The branch remained.

Dutch looked at the branch in the early morning light and added it to the mental list he maintained of the things he was managing versus the things he had resolved, the distinction between those two categories being one he kept clear.

He completed the fence line and went to his office and made coffee and opened the morning log and began.

* * *

The school was a private Christian school in Indian River County, kindergarten through twelfth grade, four hundred students at current enrollment. It occupied a campus of twelve acres that had been expanded twice in the previous decade as the enrollment grew and that now had the particular architectural quality of an institution that had been added to rather than planned — the original buildings in one register, the additions in another, the whole connected by covered walkways that were useful in the Florida summer and that Dutch had mapped for sightlines in his first week.

He had taken the position three years ago after his last contract work concluded and after he and Linda had spent two months in a conversation that was not quite a negotiation and not quite a decision and was somewhere between those things — a sustained examination of what the next part of their lives was going to look like and what it was going to require of both of them.

Linda had been clear about one thing. She was not going to spend the next ten years the way she had spent the previous thirty — holding the household together against an operational calendar that she had no input into and that took her husband to places she couldn’t name for durations she couldn’t predict. She had done that. She had done it without complaint and with the steadiness that he had relied on more than he had ever adequately acknowledged. She was not going to do it anymore.

Dutch had been clear about one thing. He was not a man who sat still. He had tried it for three weeks after the last contract ended and the sitting still had produced in him a quality of restlessness that was not comfortable to live with and that Linda had observed without comment, which was its own form of comment.

The school had been Linda’s suggestion. A friend from their church had mentioned the position. Security director, full campus, direct report to the head of school. Dutch had gone to meet the head of school expecting to feel nothing in particular about it and had left the meeting having accepted the position, which surprised him and did not surprise Linda.

‘You walked the campus before you came in,’ she said that evening.

‘I was early.’

‘You were twenty minutes early and you walked the whole fence line.’

He had looked at her.

‘You were already assessing it,’ she said. ‘Before you decided anything.’

This was true. He hadn’t noticed it happening, which was itself information.

‘It’s a good campus,’ he said.

‘I know,’ Linda said. ‘That’s why I suggested it.’

* * *

Drop-off began at seven-thirty.

Dutch was at the main entrance by seven-twenty. The fifth-grade patrols were already in position — two of them this morning, stationed at the drop-off lane with the gravity of students who had been given a responsibility and intended to discharge it. They opened car doors. Every vehicle that came through, every child who climbed out — the patrol had the door. And before they closed it they said it, every time: Have a blessed day. Said to the parent, to the child, to whoever was in the car. Said without diminishment, as though each saying were the first.

Not because earliness indicated a problem. Because earliness gave him time. Time to see the children before the rush, time to read the morning’s conditions in each face, time to register when something was different from usual without the pressure of a hundred other things happening simultaneously.

The first car was the Hendersons. Always. Dr. Henderson, pediatrician, began his rounds at seven thirty and dropped his daughter Sophie on the way. Sophie was eleven, precise, and conducted her morning departure from the vehicle with the efficiency of someone who had decided that sentiment was a separate category from punctuality. She got out, collected her bag, said goodbye to her father through the window, and walked toward the entrance with the focused tread of someone who had a plan for the day and was already executing it.

‘Morning, Sophie,’ Dutch said.

‘Morning, Mr. Dutch.’ She had decided two years ago that his name required a title and Dutch had not corrected this. ‘The forecast says possible afternoon thunderstorms.’

‘I saw that.’

‘The soccer field drains slowly on the east side,’ she said. ‘I mentioned it to Coach Alvarez last time.’

‘I’ll check the drainage.’

Sophie nodded and went inside, apparently satisfied. Dr. Henderson gave Dutch a small wave from the car and pulled out.

Cooper arrived at seven twenty-two.

Cooper was six, in first grade, and had decided approximately three months into Dutch’s first year that Dutch was a rock collector. Dutch was not a rock collector. He had not said anything that Dutch could identify as having produced this impression. Nevertheless Cooper had arrived one Monday morning with a piece of rose quartz and presented it with the seriousness of a gift that had been considered carefully, and Dutch had accepted it with equivalent seriousness and put it on his desk, and the following Monday Cooper had brought another rock, and the Monday after that another, and now there was a jar on Dutch’s desk that contained thirty-seven rocks of varying geological interest and that Dutch had stopped trying to explain and had simply incorporated into the fact of the desk.

Cooper came across the drop-off lane this morning with both hands in his jacket pockets and the expression of someone managing a secret.

‘Morning, Cooper.’

Cooper reached into his right pocket and produced a piece of limestone with a fossil impression on one face — something small and segmented, partially revealed, the rest still in the stone.

Dutch took it. Held it up to the morning light.

‘Found it at the creek,’ Cooper said. ‘My dad said it might be a trilobite.’

‘Your dad might be right,’ Dutch said. ‘These were ocean creatures. This part of Florida was underwater a long time ago.’

Cooper looked at the limestone with the recalibrated attention of a boy absorbing the fact that the ground he stood on had once been ocean floor.

‘The whole school?’

‘The whole state.’

Cooper considered this for a moment. Then he went inside, apparently needing time to work through the implications.

Dutch put the fossil in his jacket pocket and watched the lane.

* * *

Lily arrived at seven thirty-one with her mother, who drove a green minivan and who had the quality of a woman navigating a complicated morning with practiced efficiency. Lily was seven, in second grade, and had one of the most complete inner lives of any child Dutch had encountered in three years of this work. She did not keep it to herself.

She came across the lane with her backpack listing slightly to the left, which was its normal orientation, and stopped in front of Dutch with the air of someone who had something to deliver.

‘I had a dream,’ she said.

‘Tell me.’

This was the correct response. Dutch had learned early that Lily’s dreams required the same quality of attention that Samuel had given to the lion walk and that Robert had given to the park — not passive reception but active presence, the kind that communicated that what was being said was worth saying.

‘There was a library,’ Lily said, ‘but all the books were the wrong size. Like, the big books were tiny and the tiny books were enormous. And I needed a specific book and I couldn’t find it because I didn’t know which size it would be.’

‘Did you find it.’

‘Not yet. I woke up.’ She considered the unresolved ending with the equanimity of someone who had accepted that dreams had their own timeline. ‘Maybe tonight.’

‘I hope you find it.’

She nodded and went inside. Her mother gave Dutch the small exhausted smile of a parent who had been hearing about this dream since six fifteen and was grateful for someone else’s reception of it.

Dutch watched the lane fill and empty and fill again.

At seven thirty-eight a boy named Theo arrived crying, which was unusual enough that Dutch moved from his position at the entrance to the car window. The father, a man Dutch had spoken to twice before — an attorney, precise and slightly formal — was talking to Theo in a low voice that was patient in the way of someone whose patience was being sustained by effort. Dutch caught the father’s eye. The father gave a small headshake — not a crisis, a morning, the kind of morning that happened. Dutch gave him a nod and moved to Theo’s door.

Theo was nine and the crying was the crying of someone who had already started to be embarrassed about the crying, which made it harder to stop. Dutch opened the door.

‘Hey, Theo.’

Theo looked at him with the reddened eyes of a boy who did not want to be seen this way by an adult he respected.

‘Hard morning,’ Dutch said. Not a question.

Theo nodded.

‘You want to walk in with me.’

Another nod. Dutch stood back and Theo got out of the car and they walked together toward the entrance, Dutch not asking what the morning had contained and Theo not offering it and the walking itself doing what walking did, which was give the body something to do while the harder things settled.

At the entrance Theo had stopped crying. He went inside without looking back. The father, pulling away, gave Dutch a wave that carried more than a wave usually carried.

Dutch returned to his position and watched the lane.

* * *

By eight o’clock the lane was empty and the school had absorbed its population and the morning had its own momentum.

Dutch went to his office. Coffee, the incident log from the previous week that needed a final notation, a message from the head of school about the upcoming safety audit. He handled these in order with the same focused competence he brought to everything — not because school administration required the same quality of attention as operational work but because he had decided, in his first month, that the work deserved whatever he had. The children deserved it. The families who had brought their children here and trusted the institution with them deserved it.

What he had not expected was the children themselves.

Not that he had expected not to like them. He had assumed a neutral relationship — he was there to protect them, they were there to be educated, the two functions would operate in parallel without much intersection. This had proven entirely wrong in ways he found difficult to articulate.

Cooper’s rocks. Lily’s dreams. Sophie’s drainage assessments. Theo walking in silence through a hard morning because Dutch had offered to walk with him and Theo had decided that was what he needed. Four hundred children moving through this campus every day, each one a complete world in the process of becoming itself, and Dutch standing at the entrance every morning watching them arrive.

He had told Linda about the rocks after the first month.

‘He thinks you collect them,’ Linda had said.

‘I don’t collect them.’

‘You have a jar of them on your desk.’

‘That’s different.’

Linda had looked at him with the expression she had when he was arriving at a thing she had already understood.

‘You keep them,’ she said.

He kept them. The little girls gave him hugs. Not all of them, not every morning, but enough of them regularly enough that it had become part of the texture of drop-off. They came across the lane and hugged him before going in and Destiny or whoever was working the patrol held the door and said have a blessed day and they went. Dutch had not known what to do with this at first and then he had understood that nothing was required of him except to receive it correctly, which meant with full attention and without making it more or less than what it was. A child saying good morning with her whole self. Parents brought him coffee sometimes. A Starbucks appearing at the entrance window, passed out without ceremony, the parent already pulling away. He drank it. He waved to the next car.

He checked the east gate lock notation in the log and submitted the fifth maintenance request, this one with a photograph of the mechanism and a specific note about operational delay under stress conditions. Then he went to the morning briefing with the two staff members who handled the secondary entrances.

* * *

Linda came at noon on Tuesdays.

She brought food — not because the school lacked a cafeteria or because Dutch couldn’t feed himself but because Tuesday lunch had become a thing that neither of them had explicitly decided to make a thing and that had become a thing anyway, the way certain practices became fixed in a long marriage without anyone choosing them. She arrived at twelve-fifteen with a bag from whatever she’d made that morning and they ate in his office with the door open and the school sounds coming in and talked about whatever needed talking about.

This Tuesday she brought chicken and rice and a container of the cucumber salad that Dutch liked and that she made in large batches on Sunday afternoons.

‘How was the morning,’ she said, settling into the chair across from his desk.

‘Good. Theo had a hard one. He walked in okay.’

‘What happened.’

‘I don’t know. Didn’t ask.’

Linda nodded. She knew his practice — the walk was the thing, not the explanation. The explanation came later if it came at all and the walk worked regardless.

‘Cooper brought a fossil,’ Dutch said.

He produced it from his pocket. Linda took it and held it up to the window light the same way Dutch had held it up to the morning light.

‘Trilobite,’ she said.

‘Possibly.’

‘His father told him.’

‘How do you know that.’

‘Because Cooper wouldn’t know what a trilobite was without being told and he would have led with it if he’d known before his father said it. The way he presented it — he was still absorbing it.’ She handed the fossil back. ‘The father is the rock person.’

Dutch looked at the fossil. He had not thought about it this way and it seemed correct.

They ate. Linda told him about the morning — the Uganda ministry call that had gone long, a complication with the school fees for a family in Jinja that she was working through with the ministry’s local coordinator. The garden, which needed attention he hadn’t given it. The small group Bible study on Thursday and the chapter they’d been working through for two weeks that kept producing more conversation than the session could hold.

Dutch listened with the attention he gave to Linda, which was the same attention he gave to everything that mattered and which Linda had learned, over forty years, to read accurately. She knew when he was fully present and when some part of him was somewhere else. Today he was fully present, which she noted without remarking on it.

‘The memoir,’ she said, after a while.

‘I know.’

‘You haven’t mentioned it in two weeks.’

‘There’s nothing to mention.’

‘That’s what I mean.’ She was not pressing. Linda rarely pressed. She stated the fact and let him do what he would with it.

Dutch put down his fork.

The memoir had been his intention for two years. He had the room — the spare room with the good desk and the lamp and the legal pad that Linda had set up without being asked. He had the material. He had forty years of it, more than a man could reasonably organize into a narrative that other people would find meaningful. The problem was not material.

The problem was the first line.

He had written it four times. Four different first lines, each of which had seemed, in the moment of writing it, to be the correct beginning, and each of which had revealed itself in the reading to be wrong in a way he couldn’t precisely identify. Too formal. Too distant. Beginning in the wrong place, with the wrong fact, at the wrong angle to the thing he was actually trying to say.

He didn’t know what he was trying to say. That was the underlying problem. The material was clear. The shape of the account was clear. What he wanted the account to mean — what he wanted a reader to take from the forty years, what he himself understood them to add up to — that was not yet clear, and without that clarity the first line had nothing to orient itself toward.

Linda was watching him with the patience of someone who had sat with his silences for four decades and had never once tried to fill them prematurely.

‘I don’t know what it’s for yet,’ he said.

She considered this. ‘You mean the memoir.’

‘I mean what I want it to do. What I want someone to understand after they’ve read it that they didn’t understand before.’

‘Maybe you don’t know yet because you haven’t written it yet,’ she said. ‘Maybe the writing is how you find out.’

Dutch looked at her.

‘That’s either very wise or very convenient,’ he said.

‘Both,’ she said. ‘Eat.’

He ate.

She stayed until one, which was her usual time, and left him with the second half of the cucumber salad and the empty containers and the particular quality of the office after she left it, which was different from the quality of the office before she arrived in a way that he had never found a satisfactory way to describe.

* * *

The afternoon was administration and a meeting with the head of school about the safety audit and a conversation with Coach Alvarez about the east soccer field drainage that Sophie had correctly identified as a recurring issue. Dutch had checked the drainage that morning and Sophie had been right — the east side ran slow after rain and the ground was soft in a strip along the fence that created both a maintenance problem and a sightline issue he wanted to address before the audit.

The head of school — a woman named Dr. Patricia Reeves, sixty, who had been running Christian schools for thirty years and who had hired Dutch because, she had told him at the end of their first meeting, she had never met anyone who walked a campus the way he had walked hers — listened to his audit preparation report with the focused attention she brought to everything operational. She was not a woman who nodded through reports she wasn’t fully absorbing. She asked two questions, both of them good, and noted one thing he had not considered, which he added to the preparation list.

At four o’clock, after the last bell and the campus had quieted to its after-school register — a few teachers still in classrooms, the athletic programs running on the fields, the particular late-afternoon quality of a school that had done its primary work for the day and was settling into the margins of it — Dutch did the afternoon perimeter walk.

Same route as the morning. Fence line, east gate, north corner with the oak branch. He did it slowly, the way he did it every afternoon, the day’s accumulated attention having produced in him the particular quality of fatigue that was not tiredness but the gentle deflation of sustained focus. He let his mind go where it went.

It went, as it often did at this hour, to the writing room.

The legal pad on the desk. The pen beside it. Four starts, four wrong first lines, the blank space after each of them where the second line had refused to follow.

He was afraid of it. Linda was right. Not afraid of the material — he had spent thirty years in rooms that produced genuine fear and he knew what that felt like and the legal pad did not produce it. It was a different variety. The fear of someone who understood that the account, once written, would be the account. That the choices about what to include and what to leave out, about where to begin and where to end, about what the forty years meant and what they didn’t mean — those choices, once made on paper, would be the choices. Not provisional. Not adjustable. The thing itself.

He had spent thirty years in work where the choices could always be revised. You could change the cover, adjust the approach, adapt to what the room actually was rather than what you’d expected. The memoir didn’t work that way. You wrote it and then it was written and then whoever read it read what you had decided to say, which was also what you had decided you were.

He reached the east gate.

The lock engaged correctly when he tested it. Three seconds, the same sequence. He stood at the gate for a moment and looked through it at the road outside, the late afternoon light on the ordinary Florida street, a car going past, a woman walking a dog, the entirely unremarkable texture of a place where people lived their lives.

He had protected things in a lot of places. He had done it in ways that those places would never know about and could not have named if asked. He had done it at considerable cost across thirty years that had taken him to countries and situations that the people on this street could not have fully imagined.

Now he did it here. Four hundred children. A jar of rocks. A lock that took three seconds and ought to take one.

He did not find the comparison diminishing. He had thought he might, before he took the position, and he had found instead that the scale of the thing protected had no relationship to the quality of attention it deserved. Cooper had brought him thirty-seven rocks. Lily had shared thirty-seven mornings of her interior life. Theo had walked in silence through a hard morning because Dutch had been standing at the entrance.

This was the work.

He locked the east gate and walked back to his office and closed up and drove home.

* * *

Linda was in the garden when he got there.

The garden at this hour had the quality it always had in the late afternoon — the light coming through the hedge at the far end in long bars, the flower beds she had been working on for two years now something she was satisfied with, her gloves on the edge of the bed where she always left them when she stood up. She was deadheading something in the far corner and did not look up when she heard him on the path.

He stood on the back step and watched her work.

Forty years. He had tried to write about what forty years looked like and had not found the language for it. It was not a single thing — not love, though that, not habit, though that also, not the gravity of a shared life, though that most of all. It was something that had accumulated past the point where any individual component could account for the whole of it. He had made her a promise in a church in 1983 and he had not kept every element of it with equal fidelity — the absences alone were a ledger he carried — but he had kept the essential one, which was that he would come back, and he had always come back, and she had always been here when he did.

She looked up.

‘How was the afternoon.’

‘Fine. Drainage issue on the east soccer field. I’ll get it sorted before the audit.’

‘Sophie will be relieved.’

‘Sophie will notice if I don’t.’

Linda smiled. She pulled off her gloves and tucked them under the edge of the bed and came across the garden.

‘I’m going to sit on the back step with you for a while before I start dinner,’ she said.

‘All right.’

They sat on the back step together and looked at the garden in the late light. The bougainvillea on the back fence. The cardinal that came every evening to the feeder she’d put up three years ago and that Dutch had initially assessed as a potential concealment issue and had reconsidered.

The cardinal came.

They sat with it.

‘I’m going to try the first line again tonight,’ Dutch said.

Linda said nothing. She leaned against him slightly, the weight of her familiar and not a thing he could have described to anyone adequately.

‘Maybe you’re right,’ he said. ‘Maybe I find out what it’s for by writing it.’

‘I usually am,’ she said.

The cardinal ate and left and the garden settled into its evening quality. They sat on the step until the light went fully and then Linda went in to start dinner and Dutch sat for another few minutes alone in the garden in the dark thinking about nothing in particular and everything in general, the way he sat at this hour on these evenings, the school and the forty years and the legal pad and the texture of a life that had turned out to be what it had turned out to be.

He went in.

Dinner. Linda’s account of the Uganda ministry call — the Jinja school fees situation, the family, what it would take to resolve it. Dutch listening. They prayed before the meal the way they always did — Linda’s prayer, short and direct, the way she talked to God, which was the same way she talked to everyone. For the Uganda families. For the work at the school. For the people they knew who were in hard places.

She did not pray for Jackson by name. She prayed for the people they loved who were far from home doing dangerous work. She had been praying this for thirty years and Dutch knew it covered everyone it needed to cover.

He said amen.

After dinner he went to the writing room.

He sat at the desk. Turned on the lamp. The legal pad in front of him, the pen beside it. The photograph Linda had framed and hung on the wall without asking — the two of them on a beach somewhere, early years, both squinting into the sun.

He picked up the pen.

He looked at the blank page.

He sat there for twenty minutes and wrote nothing and put the pen down and went to bed.

The fifth first line would wait.

Tomorrow he would try again.

Chapter V
The Briefing

The evening briefing was at seven o’clock in the lodge’s small conference room — a wood-paneled space off the main veranda where Robert laid out the following morning’s game drive on a laminated park map and went through the logistics with the methodical care of someone who had done this many times and had learned that the details mattered.

Vehicle assignments. Departure time. The track they would take — the northern loop, he said, which at this time of year was the most productive in the early morning hours. The animals moved at night and tended to concentrate in the northern section’s acacia stands as the day warmed. He expected elephants. Possibly buffalo at the water crossing eight kilometers in. Lions were always possible in the northern section though he made no promises.

Marcus had his notebook open and was writing.

Amara sat beside Gloria with the elephant in her lap and listened to Robert with the attention she brought to people she had decided were serious.

The three Germans were there, Dieter and Hanna and Klaus, and the South African couple who had arrived two days ago and whom Gloria had exchanged pleasantries with at dinner without yet learning their names. Seven guests in the small room, Robert at the map, the park sounds coming through the window behind him.

And Pius at the door.

Gloria had seen him there for ten minutes, standing at the edge of the room with the posture of a man attending to logistics — confirming vehicle assignments from a clipboard, making a note when Robert indicated which guests were on which vehicle, the ordinary efficiency of a driver doing his job. She had noticed him in the way she noticed the background details of any room, not because anything had drawn her attention specifically but because she noticed things and Pius was there to be noticed.

She had not yet named what she was noticing.

At the briefing’s end Robert told them departure was six o’clock, breakfast available from five-thirty, and that the morning temperature would be cool enough to want a layer. He looked at Marcus. ‘Notebook.’

‘Ready,’ Marcus said.

Robert almost smiled. He rolled the map and the room began to empty and Gloria gathered the children and moved toward the door and as she passed Pius she looked at him because he was there and because she looked at people.

He did not look back.

Not rudeness. Something else. A man managing his own face. She noticed it and moved through the door into the evening and the moment passed and she did not think about it again.

Not yet.

* * *

She woke before the alarm.

Four fifty-eight. The room still dark, the falls audible to the west, the park outside doing what the park did in the last of the night before dawn committed to itself. She lay for a moment in the wakefulness that came before something anticipated — not anxiety, not excitement exactly, the alert stillness of a body that had known before the mind confirmed it that today was a day that required readiness.

She got up.

Dressed in the dark. The layer Robert had recommended — a light fleece, the predawn temperature genuinely cool at this elevation near the river. She moved quietly so the children could have their last minutes of sleep before she woke them.

At the window she looked at the park.

Dark still, the eastern sky just beginning the first suggestion of what was coming, the quality of grey that preceded light without yet being it. The acacia visible as silhouettes. The river somewhere to the west, the falls somewhere beyond the river, their sound the permanent bass note of this place.

She thought about Jackson.

She had called him last night after the briefing and the children were in bed. The connection had been poor — worse than the previous nights, his voice coming through with the broken quality of a signal from a place that was serious about being unreachable. She had told him about the cubs, about Amara’s hand on the cub’s side, about Marcus and F-7. He had laughed at Marcus holding his ground with the lion. The connection had dropped before she finished.

She hadn’t called back.

She looked at the park going from black to grey and thought: he is somewhere in eastern Congo in the last of the night and I am here and in two days Joseph comes at ten and we go home and he comes home when he comes home.

This was the arithmetic of their life. She had made her peace with it years ago and the peace held, mostly, in the way that things you have made your peace with hold — not perfectly, not without the occasional awareness of the cost, but sufficiently.

The alarm went at five fifteen.

She went to wake the children.

* * *

Marcus was already sitting up.

He had the notebook. He had been awake, she could tell, for some time — reading back through the previous two days’ entries with the focused review of someone preparing for a continuation of work already in progress. He looked up when she came in with the expression of a boy who had been ready for an hour and was glad the readiness was finally required.

‘Is it time.’

‘It’s time. Breakfast first.’

He was already up.

Amara required the full ceremony. The gradual surfacing, the elephant located and verified, the arrangement of herself that sleep had disrupted and that needed restoring before the day could begin. Gloria sat on the edge of the bed and waited with the patience she had developed for Amara’s mornings, which was a different patience from the patience she had developed for everything else — more complete, more without agenda, the patience of someone who had understood that Amara’s pace was not a problem to be solved.

The elephant was found. Amara sat up.

‘Game drive today,’ Gloria said.

Amara looked at the elephant. Then at the window where the park was beginning to show itself in the early light.

‘Will we see the cubs.’

‘That was yesterday. Today is the park. Different animals.’

Amara considered this. ‘Elephants.’

‘Robert thinks so.’

Amara got up with the decision in her face that meant she had assessed the situation and found it acceptable. She carried the elephant to the bathroom and set it on the edge of the sink while she washed her face, which was where it went when she washed her face, and the morning began.

* * *

Breakfast on the veranda at five-thirty.

The sky to the east going from grey to the first pale color, not yet gold, the shade that came before gold when the light was building toward something. The park emerging from the darkness in stages — the near grass first, then the tree line, then the distance. A pair of Egyptian geese moving along the river bank below the lodge’s terrace, purposeful and unhurried.

Marcus saw them and wrote without looking down.

Amara ate with her ceremony and the elephant beside her plate and looked at the park coming visible and said nothing. She was in the mode she had for mornings that required observation rather than conversation.

The Germans were at the next table, Dieter cheerful and Hanna with coffee and Klaus with his camera already around his neck checking settings with the automatic attention of someone for whom the camera was an extension of the hand. They exchanged good mornings. Dieter said something about the light that Klaus agreed with. Robert came through and confirmed departure in twenty minutes and the table began its preparations.

Gloria drank her coffee and looked at the park.

It was the third morning of looking at it and it had not become familiar in the way that usually happened with landscapes over time. It was still entirely itself, still organized by its own logic and not by her presence in it, still the thing that it would have been if none of them had come. She had thought about this on the first morning and she thought about it again now — a place that didn’t need you to be in it and that was more itself for not needing you.

She thought: I will tell Jackson about this. The quality of it. He will understand what I mean.

She finished her coffee.

‘Ready,’ she said to the children.

Marcus was already standing with the notebook.

Amara picked up the elephant.

* * *

The vehicle was a white Land Cruiser with open sides and a roof rack and bench seating, the same configuration as the afternoon drive on the first day. It was parked in the lodge’s small vehicle area with two others, and Robert was there checking it over with the practiced eye of someone who knew what the park track did to vehicles.

Pius was at the wheel.

Gloria saw him when they came around the corner of the main building — sitting behind the wheel with the engine idling, his hands at the correct position, looking at the windshield rather than at the guests arriving. She noted this the way she had noted him at the briefing. The same quality. A man managing something in himself.

She almost said something.

The thought arrived and she moved through it without stopping because there was nothing definite to say, nothing that had crossed the threshold from impression to information, and the morning was beginning around her and Marcus was already at the vehicle asking Robert about the northern loop’s buffalo crossing and Amara was carrying the elephant across the gravel with the care she brought to carrying things that mattered.

She got in.

Left rear seat, the window side. Amara beside her, the elephant in her lap. Marcus directly behind Robert in the front passenger seat, which was where Marcus had been on every drive and which Robert had apparently accepted as the boy’s permanent assignment.

The Germans filled the remaining seats. Klaus at the far right rear, camera ready. Dieter and Hanna in the middle row with the easy comfort of people who had done this many times.

Robert said: ‘Everyone ready.’

Marcus said: ‘Ready.’

Pius put the vehicle in gear.

They moved out of the lodge compound onto the park track and the morning game drive began.

* * *

The track went north.

In the early morning light the park had a quality it didn’t have in the afternoon — cooler, the colors not yet driven to their midday intensity, the grass silver rather than gold, the tree line holding the last of the night’s shadow. Animals that had been moving through the darkness were settling now or already still, caught between the night’s activity and the day’s waiting.

Robert was on his radio, in contact with another vehicle working a different section, the low crackle of it part of the morning’s soundtrack. He pointed twice in the first kilometer — once to a pair of oribi in the grass that Gloria saw only after he pointed and once to something in the tree line that resolved itself as a grey crowned crane standing absolutely still in the shadow of an acacia as though it had decided to become part of the tree.

Marcus wrote. Amara looked. The Germans murmured to each other in low voices and Klaus’s camera worked steadily.

Pius drove.

Gloria was watching the park and she was watching Pius and she was not watching Pius in any way she would have been able to explain — peripherally, the way you watched things that hadn’t given you a reason to watch them directly but that occupied a position in your attention that you hadn’t assigned consciously.

His hands on the wheel were correct. His speed was correct. He followed the track the way a driver followed a track he knew well.

What was not correct was his breathing.

She was close enough to the front of the vehicle to notice it — the slight elevation of it, the quality of a man whose respiratory rate was telling a different story from his posture. She had been a nurse for long enough that bodies communicated information to her that they were not intending to communicate and Pius’s body was communicating something she did not yet have words for.

Three kilometers from the lodge the track curved northeast and the vegetation thickened on both sides, the acacia pressing closer, the grass higher. Robert was still on the radio. Marcus was writing something about the crane. Amara had the elephant against her side.

Four kilometers.

Five.

The track bent north again and straightened into a long flat section where the acacia thinned and the grass opened on both sides and the sky came back fully overhead, the early light now genuinely golden, the kind of morning light that the park produced at this hour that she had been storing all week.

She was looking at the light.

She felt the vehicle slow before she registered it consciously — the deceleration, subtle, not the deceleration of a driver responding to an animal on the track but something earlier than that, the deceleration of a decision already made.

Pius stopped the vehicle.

He got out without a word.

Robert looked at him. ‘Pius.’

Pius was already at the front of the vehicle. He opened the hood. He stood looking at the engine with his hands on the frame.

Robert unclipped his radio.

Gloria looked north.

The track ran straight for two hundred meters before it bent west into the acacia. At the bend, barely visible in the shadow of the trees, something was parked. A vehicle. Dark. Not moving.

Not a park vehicle.

She knew it was not a park vehicle in the same moment she understood that Pius had stopped here on purpose and that the vehicle to the north had been waiting for him to stop here on purpose and that the understanding had arrived too late for anything except what she could do in the seconds before the waiting vehicle began to move.

She looked at her children.

Marcus had seen her face. He was very still, the notebook in his hands, looking at her with the expression he had — the one that meant he had understood something about the people around him and was waiting.

Amara had the elephant in her lap. She was looking at her mother.

Gloria counted.

Not seconds. Resources.

The vehicle to the north had begun to move. Slowly, with the unhurried certainty of something that had timed itself and knew it had time.

She had thirty seconds. Perhaps less.

She looked at Amara’s elephant.

She reached across and took it from Amara’s lap.

Amara’s hands came up — the beginning of a protest, the instinctive resistance of a six-year-old to the removal of something essential. Gloria put her left hand on Amara’s knee, firm and warm, the pressure that meant something serious is required and you know how to be serious when it is.

Amara’s hands stopped.

With her right hand Gloria pushed the elephant under the seat. Deep. Into the angle where the seat met the floor, as far as her hand could reach, where it would stay.

She looked at Amara’s face.

Her daughter was looking back at her with the eyes of a child who did not yet know what was happening but who knew completely that her mother did and that her mother’s face was asking something of her.

‘Whatever happens,’ Gloria said. Quietly. Level. The voice she used for things that mattered absolutely. ‘You stay close to me. You hold my hand. You do not let go.’

Amara looked at her for one second.

‘Okay, Mama,’ she said.

The second vehicle stopped.

The doors opened.

Four men.

* * *

It happened quickly and without noise.

The four men moved with the efficiency of people who had planned this and rehearsed it and were executing the plan. No weapons raised, no shouting, nothing that would produce the chaos that chaos produced. One man to Robert’s door. One to Pius, which was not needed because Pius stepped back from the engine and stood at the roadside with his hands at his sides and his eyes on the ground. Two to the rear of the vehicle.

Robert said something. His voice sharp, the voice of a man whose professional authority had just been directly challenged and who had not yet understood that professional authority was not the operative framework.

The man at his door said something back. Low. Robert went quiet.

The two men at the rear of the vehicle spoke to the Germans first. Hands out, the universal gesture. Out. The Germans exchanged a look — Dieter’s face doing the rapid recalibration of a man who had been in difficult places before and was applying that experience now, Hanna with her hand on his arm, Klaus very still with the camera at his chest.

They got out.

The two men looked at Gloria.

She looked back at them with the full steadiness she had. Not performing steadiness — producing it, from the place where the thing underneath fear lived when you had trained yourself to go there. She had been a nurse in understaffed clinics in difficult places. She had been a military spouse for fifteen years. She had built a life in a walled compound in a city that was safe enough by standards that acknowledged safety was relative.

She had always known, in the way that people who pay attention know things they do not say out loud, that a day like this day was somewhere in the range of possible days.

She had her left hand on Amara’s knee.

She found Marcus’s hand with her right.

She squeezed once.

Marcus squeezed back.

‘We’re going to get out of the vehicle,’ she said to her children. Her voice entirely level. ‘Together. Amara on my left, Marcus on my right. We stay together.’

She got out.

The morning was still beautiful. That was what she would carry — the cruelty of the morning being exactly as beautiful as it had been when they left the lodge. The golden light on the grass. The acacia. The sound of a bird somewhere in the tree line that was going about its morning entirely unaffected by what was happening on the track below it.

Amara’s hand in her left hand.

Marcus’s hand in her right.

She looked at Robert, who was standing by the front of the vehicle with the expression of a man in a situation he had no preparation for. He looked at her. She held his gaze for a moment — not asking for anything, not sending anything except the information that she was composed, that she was managing, that he did not need to do anything that would make this worse.

He heard it. She could see it land.

She looked at Pius.

He was still looking at the ground. His hands at his sides. The posture of a man who had done something and was living inside the having done it and had nothing left for the moment that the doing had produced.

She looked at him until he felt it.

He looked up.

She held his eyes for one second. Not with anger — she did not have time for anger and anger was not useful. With the full knowledge of what he was, stated without words, held for one second so that he would know she knew and would carry that.

Then she turned away from him.

The men directed them to the second vehicle. She walked with her children’s hands in hers and she did not look back at the park vehicle and the Germans standing beside it and Robert with his radio and Pius with the ground.

She looked forward.

She counted the men. Their positions. The second vehicle’s configuration. Which door they were being taken to and where she and the children would sit relative to the doors.

She observed everything.

She filed everything.

She did not know if any of it would ever matter. She collected it anyway because collecting it was what she had instead of anything else and because the woman who came out the other side of this was going to need everything the woman going in had been able to gather.

Amara’s hand tightened in hers as they reached the vehicle.

Gloria tightened back.

‘I’ve got you,’ she said. Quietly. Just for Amara. ‘I’ve got both of you.’

They got in.

The door closed.

The vehicle moved.

The park track went north and the morning went with it and behind them the white Land Cruiser sat at the six-kilometer point with its hood open and Robert and the Germans beside it and Pius standing with his hands at his sides and the park continuing around all of them exactly as it had always continued, indifferent and complete and entirely itself.

Under the left rear seat, in the angle where the seat met the floor, a small fabric elephant sat in the dark and waited.

Chapter VI
The Notification

The notification arrived at 11:14 on a Tuesday morning.

Dutch was at his desk in the writing room. Not writing — he had been at the desk for forty minutes with the legal pad in front of him and the pen in his hand and nothing on the page, which was the same result as the previous four attempts and which he had decided to stop calling failure and start calling process, on the grounds that Linda’s advice deserved a genuine trial before he concluded it wasn’t working.

The process, so far, had produced forty minutes of looking at a blank page.

He had the laptop open beside the legal pad because the two did not contradict each other in his understanding of the work — the laptop for the research and the chronology, the legal pad for the actual sentences, the ones that were supposed to emerge from the combination of memory and intention and the particular quality of a Tuesday morning in a Florida writing room with the garden outside the window and Linda somewhere in the house.

The email notification appeared in the corner of the screen.

Automated. A donation confirmation from the relief organization he had supported for eleven years. He almost didn’t open it — these came once or twice a month and required nothing from him beyond existing in the inbox.

He opened it.

The amount was $1,030.00.

He read it twice. Set the pen down on the blank legal pad. Read it a third time.

The number didn’t change.

He sat very still.

1-0-3-0.

Every man who had come up through the agencies in the last thirty years carried it the way you carried certain things — not actively, not in the front of the mind, just present in the background of professional memory the way cardinal directions were present, available when needed without requiring maintenance. Need immediate assistance. Emergency. Clear the channel.

He had received it in many forms across thirty years. Over a radio in a safe house in Karachi. Through a dead drop in Amman. Spoken directly in a parking garage in Langley by a man whose face he hadn’t seen since. Never like this — sanitized through a charitable account, scrubbed of anything traceable, arriving in his inbox as quietly as a receipt for something ordinary.

The sender name meant nothing. A shell, set up to be used once. But the amount was the message and the message had only one meaning and there was only one person Dutch could think of who knew about this account and knew what that number meant and had reason to send it this way rather than any other way.

Which meant every other channel was closed.

He sat with that for a moment. The garden outside the window. The legal pad with its blank page. The pen he’d set down.

He thought about Kaduna. The warehouse by the river. The handshake at the aircraft door and what Jackson had said.

‘Tell him you’ll go. But tell him you need me.’

He closed the laptop.

* * *

He heard Linda in the hallway — her footsteps on the tile, then the pause. He knew that pause. The pause of a woman who had been reading the quality of the silence behind closed doors for forty years and had just read this one.

The door opened.

She looked at him. Not at the desk or the laptop or the legal pad. At him.

‘What happened,’ she said.

Not a question. Linda had stopped phrasing the important things as questions years ago. She stated them and waited.

‘Jackson,’ Dutch said.

She came into the room and sat in the reading chair by the window — her chair, the one she’d brought in when they set up the writing room because the light was better and that she hadn’t moved back. She sat in it now and folded her hands in her lap and looked at him with the full attention she brought to things she had decided to take seriously.

‘Tell me,’ she said.

He told her what he knew. A code sent through a channel that had been constructed to be used once and leave no trace. What the code meant. The fact that Jackson had used it this way — not through Coles, not through any institutional channel, not through any of the architecture that theoretically existed for situations requiring assistance — meant those channels were exactly what he was trying to avoid.

Linda listened without interrupting.

‘Gloria,’ she said when he finished.

Dutch looked at her. She had arrived there before him, which was Linda’s consistent quality — the thing underneath the thing, reached before he finished explaining the thing itself.

‘I don’t know yet,’ he said.

She was quiet for a moment. Outside the garden sat in the late morning light. The cardinal at the feeder. The bougainvillea on the back fence doing what it did in the heat.

‘She was just at the park,’ Linda said. ‘With the children.’

Dutch looked at her.

‘The Jinja school fees family,’ Linda said. ‘I’ve been talking to the ministry coordinator about them. Gloria told the coordinator she wanted to help directly when she got back from Murchison Falls. The coordinator mentioned she was there with the children this week.’

The weight of it settled into the room.

Linda knew Gloria. Not well — they had met twice, once when Dutch and Linda had visited Uganda for the ministry and once when Jackson’s family had come through Florida on a connection, four hours in a city neither family lived in, lunch at an airport hotel with the children running out of patience by the end of it. But Linda knew Gloria the way she knew people she had prayed for by name across a sustained period, which was a different kind of knowing and in some ways a more complete one.

She knew Marcus and Amara by name. She had photographs of them on the refrigerator from a Christmas card that had arrived two years ago — Jackson’s family in front of a jacaranda tree, Nile Crescent visible behind them, everyone squinting into the same equatorial light.

Dutch looked at his wife.

‘I have to find out what it is before I know what it means,’ he said.

‘And if it’s what we think it is.’

He held her gaze. ‘Then I don’t have a choice.’

Linda looked at her hands. Turned them over once in the way she had. Then she looked back at him.

‘You have a choice,’ she said quietly. ‘You’re choosing. Those aren’t the same thing.’

‘Linda —’

‘I know,’ she said. ‘I know what he is to you. I know what Kaduna was.’ She paused. ‘I know what Gloria is carrying right now if it’s what we think it is. I’m not asking you not to go.’ She stopped. Looked at the window. ‘I’m asking you to know what you’re doing when you go. To choose it fully and not call it having no choice.’

Dutch sat with that.

Forty years. She had been saying true things to him for forty years and he had been receiving them with varying degrees of readiness and this one he received without resistance because it was true and because she deserved the honesty of him knowing it was true.

‘Will you pray with me first,’ she said.

He reached across and took her hands.

She prayed the way she always prayed — directly, without performance, as though the conversation were with someone in the room who could be trusted with the full weight of the situation. She prayed for Gloria, said her name. For Marcus and Amara, said their names. For Jackson, wherever he was and whatever he was carrying. For Dutch, that his hands and his judgment would be what was needed.

For wisdom, she said. Not just courage. Wisdom.

Dutch said amen.

He opened his eyes and looked at his wife in the chair by the window and thought about forty years and what they were made of.

‘I’ll call when I know something,’ he said.

‘I know you will.’

Her hand on his shoulder — brief, complete, the weight of it exactly itself. Then she went back to the kitchen.

Dutch opened the laptop.

He needed a way to reach Jackson.

Not Jackson’s operational number — if Jackson had gone dark enough to use the 1030 channel then his operational phone was either gone or compromised or both. He needed something adjacent. Someone who could carry a message without knowing they were carrying it. Someone clean, someone Jackson would know Dutch would try.

He sat for a moment.

Then he picked up his phone and dialed a number he hadn’t dialed in eighteen months.

Four rings. Then the careful pickup of a man who had learned to answer unknown numbers with caution.

‘Matar,’ Dutch said. ‘It’s Dutch.’

A pause. The recalibration of a man updating his read on who was calling and why.

‘Dutch.’ Elias Matar’s voice settling. ‘It has been some time.’

‘Yes. I need to find someone. I think you may know how.’

A longer pause. Matar, Dutch knew, was already running the calculation — who Dutch might need to find, what that finding might involve, what his involvement in the finding would cost him. Matar was not a brave man by his own accounting. He was a practical one, and practical men in his position ran the calculation before they answered.

‘Tell me,’ Matar said.

* * *

Matar called back in forty minutes.

Dutch had spent them at the kitchen table with coffee and his phone and the open-source research he could do from a house in Florida on a Tuesday morning. Not much. A report from the Ugandan Wildlife Authority about a tourist incident at Murchison Falls National Park — a vehicle reported missing on a northern track, a search underway, no further details. A follow-on silence that had the quality of an official story being actively managed. No names. No second report. The silence of a government that had decided a story was more dangerous than the event it described.

He read the single report three times.

A tourist vehicle. A northern track. The language of the report careful in the way of language that was trying not to say things while appearing to say them.

Not a breakdown. Not an animal incident, which would have been handled differently, reported differently, the wildlife authority’s protocols producing a different kind of language. This language was the language of people managing something they hadn’t decided how to characterize yet.

He thought about Gloria and the children on a morning game drive in the northern section. Marcus with the notebook. Amara with the elephant.

He thought about Pius, which was a name he didn’t have yet but would.

When Matar called back his voice had the careful quality it had when he was managing information he’d rather not have.

‘Your man is in Kampala,’ Matar said. ‘I have a number.’

Dutch wrote it down.

‘Elias.’

‘Yes.’

‘What did you hear.’

The pause.

‘I heard a family was taken from a game vehicle in Murchison Falls two days ago. A woman and two children. The park’s northern track, early morning.’ A pause. ‘I heard the Ugandan government has a report and has decided officially that it doesn’t.’

Dutch was quiet.

‘Dutch,’ Matar said carefully. ‘What I heard about whose family — if it’s accurate, this is not a simple matter.’

‘It’s never simple.’

‘No. But some matters are less simple than others. The people who would take his family would know what they were doing. They would know what they were inviting.’ A pause. ‘Which means they believe they have something worth that risk.’

‘Or they have a reason that isn’t transactional,’ Dutch said. ‘A grievance.’

‘Those are harder,’ Matar said. ‘Much harder.’

Dutch looked at the number on the notepad. Ugandan mobile prefix. Jackson, somewhere in Kampala, waiting.

‘Thank you, Elias.’

‘Be careful,’ Matar said. Then: ‘Give my regards to Linda.’

Dutch ended the call and dialed the Kampala number.

One ring.

Jackson’s voice. Flat, controlled, the voice of a man on a line he didn’t fully trust giving nothing to anyone who might be listening.

‘It’s me,’ Dutch said.

A silence that lasted exactly as long as it took Jackson to confirm what he already knew.

‘They moved them once already,’ Jackson said.

Dutch closed his eyes for one second.

‘Tell me everything,’ he said. ‘From the beginning. Leave nothing out.’

Jackson talked for twenty-two minutes.

Dutch listened the way he always listened — completely, without interrupting, storing not just the facts but the texture beneath them. He did not write anything down. The writing could come after. What mattered now was the shape of it, what Jackson knew and what Jackson didn’t know he was telling Dutch alongside what he was.

The facts as Jackson had them:

Gloria and the children had been on a morning game drive in Murchison Falls National Park two days ago. Standard lodge booking, standard game drive vehicle, standard guide and driver. Six kilometers from the lodge, on the northern track, the vehicle had developed a mechanical problem. A second vehicle appeared within ten minutes. Four men in it who were not park staff.

The guide and driver were left behind. Unharmed.

The three German tourists who had been in the vehicle were left behind. Unharmed.

Gloria and Marcus and Amara were taken. Only them. Specifically.

Someone had known they would be there.

The Ugandan Wildlife Authority had a report. The Ugandan Police had a report. The Internal Security Organisation had a report. None of these had produced anything Jackson could identify as a genuine response. When he had gone to the U.S. Embassy in Kampala the previous day the conversation had lasted forty minutes and produced careful language about bilateral relationships and proper channels.

The bilateral relationship with Uganda.

Jackson had said it in the flat tone of a man who could not believe he had heard it said. Dutch understood the tone. He had heard that language before in rooms where institutions were protecting something they valued more than the thing that was actually at stake.

Jackson’s SAD affiliation was the problem. An acknowledged operation in Uganda required cooperation the Ugandan government had no interest in providing because providing it meant acknowledging a problem they had officially decided didn’t exist. An unacknowledged operation by an active SAD officer in a partner nation without authorization was the thing that ended careers and created diplomatic incidents. The institution had calculated and the calculation had produced paralysis.

So Jackson had stepped outside every channel he had. Had sent a number to a donation account in Florida and waited.

Dutch let the silence sit for a moment after Jackson finished.

‘The vehicle breakdown,’ Dutch said. ‘Staged.’

‘Has to be. The driver is an eight-year park employee. I talked to him yesterday. He’s scared. Not of me.’

‘He was paid.’

‘Or threatened. Probably both. He’s the first thread.’ A pause. ‘I haven’t pushed him yet.’

‘Don’t,’ Dutch said. ‘Not until I’m there.’

A silence.

‘Dutch,’ Jackson said. Something in his voice that Dutch recognized from Kaduna — not fear, Jackson didn’t do fear in the way most people did it. The thing underneath fear in men who had trained themselves past the response. The thing that was left when fear had nowhere to go.

‘Tell me,’ Dutch said.

‘This was supposed to be a family trip,’ Jackson said. ‘All four of us. I extended. I told her to go anyway. I told her Joseph would keep them safe.’

Dutch said nothing.

‘Joseph is the driver. The one I use when I’m between deployments. He’s reliable — completely reliable. He drove them up and dropped them at the lodge and went to visit family in Gulu. He was supposed to pick them up Monday morning.’

‘He heard.’

‘He called me before I called him. He was already driving back to the park when I reached him. He went to the lodge, talked to the staff, talked to the Germans who were left behind. He has more of the ground picture than anyone official.’ A pause. ‘He’s in Kampala now. I’m with him.’

Dutch processed this. Joseph — a name and a function at this point, a driver who drove back without being asked, who gathered information and came to Jackson without a call. A man who moved toward the problem rather than away from it.

‘The group,’ Dutch said. ‘What do you have.’

‘Not LRA. Wrong methodology, wrong targeting pattern. The LRA doesn’t take Western-adjacent targets. Someone more focused. Someone who knew who Gloria was.’ His voice was controlled and underneath the control was the thing Dutch had heard. ‘They knew what I do. This wasn’t about her. It was about me.’

‘Someone told them.’

‘Yes.’

One word. Dutch let it sit.

He had known Jackson for three years. Had spent six days with him in conditions that showed you what a man was made of more completely than most people showed themselves in a lifetime. Jackson was not a man who frightened easily or let the personal enter the operational without his permission.

He was afraid. Managing it with everything he had. But it was there and Dutch could hear it and what he heard told him more than the twenty-two minutes of facts had.

Jackson had sent the 1030 because there was no one else.

Dutch looked at the kitchen table. The notepad with the Kampala number. The coffee he hadn’t drunk. Through the window the garden sat in the Florida afternoon, the cardinal at the feeder, the ordinary world doing what it did.

‘I’ll be on the first available flight to Entebbe,’ Dutch said. ‘Don’t touch the driver until I land. Don’t move on anything.’

A pause.

‘Dutch.’

‘Yes.’

‘Marcus,’ Jackson said. His voice doing the thing it did when it was carrying more than professional weight and had run out of places to put it. ‘He’s eight years old.’

Dutch waited.

‘He brought his animal notebook,’ Jackson said. ‘He’s been working on it all week. He had it in his hands when they left the lodge. I know he had it in his hands because Gloria texted me a photograph of him in the back of the vehicle with it.’ A pause. ‘I don’t know if he still has it.’

Dutch sat with that. The animal notebook. Eight years old, working on it all week, and the question of whether he still had it in his hands.

‘And Amara,’ Jackson said. ‘She has a fabric elephant. She doesn’t go anywhere without it. Gloria told me she had it in her lap in the vehicle.’ His voice held what it held. ‘She’s six years old.’

‘Jackson.’

‘Yeah.’

‘I’m coming.’

The line was quiet.

‘There’s a parking structure at Entebbe,’ Jackson said. His voice back to what it was, the discipline returning the way it always returned, because that was who he was and the discipline was not suppression but the form his care took. ‘Third level. Joseph will have the Land Cruiser. Come out of arrivals and call me.’

‘I’ll call when I land.’

Dutch ended the call.

He sat at the kitchen table for a moment. The notepad. The cold coffee. The garden.

Then he went to find Linda.

She was at the kitchen counter when he came in, her hands around a cup of tea, and she turned when she heard him and looked at his face and he watched her read it.

‘Gloria and the children,’ she said.

‘Yes.’

She set the cup down. She looked at the window above the sink, the garden beyond it, for a moment that Dutch did not interrupt.

‘Marcus and Amara,’ she said. Their names said the way she said the names of people she prayed for — specifically, with the weight of having said them before and meaning to say them again.

‘Yes.’

She looked back at him. Her face had the quality it had when she was fully in a thing and not performing her way through it. Not performing calm, not performing strength. Just there, with the full truth of the situation, in the way Linda was always fully in the true things.

‘How long,’ she said.

‘I don’t know. However long it takes.’

She nodded. Not accepting this — she was not a woman who accepted things passively. Receiving it. Filing it into what she would carry while he was gone.

‘The school,’ she said.

‘I’ll call Dr. Reeves from the airport. She’ll manage.’ He paused. ‘Will you call her?’

‘Yes.’

‘Tell her —’ He stopped. There was nothing tellable about the situation that wasn’t either classified or beside the point. ‘Tell her there’s a family emergency. She’ll understand family emergency.’

Linda looked at him. ‘Cooper will ask where you are.’

Dutch stood very still for a moment.

Cooper with his rocks. Lily with her dreams. The fifth-grade patrol who opened the car doors every morning and said have a blessed day to every family that came through. Four hundred children whose mornings he had been standing in for three years.

‘Tell him I’ll be back,’ Dutch said.

‘I will.’ She looked at him with the forty-year look, the one that saw everything he was and everything he wasn’t and had decided, forty years ago and again every day since, that the whole of it was what she wanted. ‘You come back, Dutch.’

‘I always come back.’

‘I know. I’m still saying it.’

He held her gaze for a moment. Then he went to the bedroom to pack.

He packed the way he had always packed for operations — light, specific, nothing that announced itself. The journalist’s cover was already established from previous work, the credentials backstopped and maintained on the off chance they would ever be needed again. He had never let them lapse. He had not known why, exactly, until now.

Thirty minutes. One bag.

Linda was at the front door when he came out. She had made him a sandwich, wrapped, which she held out with the practicality of a woman who had learned that the moments before departures were not the moments for extended conversation.

He took the sandwich.

She put her hand on his face for a moment. Just that.

Then she opened the door.

‘For Gloria and the children,’ she said.

‘Yes.’

‘And for Jackson.’

‘Yes.’

‘And for you,’ she said. ‘Because you’re the one going.’

He went.

The Florida afternoon received him — the heat, the light, the smell of the neighborhood at this hour, the cardinal somewhere in the hedge that he heard but didn’t see. He put the bag in the car and backed out of the driveway.

In the rearview mirror Linda stood in the doorway until the road curved and she was gone.

He drove to the airport.

He did not look at the rearview mirror again.

Chapter VII
The Concrete Room

The room was concrete block, single window, a mat on the floor.

Gloria assessed it in the first sixty seconds and then stopped assessing it because sixty seconds was sufficient and additional assessment would not change what it was. Concrete block meant solid construction, not temporary. The window was high and small and faced east, which she determined from the quality of the light and the angle of the shadows on the floor across the first morning. The mat was clean, which told her something about the people who had put them here, and the bucket in the corner and the second mat beside the first told her they had been expected, which told her something else.

They had been expected. This room had been prepared for them specifically.

She sat with that for a while on the first day and then she set it aside because sitting with it produced nothing useful and useful was what the next hours required.

The children were with her. That was the first fact and the most important and she returned to it throughout the first hours the way you returned to a fixed point when the other coordinates were uncertain. Marcus on the mat beside her, the notebook still in his hands because he had been holding it when they took them and no one had taken it from him, which she noted and which she did not yet understand but filed. Amara against her left side, not speaking, her face doing what Amara’s face did when she was in full interior mode — present and processing and not yet ready to surface what the processing was producing.

Gloria put her arm around her daughter and held her and looked at the room.

* * *

The first thing she did was establish a schedule.

Not because a schedule was what the situation called for in any conventional sense. Because a schedule was what she had and what the children needed and what the hours would become without one. She had seen this in the clinic — the way that structure, even minimal structure, even arbitrary structure, gave people in unbearable situations something to organize themselves around. The unbearable thing didn’t change. The structure gave you a handhold.

Meals when food came, which on the first day was twice — mid-morning and late afternoon, brought by a boy of perhaps fifteen who opened the door and set the tray on the floor and left without speaking or looking at them. The food was adequate. Rice, beans, a piece of bread, water. Not generous and not punitive. The food of people who had made a calculation about keeping them functional.

She made Marcus eat. He wasn’t hungry — she could see that, could read the quality of his not-hunger, which was not illness but the body’s reasonable response to an unreasonable situation. She told him that his body needed fuel regardless of what his mind was doing and that she needed him to eat because she needed him functional and he looked at her with the eight-year-old’s version of the look she had given Pius on the track — full knowledge, accepted and acted on — and ate.

Amara ate without being asked, which was Amara. Her appetite was always independent of her emotional state, a physiological consistency that Gloria had observed from infancy and that served her daughter well now.

After the first meal she told them they were going to rest. Not sleep necessarily. Rest. They lay on the mats and she told them to close their eyes and breathe slowly and she did the same thing beside them and for twenty minutes the room was quiet and the breathing was the only sound and it was not nothing.

After the rest she told them a story.

* * *

The stories were old ones, the ones Marcus had known since he was Amara’s age, expanded and embellished to fill the hours in the way that stories could be expanded when the teller had time and the listeners had nowhere else to be. She told them the one about the boy who wanted to be a fisherman and kept catching the wrong things — boots and old pots and a talking catfish who gave increasingly unhelpful advice — and she expanded it with new characters and new complications and new varieties of unhelpful advice until Marcus was laughing despite himself, the laugh of someone who had decided not to laugh and then found the decision overridden.

She watched him laugh.

She felt what it cost her to produce that laugh in this room on this day and she set the cost aside and continued the story because the story was working and the laugh was real and real was what she had to give them.

Amara listened with the focused interior attention she brought to stories, not laughing but present, her face doing the thing it did when she was building the story’s world inside her own mind. At one point she asked a clarifying question about the catfish’s motivations that was so precisely targeted at the story’s internal logic that Gloria stopped and looked at her daughter.

‘Why does he keep giving bad advice,’ Amara said. ‘If he knows the good advice.’

Gloria thought about it. ‘Maybe he’s afraid that if he gives good advice the boy will catch what he’s looking for and stop coming back,’ she said.

Amara considered this with the seriousness it deserved.

‘That’s sad,’ she said. ‘He should just be honest.’

‘Yes,’ Gloria said. ‘He should.’

She continued the story. She gave the catfish an honest conversation and the boy a resolution and the story an ending that Amara found acceptable and Marcus found satisfying in the way of endings that cost something and then deliver something.

Then she told another one.

* * *

She observed.

This was the other thing she did, running parallel to the schedule and the stories, requiring no visible attention because she had trained herself to observe without appearing to observe across years of clinical work in environments where appearing to assess a patient too directly made the patient defensive and the assessment less accurate.

The room first. Already done in the first sixty seconds but she continued it, adding detail as the light changed and the hours accumulated. The door was solid wood, not hollow — she could tell from the sound of it closing, the weight of the closure. The lock was external. The hinges were on the outside. The window was too high and too small for an adult but not too small for a child to pass through if the child were lifted and if the outside of the window were accessible, which she did not know.

She noted the window dimensions. She did not act on the notation. Not yet. Perhaps not at all. But she noted it.

The compound sounds. She had learned, on the road from the park, that they had crossed into different country — the road surface changing under the vehicle, the quality of the vegetation visible through the vehicle’s covered windows in glimpses, the duration of the drive telling her something about distance if not direction. The sounds of the compound were consistent with a structure of some size — multiple people moving, a generator that ran at intervals, a vehicle arriving once on the first day and departing an hour later, voices that she could not parse for language at this distance but that had the quality of Acholi or something related.

She noted all of it.

The guards. Two that she had identified from sounds alone — footsteps at consistent intervals outside the door, the rhythm of a patrol that had been established and was being maintained. The interval was approximately forty minutes, which was either the genuine patrol interval or the interval they maintained when they thought no one was counting.

She was counting.

The boy who brought food. Young — she had revised her estimate to fourteen on the second visit, when the light was better. He kept his eyes down. He set the tray and left. On the second day, when he came in the afternoon, Amara said good morning to him.

He stopped.

He had been in the motion of turning to leave and he stopped. He did not look at Amara. He did not respond. But he stopped and stood very still for a moment before he continued to the door.

Gloria noted this.

On the third day, when he came in the morning, Amara said good morning again. He stopped again, the same way, for a longer moment. He still did not look. He still did not respond. But the stillness was different from the first time — less automatic, more considered. The stillness of someone who was having a reaction he was trying to manage.

He was fourteen years old in a compound in the bush and a six-year-old girl was saying good morning to him every day.

Gloria did not know yet what she would do with this. She kept it where she kept everything else and continued to observe.

* * *

Marcus was managing.

This was not a small thing and she did not treat it as a small thing internally, though what she showed him was equanimity because equanimity was what he needed from her. He was eight years old and he had been taken off a game drive vehicle in a national park by four men he had never seen and he was in a concrete room in a place he didn’t know with his mother and sister and he was managing.

The notebook helped. She had understood this from the first hours — the notebook was his instrument of order, the thing he used to make sense of the world by recording it, and the world he was currently in was not a world he could record in the ways he usually recorded. He couldn’t observe animals. He couldn’t note the light on the grass or the her behavior of F-7 on his termite mound. But he could record what he observed and what he observed was the room and its contents and its rhythms, and after the first day he began doing this with the same methodical attention he had brought to the park.

The window dimensions, estimated. The patrol interval, timed with a count he kept in his head. The boy’s approximate age and the times of his appearances. The sounds of the compound at different hours.

She saw him doing this and said nothing about it. Let him do the work that the work required of him.

On the second night, after Amara was asleep, Marcus moved closer to her on the mat.

‘Mama.’

‘Yes.’

‘Is Dad coming.’

She looked at her son in the dark of the room, his face barely visible, his voice entirely controlled in the way he controlled things when control was what he had decided was required.

‘Yes,’ she said.

‘How do you know.’

She held his gaze in the dark. Eight years old, in a concrete room in the bush, asking her a question he deserved an honest answer to.

‘Because I know your father,’ she said. ‘And because there is nothing in the world that would keep him from coming.’

Marcus was quiet for a moment.

‘The notebook,’ he said. ‘Everything I’ve been writing. About the room and the sounds and the times.’

‘Yes.’

‘Is it useful.’

She looked at her son.

‘It might be,’ she said. ‘And even if it isn’t, it’s yours. You made it. Nobody can take that.’

He was quiet for another moment. Then he lay back on the mat and looked at the ceiling.

‘I had the notebook when they took us,’ he said. ‘They didn’t take it.’

‘No. They didn’t.’

‘I don’t know why.’

Gloria looked at the ceiling too. The room was very dark at night — no light from the window, no ambient glow from anywhere. A darkness that was complete in the way of places far from towns.

‘Maybe they didn’t think it mattered,’ she said. ‘That’s the thing about people who underestimate you. They don’t take the things that matter because they don’t know what matters.’

Marcus absorbed this.

‘They didn’t take the elephant either,’ he said. ‘You hid it.’

‘Yes.’

‘Amara asked me where it was,’ he said. ‘I told her you put it somewhere safe.’

Gloria turned her head and looked at her son in the dark.

‘Thank you,’ she said.

‘She believed me,’ he said. ‘She didn’t ask again.’

Gloria looked back at the ceiling.

Eight years old. She thought about Jackson and what he would see when he saw his son and what Marcus would see when he saw his father and she held that thought carefully and did not let herself feel it fully because feeling it fully was not available to her right now and she needed everything she had for the room she was in.

Later, she told herself. Feel it later.

She listened to her son’s breathing even out into sleep.

Then she lay in the dark and thought about everything she had observed and what it added up to.

* * *

Amara was different from Marcus and her management of the situation was different and Gloria watched both with the attention of a woman who knew her children precisely enough to read the difference between managed distress and genuine function.

Amara was genuinely functional.

Not because she didn’t understand the situation. Amara understood things at a frequency that most adults underestimated, and she had clearly understood from the moment on the track that this was a serious thing and not a thing that would resolve quickly. But she had made some interior decision in the first hours that Gloria couldn’t fully account for and that produced in her daughter a quality of settled presence that was not performance and not denial.

She asked for the elephant once, on the first morning. Directly, without preamble, in the way she asked for things when she needed them.

‘Where is the elephant.’

Gloria looked at her. Marcus had already told her it was somewhere safe but Amara was asking her mother directly because her mother was the one who knew.

‘I put it somewhere it would be waiting for you,’ Gloria said. ‘When we go back.’

Amara looked at her with the look she used when she was evaluating the complete truth of something.

‘Under the seat,’ Amara said.

It was not a question.

‘Yes,’ Gloria said.

Amara nodded. She did not ask about it again.

What she did instead — and this was the thing that Gloria watched and stored in the place where she stored the things about her children that she would carry the rest of her life — was take the giraffe from her bag. The small carved wooden giraffe that Hanna had given her at dinner two days ago. She had put it in the bag herself that morning, organized alongside the elephant with the interior logic that was entirely Amara’s.

She took it out. She held it in her lap the way she held the elephant. She looked at it with the assessing look she had given it when Hanna first presented it.

Then she held it against her chest and closed her eyes.

She did this every day. Not all day — at set times, for set durations, with the ceremony she brought to things that required ceremony. The giraffe was standing in for the elephant. Not pretending to be the elephant. Standing in for it, holding the position, keeping the place warm.

Gloria watched her daughter do this and said nothing and felt everything she had told herself to feel later arriving anyway.

She held it. Let it come. Then she set it aside and returned to the room and what the room required.

* * *

On the fourth day the boy said something.

He had come in the morning with the tray and Amara had said good morning and he had stopped as he always stopped and stood for the stillness that had been growing longer each day. Gloria was watching him from the mat. Not directly — the peripheral attention that looked like inattention but missed nothing.

He said something very quiet, almost under his breath, in a language that was not English. Then he left.

Gloria looked at Amara.

Amara was looking at the door.

‘What did he say,’ Gloria asked.

Amara looked at her. ‘I don’t know the words,’ she said. ‘But I think he said good morning back.’

Gloria looked at the closed door.

A fourteen-year-old boy in a compound in the bush, saying good morning back to a six-year-old girl in a language she didn’t know, in a voice so quiet it was almost not there.

She filed it.

She did not know yet what she would do with it. But she knew it was the first crack in the wall of the situation and cracks were where things entered that the wall was designed to keep out.

She would wait. She would watch. She would keep saying good morning through Amara, who required no instruction to say it and who said it with the complete natural sincerity of a child who had been raised to greet people as people regardless of their circumstance.

She thought about Jackson. About Dutch, whose name she had heard from Jackson with a quality in his voice that she had learned to recognize as the register he used for people he trusted completely. She had met Dutch once, briefly, years ago. She remembered a quiet man, attentive, who had said little and seemed to understand everything.

Jackson had told her once, after Kaduna, that Dutch was the person you wanted in the room when the room was the problem.

She thought about that now.

The room was the problem.

She thought: come to the room, Dutch. Come to the room.

Then she set the thought aside because it was not useful yet and there were children to manage and a boy who was beginning to say good morning and a window she had been measuring since the first day.

She returned to the work.

The work was keeping her children whole until the room changed.

She was good at the work.

She kept doing it.

Chapter VIII
The Landing

The flight from Amsterdam landed at Entebbe at six forty in the morning.

Dutch had slept four hours somewhere over the Mediterranean, the practiced sleep of a man who had learned across thirty years to take it in whatever windows the operational calendar provided and who had trained himself not to waste time being awake when being awake produced nothing. He woke over Sudan, ate something from a tray he didn’t taste, and spent the remaining two hours doing what he did in the hours before a room — building it in advance. Not planning. Building. The architecture of what he was flying into, assembled from everything Jackson had told him and everything Matar had given him and the open-source research he’d done from a kitchen table in Florida.

What he had: a park driver who had been recruited or coerced. A network with enough operational sophistication to reproduce a park vehicle sticker and plan an ambush around the game drive booking. A Ugandan government suppressing the story. A cashiered major named Okello who had spent four years building toward the moment he now had. A son named Patrick who had left university to follow his father into the bush.

What he didn’t have: the compound’s location. The chain between Okello and whoever had provided Jackson’s family’s itinerary. Whether Gloria and the children had been moved since the first day.

He looked at the darkness outside the aircraft window and thought about what he’d said to Matar. A grievance that wasn’t transactional. Those were harder.

He thought about Patrick. A boy who had made a choice at twenty years old to follow his father into something that had no good ending, driven by the one force strong enough to override every other calculation. Dutch had seen this before — not often, but enough times to know what it looked like from outside and what it required from the person trying to get inside it. You didn’t argue with that kind of loyalty. You found what it was protecting and you addressed that.

The aircraft began its descent.

Uganda came up from the dark — the lights of Kampala to the north, the lake below catching the first grey of the coming dawn, the flatness of the terrain around the airport surprising after the hills of the south he’d seen on previous approaches to this continent. He watched it come and felt the familiar compression of arriving — the world narrowing from the altitude’s abstraction to the ground he was about to stand on.

He cleared immigration on the journalist credentials. The officer looked at the passport, looked at Dutch, stamped it with the unhurried efficiency of a man who had been at this post since midnight and was six hours into an eight-hour shift. Dutch thanked him and went through.

* * *

He called Jackson from the arrivals hall.

One ring.

‘Third level,’ Jackson said. ‘Blue Land Cruiser. Come out the main doors and turn left.’

Dutch turned left.

The morning outside the terminal was warm already, the equatorial warmth that arrived with the light here rather than building through the day the way it did in Florida. The car park was half-full, the early arrivals’ vehicles mixed with the overnight taxis whose drivers were sleeping in their seats. Dutch found the stairwell and went up and pushed through the door onto the third level.

The Land Cruiser was parked against the far wall. Jackson was out of the vehicle before Dutch reached it, the assessment happening in the two seconds of approach — Jackson reading Dutch the way Dutch read rooms, checking the condition of the man who had just crossed nine time zones to be here.

They shook hands. Firm. Brief.

‘You look like you flew coach,’ Jackson said.

‘I did fly coach.’

‘Coles,’ Jackson said, with the flatness of a man who had expected nothing better and received it.

Dutch looked at him. Three years since Kaduna and the years showed in the way that operational years showed — not age exactly, more weight. The the gravity of a man who had been carrying something without being able to set it down.

‘How are you holding,’ Dutch said.

Jackson looked at him for one second. ‘I’m holding.’ He turned toward the vehicle. ‘Come meet Joseph.’

* * *

Joseph was behind the wheel.

Dutch got in the back and looked at the man in the driver’s seat and ran the assessment he ran on everyone in the first thirty seconds. Forty-five, lean, with the economy of movement of someone for whom physical efficiency had been a survival requirement and had become habit. The hands on the wheel were a driver’s hands — practiced, relaxed, the grip of someone who knew what the vehicle would do and had no anxiety about it. His eyes in the mirror found Dutch’s and held them for a moment with the directness of a man who was comfortable being seen.

‘Joseph,’ Jackson said from the passenger seat. ‘This is Dutch.’

‘I know who he is,’ Joseph said. His English was accented and precise. ‘Jackson has talked about you.’

‘Good things, I hope,’ Dutch said.

‘He said you were the person to call when he couldn’t call anyone else,’ Joseph said. ‘That seemed good enough.’

Dutch looked at Jackson.

Jackson was looking at the car park.

Joseph pulled out and drove toward the exit ramp and the road north and Dutch settled into the seat and felt Uganda arriving around him through the open windows — the smell of it, the combination of red earth and vegetation and morning air that was this country and not any other country he’d been in.

‘Tell me about the drive up,’ Dutch said to Joseph. ‘When you took the family. Everything you remember about the lodge and the staff and who was on the gate.’

Joseph drove and talked.

Dutch listened.

* * *

He had expected to find, in Joseph’s account, the useful background of a careful observer. What he found instead was the account of a man who had been trained by his own history to read environments for threat and who had been doing so, without naming it, for every kilometer of every drive he had made for Jackson’s family across three years.

Joseph described the lodge approach, the staff he had noted at the gate, the vehicle area and its layout. He described the briefing room he had passed on his way to the staff quarters that first evening — he had not attended the briefing but he had noted through the open door who was in it and where they were positioned. He described Pius, unprompted, before Dutch asked about him. The way Pius had moved around the vehicle area that morning before the drive. The quality of his movement. The thing about it that Joseph had filed without at the time knowing why.

‘He was checking,’ Joseph said. ‘The vehicle. But not the way a driver checks a vehicle before a drive. The way a man checks something he has already decided about and is confirming is still true.’

Dutch looked at the back of Joseph’s head.

‘You noticed that,’ Dutch said.

‘I notice those things,’ Joseph said. Without elaboration. The statement of a man for whom the noticing was simply how he moved through the world and had always moved through it.

Jackson was watching the road. He had heard this from Joseph before and was letting Dutch hear it now.

‘When you went back to the park,’ Dutch said. ‘After you heard. Tell me what you found.’

Joseph had arrived at the lodge six hours after the abduction. The vehicle had been recovered and returned — the park rangers had found it on the northern track with the hood up, the guide and driver and three German tourists standing beside it. The Germans had been taken to the lodge. Robert the guide had given a statement to the park rangers and was still there when Joseph arrived. Pius had been taken to the ranger station.

Joseph had talked to Robert first.

‘He was—’ Joseph paused, selecting the word. ‘Undone. He felt responsible. He kept saying there was nothing he could have done but he was saying it the way people say things they’re trying to convince themselves of.’

‘He couldn’t have done anything,’ Jackson said. Flat. Not reassurance. A fact being stated for the record.

‘No,’ Joseph agreed. ‘But he didn’t know that yet.’

He had talked to the Germans. Dieter and Hanna and Klaus, still at the lodge, their departure delayed by the rangers’ request that they remain available for questions. Klaus had photographs. Not of the abduction — his camera had been on the seat beside him when it happened and he had not raised it, which was its own information about the speed and authority of the event. But he had photographs from the previous days that included the vehicle, the track, the northern section’s geography. Joseph had asked to see them and Klaus had shown him without hesitation.

‘He asked if they would help,’ Joseph said. ‘I told him yes.’

‘Will they,’ Dutch said.

‘Klaus photographed the bend in the track where the vehicle stopped. He didn’t know what he was photographing at the time — he was photographing the light, the way it came through the acacia at that hour. But the bend is in the image.’ Joseph glanced at Dutch in the mirror. ‘And there is a vehicle visible at the far end of the straight section. In the shadow of the trees. He didn’t notice it when he took the photograph.’

Dutch sat forward slightly.

‘The second vehicle,’ he said. ‘Already in position.’

‘Waiting,’ Joseph said. ‘They were already there when the game drive vehicle entered the straight section. Which means they entered the park before the game drive left the lodge.’

‘Through the northern gate,’ Jackson said.

‘Through the northern gate,’ Joseph confirmed. ‘Which opens at five-thirty for staff vehicles. They had a sticker.’

Dutch looked at the Uganda going past outside the window. The road north of Kampala, the traffic thinning as the city’s outskirts gave way to the rural interior, the sky large and clear above it.

‘The photographs,’ he said. ‘Do you have them.’

‘Klaus sent them to my phone,’ Joseph said. ‘I’ll show you when we stop.’

Dutch nodded. He leaned back. The vehicle in the photograph, already in position in the shadow of the acacia at six in the morning, while Gloria was eating breakfast on the veranda with the children and the falls were audible and everything was as it was supposed to be.

He thought about the briefing the night before. Pius with the clipboard. The seat assignments confirmed in that room going to someone outside it before the morning.

Someone had passed the information. Not Pius — Pius was the execution, not the intelligence. Someone who had known the family would be at that lodge on that day in that vehicle on that track. Someone with access to information that didn’t come from inside the park.

He filed it. He would give it to Matar tonight.

* * *

They drove north for four hours.

Jackson briefed Dutch on Okello during the second hour. Not the operational record — Dutch had assembled that from the open-source research. What Jackson gave him was the human record. What he had observed in the field. The quality of Okello’s command, the nature of his authority with his men, the thing that had happened in Ituri and how it had happened and why, from the inside of a joint operation that Dutch had not been part of.

Dutch listened without interrupting.

Jackson talked about Ituri the way he talked about everything operational — sequentially, factually, stripped of editorializing. The village. The ADF’s use of it. Okello’s assessment and the decision it produced. What Jackson had seen and what he had reported and what the reporting had set in motion.

‘You were right to report it,’ Dutch said when Jackson finished.

‘I know that.’

‘Okello knows it too.’

Jackson looked at him.

‘Somewhere underneath the four years,’ Dutch said. ‘He knows the tribunal was a legitimate institutional response to what he did. That’s not the same as accepting it. But a man who believed completely that he was right and everyone else was wrong wouldn’t have spent four years building toward a confrontation. He’d have disappeared into the bush and kept fighting. He built toward a confrontation because some part of him needs someone to look at what happened and say it happened.’

Jackson was quiet.

‘The son,’ Dutch said. ‘Patrick. Tell me what you have.’

Jackson told him. Twenty-three, twenty-four. Engineering student in Kampala. Left in the weeks after the tribunal. Chose his father over everything the tribunal had destroyed.

Dutch listened and thought about a young man who had made that choice and had been inside it for four years. What four years of a father’s certainty did to someone who had walked away from his own life to be near it. Whether the certainty had held or whether something in it had begun to wear, the way certainties wore when the thing they promised kept not arriving.

He thought about what it meant that Patrick was still there. Four years was a long time to wait for something. Four years was also long enough to start wondering whether the waiting was going to produce what the waiting was for.

‘His son staying,’ Dutch said. ‘That’s everything Okello has left. You understand that.’

‘Yes.’

‘Which means Patrick is not just the door. He’s the thing that makes Okello a man with something to lose. Without Patrick’s presence in that compound Okello is a man who has already lost everything and has nothing to protect. A man like that is genuinely dangerous in a way that someone with something to lose is not.’

Jackson looked at the road. ‘So we need Patrick in the room.’

‘Patrick needs to be in the room,’ Dutch said. ‘And I need to have been in the room long enough before you arrive to know which way he’s leaning.’

They drove. The road was opening around them as they moved north, the hills of the south and center flattening, the sky larger, the quality of the country changing toward the particular openness of the north that Dutch had been told about and was now seeing for the first time.

After a while Jackson said: ‘The notebook.’

Dutch looked at him.

‘Marcus had it when they took them. Gloria texted me a photograph twenty minutes before the drive. He’s holding it in both hands in the back of the vehicle.’ Jackson paused. ‘Nobody took it from him.’

‘Tell me about Marcus,’ Dutch said.

Jackson was quiet for a moment. Not the quiet of someone deciding whether to answer. The quiet of someone deciding how.

‘He’s particular,’ Jackson said. ‘He needs things a certain way. The notebook — he carries it in his hands, not in a bag. It’s not a bag item, he’ll tell you. It has to be in his hands.’ A pause. ‘When he was small we thought it was something we were doing wrong. Then we stopped thinking that.’

Dutch nodded. He filed it the way he filed everything. Not diagnosing, not labeling. Just adding it to the picture of a boy he had never met and was going to need to understand precisely when the time came.

‘He held his ground on the lion walk,’ Dutch said.

Jackson looked at him. ‘Gloria told you that.’

‘She told me. The guide told him to hold. He held.’

Jackson looked back at the road. His face did something Dutch didn’t try to name.

‘Yes,’ Jackson said. ‘That’s Marcus. You tell him the rule and the rule is the rule.’ He paused. ‘Whatever you need him to do when you get them out — tell him clearly. One thing at a time. He’ll do it.’

Dutch heard everything Jackson had just told him. The rule is the rule. One thing at a time. The instruction that came from a father who knew his son precisely and was giving that precision to the man who was going to need it.

‘Understood,’ Dutch said.

Jackson nodded and looked at the road and didn’t say anything else about it.

In the front seat Joseph drove and said nothing. Dutch had the sense, watching the back of Joseph’s head, of a man who was listening to everything and filing it alongside his own accumulation, building the same picture from a different angle.

Dutch thought: this man has been driving Jackson’s family through this country for three years. He knows things about them that Jackson doesn’t know he knows.

He thought: I want to talk to Joseph alone at some point.

He filed that too.

* * *

They reached the park’s southern boundary in the early afternoon.

Not the lodge — there was nothing at the lodge for Dutch that justified the exposure of arriving through the front gate. Joseph knew a track that ran along the park’s western boundary and that gave access to the northern section from the outside, used by maintenance crews and by people who moved between the park and the communities on its edge in ways that the formal gate didn’t capture. He had used it before and he used it now, pulling off the main road onto a red earth track that Dutch would not have identified as a track without knowing it was one.

They drove the boundary track for forty minutes.

Then Joseph stopped.

‘The northern loop track is two hundred meters that way,’ he said, gesturing east through the bush. ‘The bend where they stopped the vehicle is approximately six kilometers north of here.’

Dutch got out.

He walked the two hundred meters to the track. The bush here was different from what the photographs had shown — denser, the vegetation pressing closer, the sound of birds in it constant and layered. He came out of it onto the track and stood on the red earth and looked north.

The track was exactly what the photographs had shown. A single vehicle width, the acacia on both sides, the grass beyond it going to the park’s interior. The light at this hour coming through the trees at the same angle it had come through them four days ago when a white Land Cruiser had driven this track with a woman and two children in it and a driver whose breathing was telling a story his posture wasn’t.

He walked north.

He walked slowly, the way he walked environments he was reading. The track surface, the sightlines, the distance between the bend ahead and the straight section beyond it. He was building the geometry of the morning. Where the vehicles had been, where the people in them had been, what Gloria had been able to see from the left rear seat in the seconds between understanding and the door closing.

At the bend he stopped.

He stood where the second vehicle would have been visible from the approaching Land Cruiser. The shadow of the acacia here was exactly what it had been in Klaus’s photograph — deep, at this angle, the tree line creating a pocket of dark that swallowed the shape of anything parked at the bend’s apex. You wouldn’t see a vehicle there until you were in the straight section and moving toward it.

Thirty seconds from that point at normal driving speed.

Gloria had had thirty seconds.

He stood at the bend and thought about what she had done with them. The elephant. The calculation of a woman who had looked at her children and at the situation and had used the time available for the thing that mattered most at the margin.

Not escape — there was no escape in thirty seconds with two children on a narrow track with a vehicle at the bend and a driver who had already made his decision. She had known that in the first second. So she had used the thirty seconds for the thing at the margin.

The elephant under the seat.

And then: whatever happens, hold my hand, don’t let go.

Dutch stood at the bend in the track and felt what he felt about that and set it aside and walked back south toward the vehicle.

Jackson was leaning against the Land Cruiser when Dutch returned. He looked at Dutch’s face.

‘Well,’ he said.

‘Professionally done,’ Dutch said. ‘The position was chosen correctly. The timing was correct. They had the gate sticker and they had the park’s internal radio protocols because the second vehicle knew when the game drive vehicle entered the straight section.’ He looked at Jackson. ‘Someone inside the park fed them the radio traffic. Not Pius. Someone else.’

‘The logistics employee,’ Jackson said. ‘From the briefing.’

‘Possibly. Or someone at the ranger station with access to the vehicle tracking system.’ Dutch paused. ‘Either way, Pius isn’t the full picture. He’s the thread we pull first.’

Jackson straightened. ‘Let’s go find Pius.’

* * *

Pius lived twelve kilometers from the park’s southern boundary.

Joseph knew the road. He had driven it the day he went back to the park, had driven past the compound once to assess it before deciding to approach the following day when he had more of the picture. He drove it now without consulting anything and pulled off the road onto a track that led to a cluster of structures behind a low fence and stopped fifty meters out.

‘That one,’ he said, indicating the nearest structure. ‘Concrete block. He lives there with his wife and three children.’

Dutch looked at the compound. A covered porch, a woman visible at the edge of it doing something he couldn’t make out from this distance. A child’s voice from inside.

‘I go first,’ Dutch said. ‘Jackson, give me five minutes. Then come in and sit. Don’t stand over him.’

Jackson nodded.

‘Joseph,’ Dutch said.

Joseph looked at him in the mirror.

‘Stay with the vehicle. If anyone comes along this road that looks wrong, one tap on the horn.’

‘One tap,’ Joseph said.

Dutch got out and walked toward the compound.

* * *

The woman on the porch was shelling groundnuts into a metal bowl. She looked up when Dutch came through the gate with the wary attention of a woman who had not been expecting a stranger and was assessing what variety of stranger this was.

Dutch stopped at the base of the porch steps.

‘Good afternoon. My name is David. I’m looking for Pius. I have some questions about the park.’

She set the groundnuts down. Called a name into the interior of the house.

Pius came to the door.

He was perhaps fifty-five, shorter than Dutch had assembled from the ground evidence, with the stillness of a man who had been waiting for a knock at his door for four days and was now receiving it and did not yet know what variety of knock it was. He looked at Dutch with eyes that were doing the same calculation Dutch had seen on the track in Klaus’s photograph — the calculation of a man assessing whether the thing arriving was what he feared or something else.

Dutch kept his face entirely open. No authority, no threat, nothing that closed any door before he was through it.

‘Pius,’ Dutch said. ‘I’m not from the government. I’m not from the police. I’m here because a woman and two children were taken from your vehicle four days ago and I need to find them.’

Pius’s face moved. Not surprise — the expression of a man bracing for a weight and feeling it arrive shaped differently than he expected.

‘May I sit down,’ Dutch said, gesturing to the porch. Asking. Not entering.

A long moment. Then Pius stepped back from the door and gestured to the chair beside his wife’s.

Dutch sat. Pius sat in the doorway, half inside and half out.

Dutch let the silence sit.

‘The woman who was taken,’ Dutch said. ‘She has a daughter. Six years old. Before they took her the mother hid the girl’s toy under the seat of your vehicle. So it would be there when the girl came back for it.’

Pius said nothing.

His wife had stopped moving. The groundnuts in her lap, her hands still.

‘A mother who has thirty seconds,’ Dutch said, ‘and uses some of them to think about her daughter coming home.’ He paused. ‘I’m not here about what you did. I’m here about what you know. Those aren’t the same thing.’

The porch was quiet. A child’s voice from inside the house, then quiet again.

Pius looked at his hands.

‘They came to me three weeks ago,’ he said. In English. Accented, slow, the English of a man who used it carefully. ‘Not here. At the park. They knew my name. They knew my family.’

Dutch waited.

‘They said the woman’s husband had done something. In Congo. That men had died because of him and justice had not been done.’ He looked up briefly. ‘I don’t know if this is true.’

‘What did they ask you to do.’

‘To make sure the woman and children were in my vehicle on the correct morning. To stop at the place they described.’ He looked at his hands again. ‘They said no one would be hurt. Only taken for a short time.’

‘Did you believe them.’

Pius said nothing. Which was no.

‘But you did it anyway.’ Dutch said it without accusation. A fact.

‘They knew about my son,’ Pius said. ‘In Kampala. He is studying there. They said if I did not help them—’ He stopped. ‘A man will do things for his children that he would not do for himself.’

The porch was very quiet.

Dutch looked at Pius. Fifty-five years old, eight years of service, a son at university in Kampala who represented everything the service was for. Dutch did not perform sympathy and he did not perform judgment. He held the full truth of what Pius had done and the full truth of why and let both of them sit in the same space without resolving them into something simpler.

‘The men who came to you,’ Dutch said. ‘How many.’

‘Two. The same two both times.’

‘A name.’

‘The one who spoke said his name was Okello.’

Dutch filed it. ‘A contact number.’

‘They gave me a number to call when the booking was confirmed.’

‘Do you have it.’

Pius looked at him for a long moment. Then he went inside. Dutch sat on the porch and listened to the park country around him. A hornbill somewhere in the trees at the compound’s edge. A vehicle on the road, distant, fading. The child’s voice again from inside.

He thought about Gloria in a concrete room four days ago and what she had been doing in those four days. He had been building a picture of her across everything Jackson had told him and everything the ambush site had told him and what she had done in thirty seconds told him more than any of it. He thought: she is managing. Whatever she found in that room she is managing it.

He thought about a six-year-old girl who said good morning to strangers as a matter of principle.

He thought about a boy with a notebook who held when he was told to hold.

Pius came back with a piece of paper. A number in pencil, the digits careful, the handwriting of a man who had written it down because he was afraid he would need it and afraid of what needing it meant. Dutch photographed it and handed it back.

‘Keep it,’ Dutch said. ‘Don’t use it. Don’t throw it away.’

Pius nodded.

Dutch heard the gate behind him. Jackson, five minutes to the second, coming across the compound with the measured pace of a man who had been waiting and was done waiting. He came onto the porch and took the remaining chair and looked at Pius with the expression Dutch had asked him to hold — not threatening, not warm. Present. The weight of him in the chair, the physical authority of a man who had spent his professional life in environments where authority was established without words.

Pius looked at Jackson and his face changed. Recognition — not of Jackson specifically, but of what Jackson was. The category. The kind of man whose family he had helped put in a vehicle and driven to a bend in a road.

He looked at the floor of the porch.

‘I have children also,’ Pius said.

Nobody responded.

It didn’t require a response. It was true and it was insufficient and Pius knew both things and had said it anyway because it was the only thing he had left to say.

Dutch stood.

‘Pius. When this is over there will be questions from the authorities. I can’t protect you from those questions. What I can tell the people asking them is that you helped.’ He held the man’s eyes. ‘Is there anything else. Anything you haven’t told me.’

Pius was quiet for a moment.

‘Okello,’ he said. ‘When he came the second time. He said something to the man with him. He did not know I understood Acholi.’

Dutch waited.

‘He said the soldier would come,’ Pius said. ‘That they were ready for the soldier.’ He looked at Jackson. ‘I think the soldier is you.’

The porch was very still.

Dutch looked at Jackson. Jackson’s face had not changed. The discipline holding everything flat. But Dutch had learned across six days in Kaduna and four hours in this vehicle what was underneath Jackson’s flat surface and what was underneath it now was the thing they’d both been moving toward since Dutch read the ambush site.

This was not a kidnapping that expected a negotiation.

It was a trap that expected Jackson.

Dutch thanked Pius. He touched the man’s shoulder briefly on the way off the porch — not forgiveness, acknowledgment, the recognition of a man who had done something he was going to have to live with and was beginning to understand the dimensions of that living.

He walked back through the gate to the vehicle.

Jackson came through behind him.

They got in. Joseph started the engine without being asked.

Dutch looked at the road ahead.

‘They know you’re coming,’ he said.

‘Yes,’ Jackson said.

‘Gloria and the children are how they get you into the room they want you in.’

‘Yes.’

‘Which means a straight extraction doesn’t work. The moment they sense extraction they move the family or they use them.’ Dutch looked at him. ‘We need a different approach.’

Jackson looked at the road. His hands on his knees. Completely still.

‘Tell me,’ he said.

Dutch told him.

Joseph drove north and listened to everything and said nothing and the road went toward Pakwach and the Nile and the border country beyond it where a man had been waiting four years for a soldier who was now in a vehicle two hours behind him and coming.

Chapter IX
The Road North

The guesthouse in Pakwach sat two streets back from the river, far enough from the Nile bridge that the traffic noise was manageable and close enough to the water that the air had a quality the inland air didn’t. Beatrice, the owner, ran the place with the complete efficiency of a woman who had decided long ago that her business model depended on guests who valued discretion and that discretion was best served by her own selective incuriosity about the nature of their business.

She gave them three rooms on the upper floor without being asked for three and charged accordingly and told them hot water was available from six to eight in the morning and that was the entirety of the conversation.

Dutch noted the three rooms. Beatrice had looked at Joseph when she said it, which meant she had decided Joseph was part of whatever this was, which meant Beatrice was more observant than her incuriosity suggested.

He filed it as an asset rather than a liability.

* * *

He called Matar at nine.

Not from his own phone — Joseph had acquired three local SIMs in Kampala, distributed them without comment, and the SIMs were what Dutch used now for anything that mattered. The phone number from Pius went to Matar first.

Matar picked up on the second ring.

Dutch gave him the number. Explained what it was and where it had come from and what he needed. Matar said he would call back. Dutch believed him.

Then he opened the laptop and worked.

The open-source side of the intelligence picture had limits he had already reached — Okello’s name appeared in nothing accessible, which was itself information about how thoroughly the Ugandan government had cleaned the tribunal record. What Dutch could find were the edges. The UN monitoring brief that described the Ituri event without naming names. The human rights report that named the UPDF unit. The journalist’s account that mentioned a tribunal.

He cross-referenced these against the timeline Jackson had given him on the road. The dates aligned. The unit designation aligned. The geography aligned.

Then he worked the supply chain.

A compound in the bush on the Congolese side of the northwestern border required logistics. Fuel. Food. Equipment. People moving in and out. None of that happened in a vacuum — it left traces in the commercial and transportation networks of the border region, faint traces, the kind that required someone with local knowledge to read. He did not have local knowledge. He had Matar’s network, which reached further than Dutch sometimes remembered.

He wrote down everything he had and everything he needed and waited for Matar to call back.

* * *

Jackson knocked at eleven.

Dutch opened the door. Jackson came in and sat in the chair by the window and looked at the notepad on the desk with the expression of a man who had been doing his own accounting in another room and had arrived at the same place Dutch was.

‘What do you have,’ Jackson said.

Dutch told him. The open-source picture, the supply chain logic, what he was waiting for from Matar. Jackson listened without interrupting, his elbows on his knees, his eyes on the floor in the posture he had when he was receiving information he intended to act on.

When Dutch finished Jackson was quiet for a moment.

‘The compound,’ he said. ‘I have a contact in Arua who tracks movement across the northwestern border. Ex-UPDF. He’s been watching the informal crossings for three years as part of a counter-smuggling network that isn’t official but is effective.’

‘He’ll know the compound.’

‘He’ll know the traffic to and from it. Which is close enough.’ Jackson paused. ‘His name is Opoka. He runs a fuel depot in Arua. He’s reliable within his limits.’

‘What are his limits.’

‘He’ll tell me what he’s observed. He won’t tell me anything that puts him between two parties he doesn’t want to be between.’ Jackson looked up. ‘Which is a reasonable limit.’

‘Arua is how far.’

‘Four hours from here. We go tomorrow morning.’ He paused. ‘What are you waiting for from Matar.’

‘The phone number from Pius. Where it leads, who it belongs to, what the supply chain looks like from the Ugandan side.’ Dutch looked at his notepad. ‘And one more thing. The family’s itinerary. The booking at the lodge, the game drive assignment, the vehicle sticker and timing. Someone provided that to whoever recruited Pius. Someone with access to information that doesn’t come from inside the park.’

Jackson was still.

‘That information lived in three places,’ Dutch said. ‘The lodge’s booking system. Joseph’s phone, if he communicated with the lodge on the family’s behalf.’ He paused. ‘And whatever records exist of Jackson’s family’s location and movements.’

‘My operational file doesn’t include my family’s travel plans,’ Jackson said.

‘No. But your SAD contractor record includes your home location. Your family’s country of residence. A determined person with access to that record and access to Ugandan commercial databases could have constructed the picture.’ Dutch held Jackson’s gaze. ‘Someone sold that information to Ogenga or directly to Okello’s network. I’m asking Matar to trace the information chain backward from Pius’s contact number. I want to know where the chain starts.’

Jackson looked at him for a long moment.

‘If Matar finds it,’ he said carefully, ‘I want the name.’

‘You’ll have it.’

Jackson nodded once. Then he stood. ‘I’m going to sleep three hours. Wake me if Matar calls with something that changes the morning.’

‘I will.’

Jackson went to the door. Stopped.

‘Joseph,’ he said. ‘He’s downstairs. He said he’d sleep in the vehicle.’

Dutch looked at him.

‘He won’t sleep in the vehicle,’ Jackson said. ‘He’ll watch the street. He’s been doing it since we got here. He did it in Kampala too — I thought he was sleeping and he wasn’t.’

Dutch thought about that. A man who drove for Jackson’s family, who went back to the park the day it happened, who had been quietly maintaining a perimeter watch without being asked since they arrived.

‘Tell me more about him,’ Dutch said.

Jackson leaned against the doorframe. ‘He’s from Gulu. He was taken by the LRA when he was twelve. Two years with them before he got out — him and one other boy after a firefight. Gave themselves up to a government unit, handed over their weapons. The government gave them a choice — army, school, or help finding their families. He chose family first, then school.’

‘He found them.’

‘Some of them.’ Jackson said it the way it was, without softening. ‘He told Marcus on the drive up. Marcus asked him directly and he told him.’

Dutch thought about that. A forty-five-year-old man telling an eight-year-old boy the true version of his own history on a road in northern Uganda.

‘And Marcus,’ Dutch said.

‘Didn’t ask the follow-on question,’ Jackson said. ‘Understood that some of them was the complete answer.’

Dutch looked at the notepad on the desk. At the window and the Pakwach street below it. At the door.

‘Go sleep,’ he said. ‘I’ll talk to Joseph.’

Jackson went.

* * *

Joseph was in the Land Cruiser with the windows down and the seat reclined to an angle that was almost but not quite horizontal. He had his eyes closed. Dutch came down the guesthouse steps and crossed the street and stood at the passenger window.

Joseph opened his eyes.

‘You’re not sleeping,’ Dutch said.

‘No.’

Dutch opened the passenger door and got in.

They sat for a moment in the vehicle with the Pakwach night around them. The bridge was audible from here — a truck crossing, the sound of weight on the river crossing that Dutch had been hearing intermittently since they arrived. The Nile somewhere beyond the buildings, moving the way it always moved, indifferent to everything that happened near it.

‘I want to ask you some things,’ Dutch said. ‘About the family. About the drive up and the lodge and what you observed.’

‘Ask.’

Dutch asked. He took Joseph through the drive from Jinja to the park — not the route, not the logistics, the human details. The children in the vehicle. Gloria in the passenger seat. The roadchecks and how she watched Joseph handle them. Whether the children had slept. What Marcus had talked about and what Amara had done.

Joseph answered everything. Not quickly — he took his time with each answer in the way of someone who was not reconstructing events from memory but retrieving observations he had stored with care.

He described Marcus with the precision of a man who had been paying attention to this child across multiple trips over three years and had accumulated an understanding of him. The notebook, always. The need to have it in his hands rather than in a bag — Joseph had learned this on the first trip when he had tried to put it in the back with the luggage and Marcus had retrieved it without drama but with the absolute certainty of someone for whom the retrieval was not optional. The questions, always in order, moving from one thing to the next with the completeness of someone who needed to understand the current thing fully before moving to the next. The way he processed new information — not immediately, but with a delay that produced, eventually, something more complete than the immediate response would have been.

‘On the drive up,’ Joseph said, ‘I told him about being taken by the LRA. About getting out. He listened to the whole account without interrupting. When I finished he was quiet for a long time.’

‘And then,’ Dutch said.

‘He said he was glad I found them. The ones I found. He said it exactly like that.’ Joseph paused. ‘Most people don’t say it like that. Most people say something about the ones I didn’t find, even if they’re trying to be kind. He said what was true about the ones I found and stopped there.’

Dutch looked at the windshield. The Pakwach street, a cat crossing in the far reach of a streetlight, the bridge and the river beyond.

‘What did you make of that,’ Dutch said.

Joseph was quiet for a moment. ‘I thought he understood more than he was supposed to understand at eight years old. And then I thought — maybe he understands differently, not more. He heard what I said and he said back what was true about it. No extra.’

Dutch nodded slowly.

‘He’ll need clear instructions when we get them out,’ Dutch said. ‘One at a time. In order. He’ll follow them exactly.’

Joseph looked at him. ‘I know. I know how to talk to Marcus.’

Dutch looked back at him. Of course he did. Three years of driving this family.

‘You’ll be the one who gets them into the vehicle,’ Dutch said. ‘When Jackson brings them out. I may still be inside.’

‘I understand.’

‘Amara,’ Dutch said. ‘Tell me about her.’

Joseph almost smiled. It was the smallest possible version of a smile, the kind that lived at the very edge of expression and didn’t commit to being seen.

‘Amara,’ he said, ‘is the most settled person I know. Including adults.’ He paused. ‘She doesn’t say much. But what she says is always the right thing. Not the expected thing. The right thing.’

‘Give me an example.’

‘On the drive up. She had been asleep and she woke when we passed through a trading center — noise, the market, people at the roadside. She looked at it for a moment through the window. Then she said: everybody is doing something.’ He paused. ‘That was all. Then she went back to sleep.’

Dutch sat with that.

‘She’ll be all right,’ Joseph said. Not reassurance. A statement of fact from a man who knew the child. ‘She has her mother and she has Marcus and she is — she is more herself than most people ever get to be. Whatever they’ve put her in, she is still herself in it.’

Dutch looked out the window at the Pakwach night.

‘The elephant,’ Dutch said.

‘Yes.’ Joseph’s voice carrying what it carried. ‘She had it in her lap when I dropped them at the lodge. She always has it.’

‘Gloria hid it under the seat before they took them.’

Joseph was quiet for a moment.

‘When I went back to the park,’ he said, ‘I went to the vehicle before I talked to anyone. The rangers had recovered it and it was in the vehicle compound behind the ranger station. I looked through it.’ He paused. ‘I found it.’

Dutch looked at him.

‘Under the left rear seat,’ Joseph said. ‘In the angle where the seat meets the floor.’ He reached behind his own seat and produced something from the bag that sat there. He held it out.

The elephant. Small, fabric, grey cotton with embroidered eyes that had slightly different expressions.

Dutch took it. Held it for a moment. Then handed it back.

‘You went back for it,’ Dutch said.

‘I didn’t know if it would matter,’ Joseph said. ‘I knew it mattered to her. That was enough.’

He put it back in the bag behind his seat. His movements careful. The movements of someone who had been carrying something that needed to be carried correctly.

Dutch sat in the vehicle for another minute. The bridge. The river. The Pakwach night doing what nights did.

‘Get some sleep,’ Dutch said. ‘Real sleep. In a room. You’re operational tomorrow and I need you functional.’

Joseph looked at him.

‘The street is fine,’ Dutch said. ‘I’ll watch it.’

Joseph looked at the street. Then at Dutch. Then he got out of the vehicle and went inside without further conversation.

Dutch stayed in the passenger seat.

He watched the street and thought about everything Joseph had given him and thought about a fabric elephant in a bag behind the driver’s seat and what it meant that Joseph had known to go back for it before he knew anything else about what he was going to do next.

Matar called at eleven forty-three.

Dutch was back in his room, the notepad in front of him, the local SIM in the phone.

‘The number belongs to a man named Deo Ogenga,’ Matar said. ‘Transport and logistics, nominally. He operates on both sides of the Ugandan-Congo border, the northwestern side. He moves things that don’t have documentation and he does work for people who can’t access official channels.’ A pause. ‘He is known to people I know in that region as the practical arm of a former military figure. The name that comes up in connection with Ogenga is Okello.’

‘I have Okello,’ Dutch said. ‘I need the location.’

‘The location I don’t have. What I have is that Ogenga moves between a depot in Arua and a crossing point north of Arua into Congo. My contact has seen him use that crossing. The compound is two hours on the Congolese side.’

‘We’re going to Arua tomorrow.’

‘Then you’re close.’ A pause. ‘Dutch. There is a second thing.’

Dutch picked up his pen.

‘The information chain,’ Matar said. ‘The family’s itinerary, the booking, the game drive timing. I traced it backward from Ogenga. One step behind Ogenga is a broker. A man who sells intelligence packages to buyers who don’t explain their purposes. He operates from Nairobi. His name is Stellan Voss.’

Dutch wrote it.

‘And the step before Voss.’

‘This is where it becomes careful,’ Matar said. ‘Voss has many clients and my contact cannot identify which client commissioned this package. But the package itself — the type of information, the level of access it required — points to someone with visibility into SAD-affiliated contractor records. Private sector. Someone who came out of the intelligence world and went into contracting and kept their contacts.’ Another pause. ‘And it is not the first time. My contact has seen two previous packages from the same compiler, both involving SAD-affiliated personnel. Both sold through Voss.’

Dutch set the pen down.

‘Someone is specifically selling SAD contractor information,’ he said.

‘Has been. Repeatedly. With a consistent signature that my contact recognizes.’ Matar’s voice was careful and quiet. ‘I don’t have the compiler’s name. I have the pattern. The pattern is someone inside the contracting world with inside access who has been monetizing it.’

‘Send me everything you have on Voss.’

‘Already sent.’ A pause. ‘Be careful, Dutch. All three of you.’

He ended the call.

He sat at the desk for a moment. Then he went to Jackson’s door and knocked twice.

Jackson opened it. He had slept — not long, but the quality of his alertness had changed from the compressed vigilance of the drive to something that had been briefly reset.

Dutch told him what Matar had given him. Ogenga. Voss. The pattern of SAD contractor information being sold. The compiler whose name Matar didn’t have but whose signature was consistent.

He read the name Voss from the notepad and watched Jackson’s face.

Jackson heard it. Something happened in his face that was not quite recognition and not quite the absence of recognition. The expression of a man who has received a piece of information that connects to something he has been keeping separate.

He said nothing.

‘You know something,’ Dutch said.

‘I know someone who fits the pattern,’ Jackson said carefully. ‘I’m not going to say a name until I’m sure.’

‘All right.’

‘After Gloria and the children are out,’ Jackson said, ‘I’ll deal with Voss.’

The way he said deal with left nothing ambiguous. Dutch didn’t ask. Jackson had earned the right to handle his own accounting on this one and Dutch wasn’t going to take that from him.

‘Okello first,’ Dutch said.

‘Okello first,’ Jackson agreed.

Dutch went back to his room and lay on the bed without undressing and looked at the ceiling of the Pakwach guesthouse and thought about everything that needed to happen in the correct order and whether the order he had was correct.

He thought about the plan. The journalist cover. The compound. Patrick in the room. The exchange.

He thought about what Matar had said. A grievance that wasn’t transactional. Those were harder.

He thought about a man who had lost everything except his son’s loyalty and what that loyalty was worth and whether it was worth enough.

He thought about Gloria in a concrete room counting patrol intervals and saying good morning through her daughter to a fourteen-year-old boy.

He slept two hours. Then the alarm and the road to Arua.

* * *

They were moving at six.

Joseph drove with the early morning country opening around them as they pushed northwest, the flat expanse of the north that Dutch had been watching since yesterday now fully established, the sky enormous above it, the light coming in from the east at a low angle that turned the red earth roads to ochre.

Marcus would want to record this, Dutch thought. The light on the road at this hour.

He set it aside.

Chapter X
The Compound

You were entirely in the place you were in.

Dutch understood why that was worse than some alternatives.

He could work with complicated.

He kept walking.

He thought he was about to find out.

‘Well,’ he said.

‘They’re all right,’ Dutch said. ‘I saw them. Gloria and both children. Marcus has the notebook.’

Something happened in Jackson’s face that Dutch did not look away from and did not comment on and gave the full second it needed.

‘Okello,’ Jackson said.

‘He’ll honor the exchange.’

‘You’re certain.’

‘Yes.’

Jackson held his gaze. The look he had described on the road — not a message through someone else, you to me, face to face. He was looking at Dutch now and Dutch was looking back and everything Dutch had read in that room was in his face and available to Jackson to read if Jackson chose to read it.

Jackson read it.

‘All right,’ he said.

He looked at the track. At the compound somewhere beyond the two kilometers of bush.

‘Tell me everything,’ he said. ‘Patrick first.’

Dutch told him.

Chapter XI
The Account

Dutch told him everything.

Not in the standing position beside the vehicle — Joseph had moved without being asked to the edge of the scrub where he could watch the track in both directions and give Dutch and Jackson the space the conversation required. They sat in the vehicle with the doors open and the Congo bush around them and the late afternoon light going quickly now toward evening.

Dutch gave it in the order it mattered. Okello first — not the account of Ituri, Jackson had that, but the texture of the man. The stillness. The injury that had calcified into something hard and certain over four years. What Dutch had heard underneath the certainty that Okello himself might not have been able to name.

‘He wants to be heard,’ Dutch said. ‘Not vindicated. Heard. He knows the tribunal’s finding isn’t reversible. He’s not asking for that. He wants the man whose report produced the tribunal to look at him and hear what he has to say.’

Jackson was looking at the track.

‘That’s why the exchange holds,’ Dutch said. ‘Once you’re inside he has what he built four years for. He’s not going to corrupt it by going back on the terms. Corruption of the moment would mean the four years were about cruelty. Okello doesn’t believe that about himself.’ Dutch paused. ‘He’s wrong about some things. He’s not wrong about that.’

Jackson said nothing.

‘Patrick,’ Dutch said.

Jackson looked at him.

‘He’s at a threshold. I saw it in the first two minutes and it didn’t change across an hour in that room. He’s been inside his father’s certainty for four years and the certainty has been wearing against something in him that his father’s account doesn’t fully answer.’ Dutch held Jackson’s gaze. ‘He isn’t against his father. He loves his father. But he came in on his father’s version of what happened and he’s been watching his father’s version produce this —’ Dutch gestured at the general situation — ‘and somewhere in the last year or two what his father told him stopped being sufficient to explain everything he’s seeing.’

‘He’s not going to break from his father,’ Jackson said.

‘No. Not break. But when you walk through that gate he’s going to see something he hasn’t seen in four years. A man from the other side of the account, present, in person, not fleeing and not fighting.’ Dutch paused. ‘That’s going to matter to him. I laid the ground for it to matter. You walking up that track is what activates it.’

Jackson looked at the track again. At the two kilometers of bush between them and the gate.

‘What do you need from me inside,’ he said.

‘Let Okello talk,’ Dutch said. ‘All of it. Everything he’s been holding for four years. You listen the way you actually listen when something matters, not the way you listen when you’re waiting for the other person to finish. He’ll know the difference.’

‘And then.’

‘Then whatever you have to say about Ituri, you say it honestly. Not in self-defense. Not as a counter-argument. What you actually believe about what you saw and what you reported and what you’d do the same way again.’ Dutch looked at him. ‘He needs to know you’re not ashamed of what you did. He needs to know it was a real act by a real person who made a judgment and stood behind it. That’s what he’s been denied. The acknowledgment that the person on the other side was real and accountable.’

Jackson looked at him steadily.

‘I’m not ashamed of what I reported,’ he said.

‘I know. Say it to him.’

A silence. The bush sounds. Something calling from the far treeline that Dutch couldn’t identify.

‘Gloria and the children,’ Jackson said. ‘Where are they in the compound.’

‘Western outbuilding. Single entrance facing north. No visible windows on the south or west face. I saw them brought to the doorway of a passage connecting the outbuilding to the main structure.’ Dutch looked at the treeline. ‘There’s a guard presence near that outbuilding. I heard voices in that direction during the meeting. One guard minimum, possibly two.’

Jackson nodded. Filing it.

‘The exchange mechanics,’ Dutch said. ‘Okello agreed to the terms as I described them. His people bring Gloria and the children to the gate. You come up the track. When you reach the gate they release the family. You cross the threshold.’

‘His men stay armed.’

‘Yes.’

‘And I walk in with nothing.’

‘You walk in as yourself,’ Dutch said. ‘That’s not nothing. That’s everything Okello planned for.’

Jackson looked at the track one more time. The assessment of a man who had been calculating risk for his entire professional life and was doing it now about a situation that was unlike any other situation he had been in because of who the risk was for.

‘Joseph,’ Jackson said.

Dutch raised his voice slightly. ‘Joseph.’

Joseph came out of the scrub edge and stood at the vehicle.

‘When the family comes through the gate,’ Jackson said, ‘you bring the vehicle forward. Not to the gate — fifty meters out. Lights off. Engine running. The moment they clear the gate you get out and you go to them.’

Joseph nodded.

‘Marcus,’ Jackson said. ‘Tell him —’ He stopped. Looked at Dutch.

‘I’ll tell him,’ Dutch said. ‘I’ll be at the gate. I’ll walk out with them and I’ll tell Marcus what to do and in what order and he’ll do it.’

‘You’ll walk out,’ Jackson said. ‘And then you go back in.’

‘Yes.’

Jackson looked at him.

‘That’s the plan,’ Dutch said.

‘I know it’s the plan. I’m registering that it’s the plan.’

‘Fifteen minutes,’ Dutch said. ‘After the exchange completes. That’s how long I need with Okello and Patrick before you come back in. Fifteen minutes and I’ll have done everything available to me.’

Jackson held his gaze for a moment. Then he looked at Joseph.

‘When Dutch comes back through the gate the second time, you go immediately.’

‘Immediately,’ Joseph confirmed.

Jackson looked at the track.

‘All right,’ he said. ‘Let’s go tell Okello his soldier is coming.’

* * *

Dutch went back through the gate alone.

The same gate, the same guards, the same track — all of it the same except the light, which had moved another forty minutes toward dark and had changed the compound’s atmosphere in the way that the shift from afternoon to evening changed the atmosphere of enclosed spaces. More interior. The shadows longer, the lit areas more deliberate.

The lieutenant who had managed Dutch’s first entry was at the gate and took him directly to Okello without the waiting. Okello was where Dutch had left him, at the table in the main room, Patrick at the wall. The arrangement of a man who had decided to wait in exactly the position Dutch had left him in.

Dutch sat.

‘The soldier will come,’ Dutch said. ‘Two hours. He’ll walk up the track from the road as I walked it. Alone. No weapon.’

Okello looked at him.

‘He agreed to this,’ Okello said.

‘He agreed.’

‘Without knowing what happens after he crosses the threshold.’

‘He knows what you want,’ Dutch said. ‘He’s coming to give it to you.’ He held Okello’s gaze. ‘That required something from him that you should know about before he arrives.’

Okello was still.

‘He chose to come this way,’ Dutch said. ‘He had other options. He came this way because he understood what this was actually for and he decided it was worth doing correctly.’ Dutch paused. ‘A man who comes that way deserves what he came for. The full hearing. Your full account. And then his.’

Okello looked at Patrick.

Patrick was looking at Dutch with the expression he’d had since Dutch walked through the door the first time. The searching quality that had been building all afternoon and that was now very close to the surface.

‘He has something to say,’ Okello said. Not about Patrick. About Jackson.

‘Yes,’ Dutch said.

‘What.’

‘That’s for when he’s in the room,’ Dutch said. ‘That part I won’t deliver for him.’

Okello was quiet for a moment. Then he stood.

‘Two hours,’ he said.

‘Two hours,’ Dutch said.

Okello left the room. The lieutenant appeared and indicated the covered porch where Dutch was apparently to wait.

Dutch went to the porch. Sat in a chair and looked at the compound in the failing light and thought about the fifteen minutes that were coming and what he was going to do with them.

Patrick appeared in the doorway of the main room.

He looked at Dutch. He looked at the compound. Then he came to the porch and stood at the rail with his arms folded and looked at the treeline beyond the fence.

Dutch said nothing. He let the young man have whatever he needed to have before he said what he was going to say.

A minute passed.

‘My father,’ Patrick said. He stopped. Started differently. ‘When the tribunal happened. I was in Kampala. I heard about it from my mother. Not from him.’

Dutch waited.

‘He called me four days later. He told me what had happened. What he had decided and why. What the tribunal had said.’ Patrick looked at the treeline. ‘He told me it was over. That the army was finished and his career was finished and the things he had believed about his service and his country — he said those were finished too.’

‘But you came anyway,’ Dutch said.

‘He didn’t ask me to come,’ Patrick said. ‘He told me it was over. I think he meant for me to stay in Kampala and finish my degree and make the life that he — that he had wanted me to have.’ He was quiet for a moment. ‘I couldn’t do that.’

‘Because he was your father.’

‘Because he was alone,’ Patrick said. ‘And because I believed him. About Ituri. About what the tribunal was.’ He looked at Dutch. ‘I still believe him about what happened in Ituri.’

‘I know,’ Dutch said.

‘But —’ Patrick stopped.

Dutch waited.

‘But this,’ Patrick said. The single word carrying everything he wasn’t finishing. He looked at the compound. The gate, the outbuildings, the men at the perimeter. ‘This isn’t what I came for. This is what it became.’

Dutch held very still.

‘A woman,’ Patrick said. ‘Children. A woman who did nothing except be married to the man my father holds responsible.’ He looked at his hands on the rail. ‘I was there when they brought her in. I saw her face.’ He stopped again.

‘What did you see.’

Patrick was quiet for a long moment.

‘Someone who wasn’t afraid,’ he said finally. ‘She came through that gate with her children’s hands in hers and she wasn’t afraid and she looked at everything. She counted us. She counted the buildings.’ He shook his head slightly. ‘I know what it looks like when someone is assessing a situation. I’ve been in this compound four years. She did it in thirty seconds.’

Dutch said nothing.

‘Her son,’ Patrick said. ‘He had a notebook. He never put it down. He sat in that room for five days and he wrote in it.’ Something in Patrick’s voice shifted — not breaking, adjusting. The adjustment of a young man who had been an engineering student four years ago and who recognized in a child who needed to record the world in order to be in it. ‘I used to be like that. With math. I needed to write it down.’

Dutch looked at the treeline.

‘His name is Marcus,’ Dutch said. ‘He’s eight years old. He’s been recording the patrol intervals since the first day. He has your guard schedule in that notebook.’

Patrick looked at him.

‘And the girl,’ Dutch said. ‘Amara. Six years old. She said good morning to the boy who brought food every day until he said it back.’

Patrick looked at the compound gate. Then at the main structure. Then at the door to the passage that led to the western outbuilding.

‘The exchange,’ Dutch said quietly. ‘It’s going to happen whether or not you’re ready for it. Your father agreed. The soldier is coming.’ He paused. ‘But how it happens after the family goes through the gate — that depends partly on you. On what you do when the soldier is in the room.’

‘What do you mean.’

‘Your father is going to say everything he’s been holding for four years. The soldier is going to hear it. And at some point in that room the question is going to stop being about what happened in Ituri in 2019 and start being about what happens to your father after tonight.’ Dutch looked at Patrick directly. ‘That’s the question I need you to be ready for.’

Patrick looked at him for a long moment.

‘You’re asking me to help you get my father out of this.’

‘I’m telling you that your father is going to need someone in that room who loves him and can see past tonight to what comes after it.’ Dutch held his gaze. ‘You’re the only person in that compound who qualifies.’

Patrick was quiet.

The compound had settled into its evening register. The light almost gone from the western sky. The generator somewhere on the compound’s far side running at its evening cycle. A voice calling something, a response, the ordinary sounds of a group of men ending the day.

‘After tonight,’ Patrick said. ‘What does after tonight look like. For him.’

Dutch had thought about this on the walk back through the bush. He had an answer and it was honest and it was not the answer Patrick would have wanted to hear four years ago and might be the answer he could hear now.

‘I don’t know exactly,’ Dutch said. ‘I know that a man who disbands this compound and crosses into South Sudan with enough head start has options that a man who doesn’t doesn’t have. I know that the story I publish changes the landscape — a suppressed tribunal made public is a different political problem for the Ugandan government than a fugitive in the bush. It doesn’t fix everything. It fixes some things.’ He paused. ‘And I know that your father has his son. That’s not nothing. That’s considerable.’

Patrick looked at his hands.

‘Go finish your degree,’ Dutch said. ‘After. Wherever you land. Go finish it.’

Patrick didn’t respond to that. But he looked up from his hands and looked at Dutch with the eyes of a young man who had heard something that had entered him at a level below where arguments lived.

A vehicle sound from the track.

Both of them heard it at the same moment. Dutch’s pulse did not change. A vehicle sound from the track was Joseph bringing the Land Cruiser forward to the fifty-meter position. Which meant the two hours were up. Which meant Jackson was walking.

‘He’s coming,’ Dutch said.

Patrick straightened.

‘I need to get your father,’ Dutch said. He stood. ‘And I need the family brought to the gate.’

Patrick looked at him for one more moment. Then he went inside.

Dutch stood on the porch and looked at the gate and the track beyond it and the Congo bush going dark in the last of the light.

He thought about a man walking two kilometers of red earth track in the dark carrying nothing except himself.

He thought: come on, Jackson.

* * *

The compound organized itself around the exchange with the efficiency of a military unit executing a prepared plan.

Okello gave the order and it moved. Two men to the western outbuilding. Two men to the gate. The lieutenant taking a position at the main structure’s entrance where he could see both the gate and the porch.

Dutch stood at the gate.

He heard them before he saw them — footsteps in the covered passage, more than two people, the rhythm of adults moving with children. Then the passage door opened and the light from the main structure fell into the compound and Gloria came through it.

She had Marcus’s hand. Amara’s hand. The children on either side of her, the three of them moving as the unit they had been for five days in a concrete room.

She saw Dutch at the gate and crossed the compound toward him without breaking stride.

Dutch looked at Marcus.

‘Marcus,’ he said. ‘My name is Dutch. I’m a friend of your father’s. We’re going to walk through this gate now. On the other side of the gate there’s a vehicle and a man named Joseph. You know Joseph.’

Marcus looked at him. The full direct assessment.

‘Yes,’ Marcus said.

‘When we get to the vehicle you get in the back on the left side. One thing at a time. Through the gate first.’

‘Through the gate first,’ Marcus confirmed. The rule stated back. Exactly as Jackson had described.

Dutch looked at Amara.

She was looking at him with the same expression she’d had in the doorway during his first visit. The category she’d assigned him then apparently confirmed by his presence here now.

‘Joseph has something for you,’ Dutch said. ‘In the vehicle.’

Amara looked at him for a moment. Something in her face — not surprise. The expression of a child whose expectation has been confirmed rather than exceeded.

Dutch looked at Gloria.

Her face was doing everything it had been doing since he saw her in the doorway — reading him, reading the situation, reading the compound and the men in it and the geometry of the gate and what was on the other side of it. Forty-eight hours of prison and five days of observation compressed into the two seconds they had for this.

‘Jackson,’ she said.

‘Coming up the track right now.’

Her jaw tightened once. Just that.

‘You walk out,’ Dutch said quietly. ‘You don’t look back. You get to the vehicle and you get the children in and you go.’

‘And you stay.’

‘I stay.’

Gloria looked at him for one second.

‘How long.’

‘Not long,’ Dutch said. ‘Fifteen minutes.’

She held his gaze.

‘All right,’ she said.

The gate opened.

Dutch stepped through it with Gloria and the children. He put himself between them and the compound’s interior and they walked forward through the gate and into the dark of the track beyond it.

Fifty meters out the Land Cruiser was there, engine running, lights off. Joseph was out of the vehicle before they reached it, coming toward them, and when he reached Amara he went down to one knee in the red earth and reached into his jacket and produced the elephant.

Amara looked at it.

Then she looked at Joseph.

‘You went back for it,’ she said.

‘Yes.’

‘I knew you would,’ Amara said.

She took the elephant. She held it against her chest the way she had held the giraffe in the concrete room — not replacing, returning. The original restored.

Joseph looked at her for a moment. Then he stood.

Dutch looked at Gloria one final time.

She was looking past him, past the vehicle, down the track into the dark. Looking for a shape coming up the red earth. Looking for Jackson.

‘He’s coming,’ Dutch said.

‘I know,’ she said. She didn’t look away from the track. ‘Go.’

Dutch turned and walked back toward the gate.

Behind him he heard Joseph’s voice — low, specific, one instruction at a time in the order they came. Marcus’s voice responding, a single word, compliance without question. The Land Cruiser’s door. Then another door.

He didn’t look back.

* * *

At the gate Dutch stopped.

He turned and looked down the track.

Jackson was coming.

He was two hundred meters out, walking at the pace Dutch had walked it — unhurried, no particular urgency in the movement, a man who had decided to be here and found nothing remarkable about that decision. He was carrying nothing. Both hands at his sides, visible, the deliberate visibility of a man communicating his terms as clearly as any words could.

The gate was open.

Okello’s men were at their positions on either side of it. They were watching Jackson come up the track with the attention of men who had been told what to expect and were verifying that what was expected was what was arriving.

Dutch watched Jackson walk.

He had seen Jackson operate. Had watched him in Kaduna when the situation required everything he had. Had seen him read the safe house, manage the Yusuf extraction, hold the fifteen-minute count across a warehouse by a river. He knew what Jackson looked like when he was doing the work.

This was different from the work.

This was a man who had put the work down and was walking toward something that required not the professional but the person. The man underneath the operator. The father. The husband. The person who had left a deployment in eastern Congo and crossed a border and driven north and sat across from Dutch in a Pakwach guesthouse room at midnight because his son still had a notebook and his daughter always said good morning.

Walking up a red earth track in the dark in the Congo bush toward the thing he was coming toward.

Dutch thought: I have never in thirty years of this work seen anything that cost more than what this man is doing right now.

He thought: I have never in thirty years of this work seen anything done more completely.

Jackson reached the gate.

He stopped. He looked at Dutch for one second.

Dutch looked back.

Everything that needed to be said between them was in that second and was said without words in the way that the things between people who have been through something together get said — not spoken, communicated, through the quality of attention that two people bring to each other when they have decided that the other one is worth the full truth of who they are.

Dutch stepped back from the gate.

Jackson walked through it.

The gate closed.

Dutch stood outside it in the dark and listened to the compound on the other side and thought about fifteen minutes and what they contained.

Chapter XII
The Gate

Dutch was at the gate when Jackson came into the sniper’s range.

He didn’t see the shooter. He didn’t need to. The elevated position on the main structure’s roof had been obvious from his first entry — a reinforced section at the northeast corner with sightlines covering the approach track from two hundred meters. He had noted it and said nothing because noting it was sufficient and saying it to Okello would have implied a threat he wasn’t making.

Jackson came up the track with the same unhurried pace he’d been walking since he left the bush. He could see the gate from where he was. He could see Dutch at it. He could feel, Dutch suspected, the position on the roof without being told it was there, because a man with Jackson’s training read elevated positions from approach the way other people read signage.

He kept walking.

Okello was in the main room. He had posted the lieutenant at the window with binoculars and was watching the lieutenant’s face rather than the track, which told Dutch something about how Okello managed operations — through trusted subordinates, reading their assessment rather than the raw information. The lieutenant was the instrument. Okello was reading the instrument.

Dutch stood at the gate.

He was the visible presence at the threshold. The thing Jackson could see that confirmed what he was walking toward was what he’d been told it was. Dutch standing at the gate in the dark was the signal that the situation inside was what Dutch had said it was when he came back out. If Dutch hadn’t been there Jackson would have known the situation had changed.

Dutch was there.

The lieutenant said something. Okello spoke. The man at the passage door to the western outbuilding opened it and the passage filled with movement.

Dutch watched Jackson come up the track.

One hundred and fifty meters. One hundred. The range at which the position on the roof had a shot that required no particular skill. Seventy-five meters. The range at which the shot was automatic.

Okello said something else.

The gate opened from the inside.

Gloria came through the passage with the children. Moving without being told to move, the way a woman moved who had been waiting for this moment and had prepared herself for every version of it. Marcus’s hand in hers. Amara’s hand in hers. The three of them crossing the compound in the formation they had maintained since the first day.

Dutch stepped to the side of the gate and let them through.

He found Marcus’s eyes.

‘Marcus. Walk straight to the vehicle. Joseph is there. Get in the left rear seat.’

Marcus looked at him for one second. Then: ‘Left rear seat.’

‘Go.’

Marcus went, and Amara went beside him, and Gloria’s hand released them both at the gate and she paused for a half-second that was not hesitation but registration. Looking down the track. Jackson fifty meters away and closing. His face visible now in the compound’s perimeter lighting.

Gloria looked at him.

Dutch could not see Jackson’s face from this angle. He watched Gloria’s face instead and read what she saw in Jackson’s and what she saw was sufficient because her face did the thing it did when the thing she had been carrying for five days was allowed to set itself down, just briefly, just for the duration of this moment before the next thing.

Then Jackson was at the gate and Gloria was through it and they passed each other in the threshold with less than a meter between them and neither of them stopped because stopping was not the plan and they both knew the plan and they both knew that the plan was the thing that made the other one safe.

Dutch heard Gloria’s footsteps on the track behind him going away.

Jackson came through the gate.

The gate closed.

Dutch looked at Jackson. Jackson looked at Dutch. One second, the same as before, except now it contained the additional weight of the gate being closed and what was on which side of it.

‘Fifteen minutes,’ Dutch said quietly.

‘Fifteen,’ Jackson said.

They walked toward the main room.

* * *

Okello was standing when they entered.

He had been standing since the exchange began — the posture of a man who had been waiting four years for this moment and was receiving it now and did not intend to receive it sitting down. His eyes went to Jackson with the complete focused attention of everything he had been building toward.

Jackson looked back at him.

Dutch stood at the edge of the room and let it happen.

The two men looked at each other across the table for a long moment without speaking. Dutch had been in rooms where significant things passed between people in silence and he had learned to let the silence do its work without interference. This was one of those rooms. Four years on one side and whatever Jackson was carrying on the other, meeting in the space between two men who had never been in the same room before and who had been in each other’s lives for four years regardless.

Okello spoke first.

‘Sit down,’ he said.

Jackson sat. Okello sat. Dutch remained standing at the room’s edge.

Patrick was at the wall. He was watching Jackson with the searching attention Dutch had been reading since the afternoon, except now there was something different in it — the quality had changed from searching to finding. A young man in the process of seeing the thing he had been looking for and not yet knowing what to do with having found it.

Dutch noted it and returned his attention to the table.

‘You came,’ Okello said.

‘Yes,’ Jackson said.

‘Without a weapon.’

‘Yes.’

‘Why.’

Jackson looked at him steadily. ‘Because this isn’t that kind of problem.’

Okello sat with that. The answer had entered him somewhere and he was letting it settle before he responded.

‘The journalist told me you had something to say,’ Okello said. ‘That he wouldn’t deliver it for you.’

‘No,’ Jackson said. ‘He wouldn’t.’

Okello looked at him. ‘Then say it.’

Jackson looked at the table for a moment. Then back at Okello.

‘I saw what happened in that village,’ Jackson said. ‘I saw it and I reported it because reporting what I observed was what I was there to do and because what I observed was serious enough that not reporting it wasn’t an option I could live with.’ He paused. ‘I’d make the same decision again.’

The room was very still.

‘You’re telling me you were right,’ Okello said.

‘I’m telling you I reported what I saw,’ Jackson said. ‘What the tribunal did with it — that was the tribunal’s decision. I had no part in the tribunal.’ He held Okello’s gaze. ‘But the report — yes. I’d write it the same way today.’

Okello looked at him for a long moment.

‘Then why are you here,’ he said. ‘If you believe you were right. Why sit across from me.’

Jackson was quiet for a moment. Dutch watched him find the answer — not construct it, find it, the way a man found something true in himself that he hadn’t articulated yet.

‘Because being right about the report doesn’t mean I know everything about what happened in Ituri,’ Jackson said. ‘I know what I saw. I don’t know what you saw. I don’t know what decision you made and why and what it cost you before and after.’ He paused. ‘I came because you built four years toward having me in this room and because whatever you have to say deserves to be said to the person it’s about.’

Okello looked at him.

‘You want to hear it,’ he said.

‘Yes,’ Jackson said. ‘I want to hear it.’

Okello was quiet for a moment. Then he began to talk.

* * *

Dutch listened with part of his attention and used the rest of it for Patrick.

Okello’s account was the same account he had given Dutch in the afternoon, the same facts in the same order with the same precise language of a man who had rehearsed this telling many times. But it was not the same account. The audience was different and the difference of the audience changed the account in ways that Dutch observed with the attention he had trained across thirty years.

To Dutch, Okello had been delivering a record. A document to be published, preserved, restored to the public record from which it had been erased.

To Jackson, he was delivering an indictment.

Not an accusation — an indictment in the formal sense. The presentation of evidence to the person who needed to hear it. Okello’s voice had a different quality now, less formal, more direct. He was not constructing a record. He was speaking to a man.

Jackson listened.

Dutch had told him to listen the way he actually listened when something mattered. Jackson was doing that. He was not waiting for Okello to finish. He was not preparing a response. He was in the account, inside it, receiving it with the full weight it had. Dutch could see it in the quality of his stillness — not the stillness of suppression, the stillness of absorption.

Okello noticed it too. Dutch saw him notice it — the slight adjustment in the account’s register as Okello recognized that the man across the table was not receiving him defensively. The account became, in the second half, less like an indictment and more like something else. A conversation between two men who had been in a difficult situation and had made different decisions about it and were now, for the first time, in the same room.

Dutch turned most of his attention to Patrick.

Patrick was still at the wall. He was watching his father and Jackson with the expression Dutch had been building toward since the afternoon on the porch — the expression that existed on the other side of the threshold Dutch had seen him approaching. He had crossed it. Dutch could see that he had crossed it, that whatever question he had been carrying for four years had been answered in some way by Jackson sitting across from his father and listening.

The answer wasn’t comfortable. Dutch didn’t expect it to be comfortable. The comfortable answer would have been Jackson defensive, counter-arguing, making Okello’s case harder and therefore Okello’s certainty easier to maintain. Jackson doing the opposite — sitting still and receiving it — was the answer that was hardest to hold alongside what Patrick had built four years on.

Dutch moved from the wall to the edge of the table. Not sitting. Positioning himself where Patrick could see him without looking away from his father.

He waited.

Okello finished. The account ran to its end the way accounts run to their end when the person giving them has finally given them to the person they were meant for. Not with drama. With the quality of completion.

Jackson was quiet for a moment.

‘I hear you,’ he said.

Two words. Dutch had heard three-hour debriefs produce less.

Okello looked at him.

‘I hear what it cost,’ Jackson said. ‘The tribunal, the record, the four years. I hear what you lost.’ He paused. ‘That doesn’t change my report. But I hear you.’

The room was very quiet.

Okello looked at the table. He looked at his hands. He looked at the table again.

Dutch watched him receive it. The thing he had built four years for, arriving in exactly two words. Not the reversal he might have wanted. Not the vindication. The acknowledgment. What Dutch had told Jackson the man needed and that Jackson had delivered with the precision of someone who understood the difference between what a situation called for and what he might have preferred to give.

Dutch looked at Patrick.

Patrick was looking at his father.

Okello’s face, in this moment, was not the face of the compound’s commander or the cashiered major or the man who had spent four years building toward this. It was the face of a man who had been carrying something very heavy for a very long time and has just been allowed to set it down, briefly, in front of someone who has acknowledged its weight.

Patrick saw it.

Dutch saw Patrick see it.

He moved.

She prayed for the man at the Nile Crescent gate and for the young man in the bush who had a degree to finish.

Dutch said amen.

He meant it.

After dinner he helped with the dishes and they sat on the back step for a while with the garden in the evening dark around them and the cardinal somewhere in the hedge not visible but audible and the bougainvillea on the back fence doing what it did at this hour.

Linda said nothing.

Dutch said nothing.

They sat with the garden.

After a while she stood and put her hand on his shoulder — the brief weight of it, the forty-year gesture that was entirely itself and needed nothing added to it — and went inside.

Dutch sat for a few more minutes alone.

He thought about the first line.

He had been carrying it since the trading center in northwestern Uganda in the back of Joseph’s Land Cruiser while the reunion happened on the other side of the vehicles. It had arrived without announcement, tide-like, and he had put it in the memory location where he kept the things that needed no active attention and were never fully inactive and it had been there since.

He stood.

He went to the writing room.

* * *

The lamp. The desk. The legal pad.

The pen beside it where he had left it the morning the notification arrived, the morning the 1030 came through and everything that had been going to happen happened.

The photograph on the wall that Linda had framed and hung without asking — the two of them on a beach somewhere, early years, both squinting into the same equatorial sun.

He sat down.

He looked at the legal pad.

He picked up the pen.

He thought about thirty years of rooms. The rooms you chose and the rooms you didn’t choose. The rooms in Kaduna and Wana and Amman and Manila and a doublewide in rural Mississippi in 1987 and a compound in the Congo bush last week. The rooms at the school — the main entrance in the morning light, the corridor where Theo had walked in silence through a hard Monday, the common room where a boy with a notebook had sat across from Dutch and showed him a drawing of a concrete room with the window dimensions noted in the margin.

He thought about what the rooms added up to.

Not the operations. Not the intelligence picture or the career or the institutional record of a man who had spent thirty years doing a particular kind of work in a particular way. What was underneath all of that. What had been there before the first room and was still there after the last one and would be there in the school and the writing room and the garden and the back step in the evening with Linda.

He thought about Joseph at the Nile Crescent gate. The handshake with duration. ‘It’s the same work. Different room.’

He thought: yes.

He thought: that’s what the memoir is about. Not the rooms. What’s in them. What you bring to them. What you find when you get there that you didn’t know you were looking for.

He thought: I know what the first line is because I’ve known it for forty years. I just needed the right room to understand it.

He put the pen to the page.

He wrote.

The first thing you need to know about me is that I have spent my life walking into rooms I wasn’t supposed to be in and finding, inside them, the thing I was actually looking for.

He read it back.

He read it again.

It was right. Not perfect — nothing he’d written in thirty years of operational reports and nothing he would write in this room would be perfect and he had made his peace with that. But right. Oriented correctly. Aimed at the true thing.

The second sentence was already there, forming, the way the second sentence forms when the first one has landed in the right place and opened the door for what follows.

He wrote it.

Then the third.

He wrote for two hours without stopping, the legal pad filling the way it had refused to fill for two years, the sentences coming not easily — nothing worth having came easily, he had known that since before he had a name for the knowing — but steadily, the way water moved through a channel that had been cleared at last.

Outside the writing room the house was quiet. Linda asleep. The garden dark. The Florida night doing what Florida nights did.

Dutch wrote.

He wrote about the rooms. The first ones, when he was young enough to be afraid without knowing how to name the fear and had walked in anyway because the walking was the job. The later ones, when the fear was managed and the instrument was refined and the rooms were different countries and different languages and the same essential problem in each of them: how to be in a room fully enough to find what the room contained.

He wrote about Linda. Not everything — everything would take years and he was only beginning. But the shape of it. What it had meant to have someone who held the ordinary world while he was in the rooms. What it had cost her and what she had given it and what the cost and the giving had made between them across forty years.

He wrote about the school. Cooper’s rocks. Lily’s dreams. The fifth-grade patrols who said have a blessed day to every family that came through as though the blessing were inexhaustible, which maybe it was.

He wrote about Kaduna and Jackson and a warehouse by the river and a piece of paper with two lines on it that had come back through the years as a 1030 in a donation account and a gate in the Congo bush and a mango tree still straight in its corner.

He did not write about Okello. That account belonged to someone else and he had promised to put it in the record and he would and it was separate from this.

He wrote about Joseph. About a twelve-year-old boy in northern Uganda and a carved wooden figure in his pocket and what happened to it and what he had done about an elephant under a seat forty years later. He wrote about that for a long time because it seemed to him to contain something essential about what the work was for and what the rooms were for and what it meant to be the person who went into them.

He wrote about what he had found.

Not the operations. Not the intelligence. The thing underneath both of those.

The same thing in every room, in every country, in every situation that had required him to be fully present in a place he had not planned to be: people. The irreducible weight of other people’s lives pressing against his own. The way that weight, if you let it, if you didn’t filter it through the professional distance that made the work manageable but the rooms empty — the way the weight of other people’s lives was also the weight of your own life becoming real to you.

He had walked into rooms for thirty years.

He had been walking into rooms his whole life.

The school was a room. The garden was a room. The back step in the evening with Linda was a room. The ordinary world was a room and he had been in it and out of it and the only thing that mattered, the thing that had mattered in every room he had ever been in, was whether he was fully there.

He put the pen down.

He looked at the legal pad. The pages filled. The quality of a thing that had been waiting to exist and had finally been allowed to.

He thought: this is the beginning.

He thought: tomorrow I’ll write more.

He turned off the lamp.

He went to bed.

Linda was asleep. He lay down beside her in the dark and listened to her breathe and felt the house around them and the garden beyond the house and the Florida night beyond the garden and whatever was beyond that — the whole improbable ordinary extraordinary world that kept being the world regardless of what happened in it.

He thought about Gloria’s prayer in the concrete room. Certainty that someone was listening.

He thought about Amara saying good morning to the boy who brought food. Not because it would work. Because it was morning.

He thought about the fifth-grade patrol at the school door. ‘Have a blessed day.’ Said as though the blessing were inexhaustible.

Maybe it was.

Dutch closed his eyes.

He slept.

Tomorrow the school and the perimeter walk and Cooper’s rocks and Lily’s dreams and the east gate lock and the oak branch and the ordinary faithful machinery of a life that had turned out to be what it had turned out to be.

Tomorrow the legal pad.

More to write.

Always more to write.
