Part Three · A Business of Generosity

The Proof
Is in the People

Twenty-five years. $10.5 million. Six communities on three continents. This is what Doing Good by Doing Well actually looks like when you run it all the way out.

↓   keep reading

Anyone can write a mission statement. It takes about twenty minutes and a whiteboard. What's harder — and what most companies never do — is let people look behind the curtain twenty-five years later and see if it's still true.

This is the part of the book where I could offer you more frameworks. More diagrams. More evidence from companies you've heard of. And maybe I will, later. But right now I want to show you something closer to home.

UnbridledACTS is our 501(c)3. It's funded by twenty percent of net profits from thirty-plus Unbridled companies. Not what's left over after expenses. Not the surplus from a good quarter. The first twenty percent — before anything else gets distributed. That's the discipline. That's the commitment we made when the company was small and kept when it got bigger.

In 2025 — our twenty-fifth year — UnbridledACTS invested $1,627,713 directly into human lives. Not overhead. Not administration. People.

$10.5M
Given directly to causes since founding

Not to institutions. Not to government programs. Directly to the humans in front of us — one meal, one counseling session, one housing placement, one family at a time.

I want you to sit with that number for a second. Ten and a half million dollars. Not because it's impressive — though it is — but because of what it proves. It proves the model works. Generosity compounded. Profit reinvested into purpose. And the whole thing self-funded from a company that started with nothing and decided, before it had much, to give the first slice away.

"Faithfulness: the quality of being loyal and steadfast; unwavering."

UnbridledACTS 2025 Theme — Faithful Steps

That was our theme in 2025. Not growth. Not scale. Not impact at all costs. Faithfulness. We talk a lot in business about disruption, about ambition, about exponential curves. But there's a different kind of greatness — the kind that shows up in the same places year after year, with the same people, long after the novelty has worn off. That's what UnbridledACTS is. Steadfast. Unwavering. Twenty-five years of faithful steps.

Here's where those steps have taken us.

Six Communities. One Commitment.

UnbridledACTS doesn't operate through intermediaries. We don't write a check to a large institution and call it generosity. We go. We send people. We build relationships. We come back. The work is slow, direct, and deeply personal — which is exactly what makes it work.

In 2025, our resources reached six communities across three continents. Each one different. Each one a story of what it looks like when a business decides to take its twenty percent seriously.

🏠
The Eagle's Nest
Colorado · Healing Retreat Home

Every organization needs a place where people can exhale. The Eagle's Nest is ours. A retreat and healing home near Denver, it's the physical center of gravity for everything UnbridledACTS does. Teams come here. Families come here. People who are carrying things they can't put down anywhere else come here.

In 2025, the Nest hosted over a hundred gatherings. Retreats built around Life Model Works principles. Family service trips. Team offsites where people got quiet enough to hear themselves think. Countless meals shared around a table, which turns out to be one of the most powerful things you can do for a human being.

Mizizi
Jinja & Kayunga, Uganda · Holistic Family Transformation

The word means "roots" in Swahili. That tells you everything about the approach: not intervention, not rescue — roots. Deep, slow, lasting transformation for families living in poverty in Uganda. Bev Harris founded this work out of the same conviction we built the company on: that two are better than one, and that real change happens in relationship, not in programs.

In 2025, Mizizi reached a milestone that only comes from fourteen years of faithfulness. We watched the children who entered this program as kids become adults — lawyers, IT professionals — now walking alongside the families behind them. Third-generation impact. The investment we made over a decade ago is now multiplying on its own.

This year also brought the largest U.S. volunteer group to ever go on the ground in Uganda, a new homeschool program for ten children, and a partnership with a local home supporting survivors of human trafficking.

Love Hard
San Diego, CA · Families Who Have Lost a Child

Jen and John Kamins lost their son Cash. Instead of collapsing inward, they built an organization for every family who has stood in that same impossible place. Love Hard supports grieving families with grants, counseling, and retreats — and partners with Ronald McDonald House and Project Sol Flower to go further than they could alone.

In 2025, they ran a 5K that raised $33,000 and drew 281 runners. They covered $3,600 in grief counseling. They supported seven families. Small numbers, maybe, by the metrics most people use. But there is no small number when the person being served just lost a child.

Transforma
San José, Costa Rica · Refugees & Survivors

Vanessa Pavely built Transforma to serve the people most societies have decided to forget: refugees, Nicaraguan immigrants living in ultra-poverty, survivors of the sex trade and domestic violence. We've been partners since 2019. In that time, together, we've sent twenty-one volunteers on four service trips and given $265,000 directly to families who had almost nothing.

In 2025 we sent our largest volunteer group yet. The capital campaign for land and a permanent headquarters is moving. Slowly, faithfully. The same way everything worth building moves.

Fremont County
Cañon City, CO · Housing, Recovery & Mental Health

This one is personal. When we made the decision to invest in Cañon City — the buildings, the hotel, the coffee shop, the event spaces — we weren't just betting on real estate. We were betting on a community. And a bet on a community means you have to care about all of it. Not just the beautiful parts.

Fremont County has real challenges. Homelessness. Substance abuse. Mental health needs that outpace resources. We don't pretend that buying a building fixes any of that. But we do believe that a business that's serious about Doing Good by Doing Well has to look at the place it's planted and ask: what does this community actually need?

Our Fremont County initiative is a long-term housing and recovery commitment. It's early. It's hard. And we're in it.

Identity Fund
Internal · Team Members & Extended Family

This one doesn't make the press release. It doesn't get a spotlight at the gala. But it might be the most important thing we do.

The Identity Fund covers fifty percent of the cost of professional counseling for our team members — and their extended family — and for others in the community who need it. No questions asked about why. No stigma. No performance review tied to whether you went. You go because you're human and humans need help processing the weight of being alive.

We've covered over $865,000 in counseling costs for more than two hundred people since the fund began. The theory is simple: a healthier person is a better colleague, a better partner, a better parent. We don't invest in the Identity Fund because it makes business sense — though it does. We invest in it because it's the right thing to do.

Response Fund
National · Crisis Response for Team & Community

Sometimes people don't need a program. They need someone to show up with a check before the lights go off or the car gets repossessed or the medical bill sends everything sideways. The Response Fund is our rapid-response arm — direct grants to individuals in acute crisis, no bureaucracy, no three-month waiting list.

We've processed 154 applicants and distributed over $350,000 in direct grants since the fund began. In 2025 alone, $71,000 went directly to individuals and families in the middle of their hardest moments.

This Is What the Model Produces

I want to be clear about something: UnbridledACTS is not separate from the business. It's not our PR strategy. It's not the thing we built so people would like us. It is the direct output of a business model that was designed, from the beginning, to generate this.

The twenty percent was never optional. It was always the first allocation — before bonuses, before reinvestment, before anything else got a dollar. We decided that before we knew if the company would survive. We decided it when it cost us something to decide it.

The Model in Motion

20% Philanthropy — First fruits, always. Not surplus. Not what's left. The first slice. UnbridledACTS is funded before the business takes its share.

20% Reinvestment — Stored for the lean years. Joseph's model. Hold the seed so you can scatter in famine when the soil is most fertile and everyone else has stopped planting.

60% People & Partners — The team, the partners, the ecosystem. Competitive pay. Real benefits. A business of generosity starts with how you treat the people closest to you.

The $10.5 million didn't happen because we had an unusually good year. It happened because we showed up the same way for twenty-five years in a row. Good years and bad years. COVID years. Years when we weren't sure what the next quarter looked like. The commitment didn't change based on the quarter.

That's what faithfulness looks like in a business context. Not a policy. Not a pledge. A practice. Repeated until it becomes part of the organism itself.

2025 Annual Theme · UnbridledACTS

Faithful Steps

"Steward what you have toward its highest and best use. Not every year is about growth. Some years are about depth."

That posture — depth over scale, stewardship over growth metrics — is something most businesses never figure out. Because it requires you to believe that the work matters more than the optics of the work. It requires humility about what you control and what you don't. It requires showing up in the same place, with the same people, year after year, whether or not anyone is watching.

We've had years where UnbridledACTS made the news. We've had years where almost no one outside our community knew what we were doing. The quality of the work didn't change between those years. That's the point.

"What would it look like if businesses did what governments are supposed to do — but far more efficiently and effectively?"

Stan Bullis · Founder, Unbridled

That's still the question. And UnbridledACTS is one part of the answer. Not the whole answer — the whole answer takes generations. But a living, funded, twenty-five-year-old proof of concept that it's possible for a business to carry this kind of weight and get stronger for it.

The generosity isn't a cost. It's the engine.
