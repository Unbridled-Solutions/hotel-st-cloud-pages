The Generosity
Nobody Counts

Money is the easy part. The generosity that changes a life is rarely financial. It's the person who sees through the performance — and keeps believing anyway.

↓   keep reading

We are in the middle of a loneliness epidemic. That is not a metaphor, and it is not hyperbole. In 2023, U.S. Surgeon General Vivek Murthy declared it a public health crisis — and the numbers behind that declaration are worth sitting with.

Approximately half of American adults report experiencing loneliness. Not occasionally. Chronically. Cigna's longitudinal research, tracking Americans since 2018, found that figure climbed from 46% to 58% by 2023 — a twelve-point increase in five years. That means when you look around a room of any size, more than half the people in it are carrying something invisible and heavy, something that does not show up in a performance review or a quarterly report.

Here is what that loneliness actually costs. In 2010, Dr. Julianne Holt-Lunstad published a meta-analysis in PLOS Medicine — 148 studies, over 300,000 participants — and found that people with adequate social relationships had a 50% greater likelihood of survival than those with poor ones. The equivalent mortality risk of chronic loneliness: smoking fifteen cigarettes a day. More dangerous than obesity. More dangerous than physical inactivity.

Murthy's 2023 advisory took that research and asked a harder question: what are we doing about it? The answer, largely, was: not much. We have built an entire economy around productivity and output and almost nothing around the conditions that make human beings able to sustain either.

Now add this: in 1978, psychologists Pauline Clance and Suzanne Imes identified what they called the "impostor phenomenon" — the experience of believing you are not as competent as others perceive you to be, and living in terror that someone will find out. They initially studied high-achieving women. The follow-on research found it everywhere. Current estimates suggest roughly 70% of people experience it at some meaningful point in their lives — across genders, industries, income levels, and levels of demonstrated achievement.

So we have a world where more than half of people are chronically lonely, and nearly three quarters are quietly convinced they are frauds. That is the operating condition of most of the people you work with, lead, and love. They are performing. And underneath the performance, they are exhausted.

"The greatest disease in the West today is not TB or leprosy; it is being unwanted, unloved, and uncared for."

Mother Teresa

What the Science Says About Being Believed In

In 1968, Robert Rosenthal and Lenore Jacobson published a study that changed how we think about expectation. They told elementary school teachers that certain students — chosen at random — had been identified as "intellectual bloomers" poised for significant growth. The teachers were given no other instruction. Eight months later, those randomly-selected students showed measurably greater IQ gains than their peers.

The teachers had not tutored them differently. Had not given them extra material. They had simply expected more — and that expectation, transmitted through tone, attention, warmth, and patience, had changed outcomes. This became known as the Pygmalion Effect: our belief in another person's potential becomes, in part, a self-fulfilling prophecy. Not because we are naive, but because belief changes behavior — ours and theirs.

The Research · Pygmalion Effect

When teachers expected more, students delivered more — not because of curriculum changes, but because the quality of the relationship shifted. The students received more eye contact, more warmth, more challenge, more time. They felt seen. And feeling seen is a biological signal, not just an emotional one.

Rosenthal, R. & Jacobson, L. (1968). Pygmalion in the classroom. Urban Review. Reprinted and extended in multiple meta-analyses since.

Amy Edmondson at Harvard Business School spent years studying why some teams dramatically outperform others. Her finding — now one of the most replicated in organizational psychology, and confirmed by Google's own internal research in Project Aristotle — is that the single most important factor is psychological safety: the shared belief that you will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up, taking a risk, or admitting a mistake.

Psychological safety is not softness. It is not the absence of standards. It is the presence of genuine belief — a signal from the people around you that your full self is welcome here, not just the polished, performing version. Edmondson's research found that teams with high psychological safety learned faster, innovated more, made fewer errors, and retained people longer.

The Research · Psychological Safety

Google's Project Aristotle — a multi-year internal study of 180 teams — found that psychological safety was the #1 predictor of team effectiveness, more important than individual talent, resources, or structure. Teams where people felt safe to take interpersonal risks consistently outperformed those where they didn't.

Edmondson, A. (1999). Psychological safety and learning behavior in work teams. Administrative Science Quarterly, 44(2). · Rozovsky, J. (2015). Google re:Work.

There is also the attachment research. John Bowlby's work on attachment theory — originally focused on early childhood — has been extended in recent decades to the workplace. Leaders who provide what researchers call "secure base" support — a combination of safety, encouragement, and genuine belief in the other person's capability — produce employees who take more initiative, explore more creative solutions, and perform at higher levels. They feel free to venture out because they trust there is somewhere safe to come back to.

None of this is soft science. It is neurological. When we feel genuinely seen and believed in, oxytocin is released — the same hormone that drives trust, bonding, and cooperation. The brain literally functions differently. The prefrontal cortex — the seat of complex reasoning, creativity, and long-term thinking — lights up. Fear-based threat responses quiet down. The person in front of you becomes more of who they actually are.

One person who believes in you changes your brain chemistry. That is not a metaphor either.

Run, Forrest. Run.

There is a scene in Forrest Gump I have never been able to shake. Jenny doesn't have it together. She carries her own wounds — a brutal childhood, choices she can't take back. She is not standing there as a polished example of someone who figured life out. But in that moment on the road, she sees something in Forrest that Forrest cannot see in himself. And she calls him toward it.

Run, Forrest. Run.

He ran. He broke the braces off his legs. And then, as only Forrest can tell it: I just kept running.

That is generosity of spirit. It costs nothing financially. It requires something rarer: the willingness to see the best in someone and say it out loud. To believe in a person's mission so completely that their inconsistencies become irrelevant. Not because you are blind to the gaps, but because you have decided the destination matters more than the detours.

Adam Grant's research on "givers" — people who contribute to others without expecting immediate return — found something that surprised even him: givers populate both the bottom and the top of performance rankings. The ones at the bottom give indiscriminately, burning out. The ones at the top give strategically and sustainably — investing deeply in the people most likely to use that investment well, building networks of mutual generosity that compound over time. The givers who win long-term are not naive. They are intentional. They know who they are believing in, and they stay with it.

Her Name Is Lindsay

I want to tell you about a person like that in my life.

Lindsay Wyss
Chief of Staff · General Manager, Hotel St. Cloud · Ten Years and Counting

Lindsay is one of those people who makes Cindy and me fearless. That is the only word for it. She believes so deeply in the mission — in what we are trying to do and who we are trying to become — that she is willing to overlook the things that are incongruent. The things that look like a double standard from the outside. The gaps between who we aspire to be and who we are on a hard Tuesday in March.

I cannot stress enough what it means to have someone like that. Every day — literally every day — knowing that she has your back is not just comforting. It is fuel. Neurologically, relationally, organizationally. It is what makes it possible to keep going when the profit and loss is telling a story you don't want to read.

She is a Type 1 on the Enneagram. Fast-paced. A perfectionist who is somehow also genuinely flexible — the kind of flexibility that only comes from being deeply disciplined about what matters. She ran when we asked her to run. And when we started in Cañon City, she came back.

People like that don't come back because of the compensation package.

How We Found Each Other

For nearly twenty years, Unbridled did the live events and incentive work for Red Robin. Lindsay's father, Ray, had been a VP there — his title, as I remember it, was something like Chief Purchaser of Fabulous Things. He had been gone from Red Robin for a couple of years when I walked onto a plane and ran right into him across the aisle.

We caught up. Before we landed he said: Will you do me a favor? Will you interview my daughter Lindsay?

What Ray didn't fully explain was that Lindsay had grown up going on the trips we designed. She had a childhood full of Red Robin experiences — moments, memories, environments — that our team had built. She knew the Unbridled way before she knew what to call it. We interviewed her. We hired her on the spot. From day one it was symbiotic — she was almost finishing my sentences.

The Arc

Lindsay was instrumental in building Unbridled Wealth. Instrumental in building Unbridled Solutions. And now she and her husband are instrumental in Cañon City — she is not only my Chief of Staff but the General Manager of Hotel St. Cloud.

Her husband is the assistant principal of the high school. A mother of three. A family making a daily, ground-level impact on Main Street — she running the economic engine for hope and hospitality, he raising up the next generation and keeping them in Cañon City.

That is a family living out Doing Good by Doing Well — not as a corporate framework, but as a way of being alive in the world.

The ROI Nobody Measures

If you were grading Cañon City on profit and loss, we have not made a dime in eight years. We have poured millions into the community. And by conventional business math, that looks like a bad investment.

But some investments are beyond financial. You are investing in a demographic, in a culture, in a city. You are lifting something. And that ROI — I may never see it fully in my lifetime. But I can tell you the local community feels it. And I can tell you that the person running Hotel St. Cloud, who grew up on the trips we designed, who came back when we needed her most — she feels it too.

The point is this: there are people in your path who are advocates. Who believe. Who see the best in you. Who call you higher. Who dismiss the behaviors that don't align — or call you up out of the ones that are worth keeping. They are not perfect. They do not need to be. But they show up.

And what that does — to the loneliness, to the imposter syndrome, to the fear that you are not enough — is something no financial generosity can replicate. It is an exponent applied to everything you are called to be.

"The power of one person who believes in you — unconditionally, regardless of your faults and mistakes — is an exponent to what you're called to."

Stan Bullis · Founder, Unbridled

My hope is that everyone finds a person like that. And my deeper hope is that you become one. Not as a program. Not as a management strategy. As a way of moving through the world — stopping for the one, seeing through the performance, calling people toward the best version of themselves whether or not it is convenient, whether or not there is an immediate return.

That is generosity of spirit. And the science, the theology, and twenty-five years of building something real all say the same thing: it is the most powerful investment you will ever make.

Hebrews 12:1 · NIV

"Therefore, since we are surrounded by such a great cloud of witnesses, let us throw off everything that hinders and the sin that so easily entangles. And let us run with perseverance the race marked out for us."

Lindsay is part of that cloud for me. Cheering not for the performance, but for the race. Not for the quarterly results, but for the finish line. She helps me run the race that was meant for me — and in doing so, she is running hers.

That is the generosity nobody counts. And it is the kind that lasts.
