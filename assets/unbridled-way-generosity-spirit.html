Part Three · A Business of Generosity

The Generosity
Nobody Counts

We talk about generosity in financial terms. Percentages. Dollars given. Impact measured. But there is a kind of generosity that never shows up in an annual report — and it may be the most powerful force in any organization.

↓   keep reading

Loneliness is killing people. That is not a metaphor. Dr. Julianne Holt-Lunstad's research — cited by the U.S. Surgeon General and reported in Forbes — found that chronic loneliness carries the same mortality risk as smoking fifteen cigarettes a day. It is more dangerous than obesity. More dangerous than physical inactivity.

And it is everywhere. Studies suggest the average person has somewhere between sixty and eighty thousand thoughts a day. The research is consistent on what those thoughts contain: the overwhelming majority are negative, and most of them are reruns — the same self-defeating loops, playing on repeat, that a person has already thought hundreds of times before.

We are living in an epidemic of people who do not believe in themselves. Who are performing a version of who they think they should be, terrified that the real version would not survive scrutiny. Imposter syndrome is not a niche phenomenon for first-time executives. It is the ambient condition of modern life.

Now hold those numbers in one hand. And in the other hand, hold this: what if one person — one person in your life — simply refused to believe the worst about you? What if one person saw through the performance, looked past the faults, dismissed the double standards, and kept showing up anyway?

What would that do to the loop?

"Run, Forrest. Run."

Jenny Curran · Forrest Gump

There is a scene in Forrest Gump that I keep coming back to. Jenny doesn't have it all together. She had a brutal childhood. She is not standing there as a polished example of someone who figured life out. But in that moment — a moment that cost her nothing financially, nothing materially — she saw something in Forrest that Forrest could not yet see in himself. And she called him toward it.

He ran. He broke the braces off his legs. And then, as only Forrest can put it: "I just kept running."

That is generosity of spirit. It does not require a checkbook. It requires something rarer: the willingness to see the best in someone and say it out loud. To believe in a person's mission so deeply that their inconsistencies become irrelevant. To show up — not when it's convenient, not when they're performing well — but every day, regardless.

Her Name Is Lindsay

I want to tell you about a person like that in my life.

Lindsay Wyss
Chief of Staff · General Manager, Hotel St. Cloud · Ten Years and Counting

Lindsay is one of those people who is so generous in spirit that she makes Cindy and me fearless. That is the only word for it. She believes so deeply in the mission — in what we are trying to do and who we are trying to become — that she is willing to overlook the things that are incongruent. The things that look like a double standard from the outside. The gaps between who we aspire to be and who we are on a hard Tuesday in March.

I cannot stress enough what it means to have someone like that. Every day — literally every day — knowing that she has your back is not just comforting. It is fuel. It is what makes it possible to keep going when the profit and loss statement is telling a story you don't want to read.

She is a Type 1 on the Enneagram. Fast-paced. Perfectionist. Flexible and adaptable in the way that only disciplined people can be. She ran when we asked her to run. And at one point — she had been with us for nearly a decade — she left to move to Spokane. Different company. Different chapter. And then when we started in Cañon City, she came back.

People like that don't come back because of the compensation package.

How We Found Each Other

The story of how Lindsay came into the Unbridled ecosystem is, like most things that matter, completely random on the surface and not random at all underneath.

For nearly twenty years, Unbridled had done the live events, travel, and incentive work for Red Robin. Bonus celebrations. Leadership conferences. The whole thing. Lindsay's father, Ray, had been a VP at Red Robin — his title, as I remember it, was something like Chief Purchaser of Fabulous Things. He had been gone from Red Robin for a couple of years.

One day I walked onto a plane and ran right into him. Sitting across the aisle. We caught up. And before we landed he said: "Hey — will you do me a favor? Will you interview my daughter Lindsay?"

What Ray didn't fully explain in that moment was that Lindsay had grown up going on the trips we designed. She had a year's worth of Red Robin swag — experiences, memories, moments — that our team had built. She knew the Unbridled way before she knew what to call it.

We interviewed her. We hired her on the spot. And from day one it was symbiotic — she was almost finishing my sentences.

The Arc

Lindsay was instrumental in building Unbridled Wealth. Instrumental in building Unbridled Solutions. And now she and her husband are instrumental in Cañon City — she is not only my Chief of Staff but the General Manager of Hotel St. Cloud.

Her husband is the assistant principal of the high school. A mother of three. A family making a daily, ground-level impact on Main Street. She is running the economic engine for hope and hospitality. He is raising up the next generation and keeping them in Cañon City.

That is a family living out Doing Good by Doing Well — not as a corporate framework, but as a way of being alive.

The ROI Nobody Measures

If you were grading Cañon City by profit and loss, we have not made a dime in eight years. We have poured millions into the community. And by the conventional math of business, that is a bad investment.

But some investments are beyond financial. You are investing in a demographic, in a culture, in a city. You are lifting something. And that ROI — I may never see it in my lifetime. But I can tell you the local community feels it. And I can tell you that the person running Hotel St. Cloud, who grew up on the trips we designed, who came back when we needed her — she feels it too.

The point is this: there are people in your path who are advocates. Who believe. Who see the best in you. Who call you higher. Who dismiss the behaviors that don't align — or call you up out of the ones that are worth cherishing. They are not perfect. They do not need to be. But they show up.

And what that does — to the loneliness, to the eighty thousand negative thoughts, to the imposter syndrome — is something no financial generosity can replicate. It is an exponent applied to everything you are called to be.

"The power of one person who believes in you — unconditionally, regardless of your faults and mistakes — is an exponent to what you're called to."

Stan Bullis

My hope is that everyone finds someone like that. A person who sees through the performance and cheers for the real thing underneath it. Who stops for the one — not for the program, not for the initiative, not for the ROI. For the one.

Hebrews 12:1 · NIV

"Therefore, since we are surrounded by such a great cloud of witnesses, let us throw off everything that hinders and the sin that so easily entangles. And let us run with perseverance the race marked out for us."

That is Lindsay to me. Part of the cloud. Cheering from a place of genuine belief — not in the performance, but in the race. Not in the quarterly results, but in the finish line. She keeps calling me higher. She helps me run the race that was meant for me.

That is generosity of spirit. And it is the most powerful kind there is.

Reflection · Generosity of Spirit
